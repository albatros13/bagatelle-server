Dalton diseased penis and abdoman

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This work belongs to the tradition of 19th-century medical illustration, likely produced by an anonymous hospital or anatomical illustrator working alongside surgeons and dermatologists. Such images circulated in atlases and teaching collections, including museum archives and public health repositories. The illustrator’s aim was didactic accuracy rather than portraiture: to convey lesion morphology with clinical fidelity for students and practitioners.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The visual language, materials, and cropping are characteristic of the Victorian era, when venereology, dermatology, and public health were consolidating as disciplines. In Britain and Europe, syphilis and other sexually transmitted infections were major urban health concerns, spurring the production of atlases to standardize diagnosis. Contemporaneous debates—such as the Contagious Diseases Acts and moral reform movements—embedded these images in broader anxieties about sexuality, labor, and urbanization. The period also saw improvements in microscopy and laboratory methods that gradually complemented, but did not replace, careful visual inspection.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The iconography is clinical: a depersonalized, headless torso that isolates the genitalia and a prominent abdominal lesion. The absence of background and clothing removes narrative context, focusing attention on texture, color, and boundary of ulcers. The painterly emphasis on erythema, crusting, and induration transforms physiological signs into legible symbols—diagnostic “tokens” legible to trained viewers. The anonymity also signals the erasure commonly practiced in venereal case imagery, where the patient becomes a specimen.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

The work uses restrained tonality, neutral ground, and precise modeling to prioritize form and surface. Highlights and shadows articulate edema and the rolled edge of ulceration; limited chroma intensifies areas of inflammation. Cropping produces a didactic composition, akin to a plate in an atlas, while measured line and subtle glazing create a convincing illusion of skin. The overall realism aligns with the period’s belief that careful depiction could function as clinical evidence.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Venereal disease was unevenly distributed along lines of class, gender, and empire. Sex workers and the poor were disproportionately surveilled and treated coercively, while middle- and upper-class clients often evaded scrutiny. Publications frequently anonymized patients, further disempowering them and masking structural causes. Colonial networks facilitated pathogen movement while exporting moralizing policies. Stigma limited care-seeking, and punitive laws displaced prevention with discipline.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The genital ulceration and an erythematous, ulcerated plaque on the lower abdomen suggest a venereal etiology. The most likely diagnosis is syphilis caused by Treponema pallidum. A primary chancre typically presents as a painless, indurated ulcer on the genitals with regional lymphadenopathy; secondary syphilis can produce disseminated mucocutaneous lesions, and late cutaneous disease may show gummatous ulceration or rupial plaques. Differential diagnoses include chancroid (Haemophilus ducreyi; usually painful, “soft” ulcers with undermined edges), genital herpes (clustered painful vesicles/ulcers), lymphogranuloma venereum (Chlamydia trachomatis L1–L3, often with tender nodes and proctitis), and granuloma inguinale (Klebsiella granulomatis, beefy red friable ulcers). Definitive diagnosis today relies on dark-field microscopy or NAAT (where available) of lesions, plus treponemal and non-treponemal serology.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Visible indicators include: an ulcer with erythematous margins and possible induration on the glans/shaft; satellite erosions; and an adjacent scrotal involvement. The abdominal plaque’s irregular border and surface crusting are consistent with ulcerative secondary or tertiary cutaneous syphilis. Although not pictured, inguinal lymphadenopathy would be expected in primary or secondary disease. The illustrator accentuates color contrast and edge definition to highlight diagnostic morphology.

[h] Treatment

Historically, mercury inunctions and fumigations were standard, later supplemented by potassium iodide for late lesions; in the early 20th century, arsphenamine (Salvarsan) became the first effective antimicrobial. Modern first-line therapy is intramuscular benzathine penicillin G, with regimens tailored to stage; aqueous crystalline penicillin G is used for neurosyphilis/ocular disease. Doxycycline is an alternative for nonpregnant, penicillin-allergic patients when desensitization is not feasible. Management includes HIV testing, partner notification, follow-up serology (e.g., RPR titers), and screening for co-infections. Public health measures emphasize condom access, prompt treatment, and de-stigmatized services.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Risk and outcomes are shaped by poverty, housing instability, criminalization of sex work, limited sexual health education, and inequitable access to clinics. Structural racism and migration barriers impede timely diagnosis. Stigma deters disclosure and partner tracing. Effective control couples biomedical therapy with social interventions—community-led outreach, confidential testing, harm reduction, and legal protections that enable care-seeking.
