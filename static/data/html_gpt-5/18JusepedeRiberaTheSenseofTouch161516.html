The Sense of Touch (c. 1615–16): Art-Historical and Biomedical Perspectives

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Jusepe de Ribera (1591–1652), known in Italy as Lo Spagnoletto, was a Spanish-born painter who forged his career in Naples under Spanish rule. Associated with the Caravaggesque circle, he combined radical naturalism with intellectual themes. Ribera’s interest in philosophers, beggars, and figures on society’s margins is central to his oeuvre and frames his explorations of the human senses.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Painted around 1615–1616, this work belongs to the early Baroque moment when artists across Italy and Spain engaged with empirical knowledge, the Counter-Reformation’s didactic goals, and a revived fascination with Aristotle’s taxonomy of the five senses. Naples, a vibrant port city marked by wealth and stark poverty, was a crucible for scientific curiosity and devotional imagery. Ribera’s series on the senses aligns with contemporary debates on how humans apprehend truth—through sight, touch, experiment, and testimony.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The central figure palpates a classical marble head, a potent emblem of ideal beauty and the authority of antiquity. By foregrounding touch rather than sight, the image proposes that knowledge can be verified sensuously and tactually. The raking beam of light symbolizes illumination: touch “discovers” form where vision fails. The book or print on the table nods to sight and textual scholarship, setting up a subtle hierarchy in which embodied tactility rivals or even surpasses ocular learning. The figure is often read as blind, his probing hands an icon of haptic cognition and skilled connoisseurship.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Ribera deploys tenebrism—stark contrasts of light and dark—to isolate the head, hands, and face against a deep shadow field. Warm earth tones, a tightly cropped composition, and emphatic modeling create a sculptural presence that echoes the marble bust. The diagonal light structures the narrative, guiding the eye from the tactile contact points to the figure’s introspective profile. Texture is paramount: rough cloth, flesh, and polished stone are differentiated with remarkable specificity, reinforcing the painting’s meditation on materiality and sensation.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Seventeenth-century Naples was marked by stark inequities—crowded housing, episodic famine, and limited medical provision. Ribera frequently represented aging, impoverished, or disabled bodies, not as curiosities but as bearers of knowledge and dignity. The coarse garments and humble setting here suggest economic precarity. Such imagery both recorded and aestheticized social disparity, inviting viewers to reflect on the learned capacities of those excluded from formal institutions.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The figure’s reliance on haptics implies visual impairment. In early modern Europe, principal causes of blindness included untreated cataracts, trachomatous scarring from chronic Chlamydia trachomatis infection, corneal opacities after smallpox, syphilitic uveitis, and angle-closure or open-angle glaucoma. Occupational trauma and malnutrition compounded risk. Contemporary physicians understood these conditions through Galenic and Aristotelian frameworks; contagion and hygiene were imperfectly theorized, and surgical interventions were rudimentary.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Ribera avoids overt lesions, instead signaling impairment through behavior: unfocused gaze, turned head, and the concentrically placed hands mapping the contours of the bust. These iconographic cues match clinical compensations observed in blindness—enhanced reliance on cutaneous and proprioceptive feedback and refined exploratory procedures (lateral motions, pressure modulation) mediated by mechanoreceptors such as Merkel cells, Meissner and Pacinian corpuscles, and Ruffini endings. Neurophysiologically, tactile information ascends the dorsal column–medial lemniscal pathway to primary somatosensory cortex; in early blindness, cross-modal plasticity recruits occipital areas for tactile tasks.

[h] Treatment

In Ribera’s time, cataract “couching” displaced the opaque lens with a needle—often temporarily improving light perception but risking infection and glaucoma. Herbal applications and topical salves were common, while trained oculists were scarce. Modern standards favor extracapsular extraction with intraocular lens implantation for cataracts; azithromycin-based strategies and facial cleanliness/environmental control for trachoma; penicillin for syphilis; and pressure-lowering therapy or surgery for glaucoma. For irreversible blindness, rehabilitation emphasizes tactile literacy (e.g., Braille), mobility training, and assistive technologies—echoing the painting’s elevation of touch as a cognitive instrument.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Risk and outcomes for visual disability are shaped by poverty, sanitation, crowding, occupational hazards, access to surgery, and stigma. Early modern Naples exemplified how epidemics and inadequate public health infrastructure intensified preventable blindness. The painting’s quiet dignity challenges ableist assumptions and suggests that expertise can emerge from embodied adaptation, not solely from institutional learning.
