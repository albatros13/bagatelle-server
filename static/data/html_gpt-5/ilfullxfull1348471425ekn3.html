[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This image is characteristic of a 19th‑century European dermatological teaching plate produced by anonymous workshop illustrators working alongside physicians. Such plates were often issued in atlases associated with the Viennese school of dermatology (Hebra, Kaposi) and disseminated widely in Latin and Romance‑language editions. The work belongs to the tradition of chromolithographic medical illustration, where artists translated clinical observation into standardized visual knowledge for students and clinicians.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The plate likely dates to the mid-to-late 1800s, a period marked by the consolidation of dermatology as a specialty and by the seismic shift from miasmatic theories to germ theory. Urbanization, military conscription, and expanding hospital systems created large clinical populations, while improved color printing enabled faithful reproduction of cutaneous signs. At the same time, venereal disease and tuberculosis carried heavy stigma; illustrated atlases mediated between scientific pedagogy and public health anxieties by presenting “objective” faces of disease.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The bust-length, front and three-quarter views labeled as figures signal taxonomic intent: the body becomes a site of evidence rather than portraiture. The sparse ground and neutral garments remove narrative context, concentrating attention on lesions. The inscription “Lámina 11a” and figure numbers embed the images within a didactic sequence. Even incidental details—an earring, braided hair—quietly index everyday subjects, underscoring how illness intersects with ordinary lives. The iconography is strictly medical, yet it also rehearses the 19th‑century “medical gaze,” where facial identity is subordinated to symptom.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Chromolithography supports nuanced tonal modeling and a restricted, clinically descriptive palette: waxy yellows and coppery reds of nodules contrast with cooler flesh. Stippling and hatchwork create a tactile topography of the skin. The bilateral pairing sets up comparison—normal versus altered contour, intact versus ulcerated surface—inviting diagnostic reading. Balance, clarity, and legibility prevail over expressive flourish; the style serves classification and replication.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Such atlases participated in regimes of knowledge that often pathologized marginalized populations. Women, the poor, migrants, and colonized subjects were disproportionately depicted because they were overrepresented in charity hospitals and colonial clinics. The images facilitated learning but also contributed to stigma around sexually transmitted infections and disfiguring diseases, which could lead to social exclusion, loss of work, and moral judgment.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The left figure shows clustered, crateriform papulonodules with yellow-brown crusting and early scarring across the forehead; the right presents a lesion at the glabella with soft-tissue contour changes. In 19th‑century atlases, such facial presentations were commonly labeled either tubero‑ulcerative syphilides (tertiary syphilis) or lupus vulgaris (cutaneous tuberculosis). Tertiary syphilis results from chronic infection by Treponema pallidum after years of latency, producing gummatous inflammation and scarring. Lupus vulgaris reflects a paucibacillary cutaneous infection with Mycobacterium tuberculosis, often arising by hematogenous spread from a pulmonary focus, yielding reddish-brown “apple‑jelly” nodules and tissue destruction.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Key signifiers include serpiginous arrangement of nodules, central umbilication, crusted ulcer rims, postinflammatory dyschromia, and the suggestion of early saddle deformity at the nasal bridge. The bilateral comparison teaches pattern recognition: symmetry, chronicity (scars adjacent to active lesions), and predilection for the midface. The illustrator highlights morphologic clues—color temperature shifts, bevelled ulcer edges, and cicatricial plaques—essential to late 19th‑century diagnosis when laboratory confirmation was limited.

[h] Treatment

Historically, tertiary syphilis was treated with mercury inunctions and potassium iodide; after 1910, arsphenamine (Salvarsan) became standard. Modern therapy is intramuscular benzathine penicillin G with serologic monitoring. For lupus vulgaris, pre‑antibiotic options included curettage, caustics, and phototherapy (Finsen light). Contemporary management uses multidrug therapy for tuberculosis (typically isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol) tailored to drug susceptibility, with surgical debulking or reconstruction for residual scarring when indicated. These shifts from toxic empiricism to targeted antimicrobial therapy mirror broader transformations in biomedical science.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Both diseases track social gradients: poverty, overcrowded housing, malnutrition, limited access to care, incarceration, and precarious labor elevate risk and delay treatment. Gendered vulnerability, transactional sex, and migration further complicate syphilis prevention, while homelessness, indoor air quality, and co-morbidities such as HIV shape TB transmission and reactivation. The image’s didactic neutrality masks these determinants, yet the very faces pictured point to the unequal burdens borne by ordinary people.
