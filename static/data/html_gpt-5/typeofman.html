Typeofman

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The image reflects the work of anonymous Egyptian artisans, filtered through a later antiquarian or archaeological illustration tradition. The figures—shown in profile head studies and a striding male wearing a shendyt kilt—embody the visual language of ancient Egypt rather than the hand of a single named artist. Such imagery likely derives from wall painting or relief conventions that were then recopied in nineteenth- or early twentieth‑century scholarly plates intended to catalogue “types” for comparative study.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The formal vocabulary evokes the New Kingdom (ca. 1550–1069 BCE), when canons of proportion, composite views, and elaborately braided wigs were pervasive. At the same time, the plate belongs to a modern period of Egyptology that organized visual material into typologies. This era’s classificatory impulse often merged art-historical documentation with pseudo-scientific anthropology. Thus the image sits at an intersection: ancient Egyptian conventions of representation and modern scholarly frameworks that sought to categorize bodies and hairstyles as evidence of “types.”

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The striding pose signifies vitality and social presence, while the kilt marks masculine respectability. The carefully segmented coiffures and probable wig forms signal status, cleanliness, and ritual propriety. In Egyptian visual culture, wigs functioned both as adornment and as hygienic technologies; shaved scalps covered by elaborate wigs communicated refinement, temple-readiness, and purity before the gods. The profile heads, with emphatic contour lines around nose and lips, conform to the Egyptian preference for clarity: each bodily part shown from its most legible angle.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Line dominates: crisp, unmodulated contours define profile, wig segments, and garment hem. Shape is simplified into geometric modules (rectilinear kilt, ovoid cranium) that echo the proportional grid known from workshop practice. Rhythm appears in the repeated wig plaits, creating visual cadence. Balance is achieved through bilateral symmetry in the head studies and through contrapposto-like leg placement in the striding figure, though without volumetric modeling. The style privileges legibility and taxonomy over individualized portraiture.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The very notion of “types” reflects a modern history in which images of antiquity were marshaled to support racialized hierarchies and essentialist views of human difference. Within ancient Egypt, hair practices also mapped social distinctions: fine wigs of human hair or plant fiber, perfumed cones, and imported oils were costly, while laborers and children often wore shaved heads. Access to grooming resources thus encoded economic status and temple or court affiliation.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

A salient biomedical lens for this imagery is scalp health—particularly pediculosis capitis (head lice) and, by extension, fungal scalp disease (tinea capitis). Pediculosis is caused by the ectoparasite Pediculus humanus capitis, transmitted primarily by head-to-head contact. Tinea capitis is a dermatophyte infection, most commonly due to Microsporum and Trichophyton species, spread via fomites or close contact. In hot Nile Valley climates with communal living and shared implements, these conditions could be persistent. The widespread ancient practice of shaving the scalp and donning wigs can be understood as a prophylactic and hygienic strategy against infestation.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Clinical signs of pediculosis include pruritus, excoriations on the nape and postauricular scalp, visible nits attached to hair shafts, and reactive cervical lymphadenopathy. Tinea capitis presents with scaly patches, hair breakage (“black dot” appearance), kerion in inflammatory cases, and sometimes alopecia. While the artwork does not depict lesions, the iconography of shaved heads under elaborate wigs functions as a cultural signifier of disease management: minimizing hair length reduces louse habitat and facilitates nit removal, while removable wigs restore the social and aesthetic functions of hair without sustaining parasites.

[h] Treatment

Archaeology has recovered fine-toothed nit combs from Egyptian contexts, attesting to mechanical removal. Textual sources indicate the use of oils and unguents—such as castor and cedar derivatives—for grooming and possibly for pest control. Shaving, washing with natron, and maintaining separate personal items would have reduced transmission. Contemporary evidence-based treatment for pediculosis includes topical pediculicides (e.g., permethrin, pyrethrins), meticulous wet combing, and environmental decontamination of fomites. For tinea capitis, systemic antifungals (griseofulvin or terbinafine, depending on organism) with adjunctive antifungal shampoos are standard. The historical-artistic solution—shaven scalp plus wig—parallels modern integrated approaches: habitat reduction, mechanical removal, and topical agents.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Crowding, shared bedding, and limited access to individualized grooming tools facilitate ectoparasite spread; conversely, elites with private quarters, barbers, and costly oils could better prevent reinfestation. Occupational environments (work gangs, temple households) and age (children’s close play) raise risk. Climate and seasonal labor cycles shaped bathing frequency and water access along the Nile. These determinants are legible in the contrast between the meticulously maintained wigs of higher status and the pragmatic shaved heads of workers and youth.
