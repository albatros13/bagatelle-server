Dalton

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
This plate belongs to the European tradition of 19th‑century medical illustration, when hospital artists collaborated with surgeons and dermatologists to catalogue disease. While the precise draughtsperson is uncertain, the rendering—graphite underdrawing, subtle watercolor tinting, and a lithographic feel—aligns with the practices of hospital schools in Paris and London. The image’s didactic clarity and restrained palette signal the work of professional medical illustrators who prioritized legibility over aesthetic flourish, yet still operated within painterly conventions of the period.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
Dermatology and venereology emerged as distinct disciplines between 1800 and 1875, driven by urban hospitals, new taxonomies (Willan, Alibert), and the demand for visual atlases. In this era, sexually transmitted infections—especially syphilis—were framed both as medical and moral questions. Laws like Britain’s Contagious Diseases Acts (1860s) policed sexuality and targeted marginalized groups. Printing advances (hand-colored lithography) enabled wide circulation of plates like this for teaching, helping standardize diagnoses across Europe’s clinics and medical schools.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
The body is cropped to the lower torso and genitals, centering the dermatologic field while anonymizing identity; the inset head offers physiognomy without full portraiture, a common iconographic device to record constitutional habitus. The coppery-red papules, some pustular and crusted, are depicted with taxonomic precision, echoing the period’s desire to classify “syphilides” by morphology. The blank ground isolates pathology as object of study, a visual rhetoric of scientific objectivity that also strips social context, turning the sufferer into a clinical specimen.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
The plate uses measured contour lines and thin washes to build form, with localized highlights that dramatize relief of papules and vesicles. The near-monochrome ground and restricted palette heighten figure–ground contrast, guiding the eye toward lesion density and distribution. Scale is carefully managed: lesions are slightly enlarged, a pedagogical exaggeration to ensure recognizability. The compositional pairing of full-field anatomy and a cameo head balances specificity with generality, fulfilling the atlas function of reproducible, standardized knowledge.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
Such images historically circulated within systems that stigmatized sexually transmitted infections, often blaming women, sex workers, soldiers, migrants, and the poor. Clinical subjects were frequently recorded with surnames or numbers, raising questions about consent, privacy, and agency. Access to treatment was uneven; charity hospitals provided care, but moralizing policies and punitive surveillance discouraged early presentation. The visual archive, while medically invaluable, also reflects power asymmetries—whose bodies were pictured, how they were labeled, and how images underwrote social control as much as therapy.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
The distribution and morphology—generalized papulopustular eruptions including the genital region—are most consistent with secondary syphilis, caused by the spirochete Treponema pallidum. Following an initial chancre at the inoculation site, hematogenous spread produces systemic symptoms (fever, malaise) and a polymorphic eruption weeks later. Lesions may be macular, papular, or pustular; a classic “coppery” hue and, at times, Biett’s collarette (a fine peripheral scale) are noted in historical atlases. Untreated infection proceeds through latent phases and can culminate in tertiary complications.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
Visual cues include symmetrical, discrete papules with some pustulation and crusting, clustering on the lower trunk and genitalia. The density, color, and varying maturation suggest disseminated lesions over time. An observable genital chancre is not definitively rendered but could be implied by focal ulceration. The facial inset implies constitutional assessment—alopecia, mucous patches, and malaise were commonly recorded in secondary disease. The artist’s attention to lesion edge, umbilication, and crust aligns with 19th‑century efforts to differentiate syphilides from variola or scabies.

[h] Treatment
Historically, mercury (inunctions, fumigation) and later potassium iodide were mainstays, with significant toxicity. In 1909–1910, Ehrlich’s arsphenamine (Salvarsan) transformed care, and from the 1940s penicillin became curative. Current first-line therapy is intramuscular benzathine penicillin G; desensitization is recommended for true penicillin allergy in pregnancy or neurosyphilis. Clinicians monitor for the Jarisch–Herxheimer reaction after initial dosing. Public health measures include partner notification, serologic follow-up (nontreponemal titers), and screening in pregnancy to prevent congenital infection.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Risk and outcomes are shaped by access to sexual health services, insurance, stigma, criminalization of sex work, unstable housing, and syndemic factors such as substance use. Structural racism and gender inequities influence testing, timely treatment, and exposure to coercive policies. Effective control rests on comprehensive education, confidential testing, culturally competent care, and harm-reduction approaches. The image’s historical context underscores how biomedical interventions must be paired with policies that address social vulnerability to interrupt transmission and reduce disparities.
