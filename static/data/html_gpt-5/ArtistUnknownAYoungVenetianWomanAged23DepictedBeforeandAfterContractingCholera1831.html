Artist Unknown: A Young Venetian Woman Aged 23, Depicted Before and After Contracting Cholera (1831)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This hand-colored print is attributed to an anonymous medical illustrator working in early nineteenth-century Italy, likely associated with a physician’s practice or a public health authority. The artist operates within the tradition of clinical portraiture, where visual accuracy was prioritized over individual authorship. Such images circulated in atlases and broadsides to teach lay and professional audiences to recognize disease.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Created in 1831, the work belongs to the Second Cholera Pandemic (c. 1826–1837), when cholera moved from South Asia through trade routes into Europe. Venice—an entrepôt bound to maritime exchange—experienced waves of infection and instituted quarantine on its lazzaretti islands. The period predates germ theory; debates raged between miasmatists, who blamed noxious airs, and contagionists, who stressed person-to-person transmission. Visual pedagogy like this plate functioned as an epidemiological tool amid uncertainty.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The diptych format juxtaposes health and disease, a moralizing and diagnostic device. The left portrait idealizes youthful normalcy; the right shows the corporeal devastations of cholera. Cool, desaturated hues on the sick figure connote hypoxia and collapse, while the loosened garment and slack posture signify loss of bodily control. The pairing invites the viewer to read the body for signs, transforming a private illness into a public message about vigilance and prevention.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Line and contour are crisp, consistent with neoclassical draftsmanship and with lithographic transfer methods common after 1820. The bilateral composition establishes balance yet dramatizes contrast through color temperature, value, and facial expression. Modeling is restrained; shadows serve didactic clarity rather than painterly effect. The piece exemplifies the medical print’s mandate: legible anatomy, frontal illumination, and minimal background to foreground clinical evidence.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Cholera’s European incursions disproportionately harmed urban poor living in crowded quarters with contaminated water. In Venice and elsewhere, authorities sometimes blamed migrants and the lower classes, justifying coercive quarantines and burial policies. Such measures could neglect structural causes—unsafe water infrastructure and inadequate sanitation. Gender and class also mediated care access; young working women like the sitter were vulnerable to income loss, stigmatization, and delayed treatment.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Cholera is an acute diarrheal illness caused by toxigenic Vibrio cholerae, primarily serogroups O1 and O139. After ingestion via contaminated water or food, bacteria colonize the small intestine and secrete cholera toxin. The toxin’s A subunit activates adenylate cyclase, increasing intracellular cAMP and driving CFTR-mediated chloride efflux; water follows osmotically, producing profuse “rice-water” stools. Rapid fluid and electrolyte loss leads to hypovolemia, metabolic acidosis, and shock.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The right-hand portrait condenses key clinical signs: sunken eyes and hollowed cheeks suggest severe dehydration; cyanotic, ashen skin tones indicate peripheral vasoconstriction and hypoxia; the drawn lips and perioral duskiness evoke acrocyanosis. The lax neck and slumped shoulders point to muscle cramps and exhaustion. Cool skin and reduced turgor, hoarse or “aphonic” voice, oliguria, and rapid thread pulse are consistent with fulminant cholera, in which mortality can occur within hours without rehydration.

[h] Treatment

Modern care centers on rapid volume and electrolyte replacement. Oral rehydration solution (ORS)—a precise mixture of glucose and salts exploiting sodium–glucose cotransport—reverses dehydration in most cases; severe cases require intravenous Ringer’s lactate. Adjunctive antibiotics (e.g., doxycycline, azithromycin) shorten illness and decrease stool volume. Zinc is recommended for children. Prevention relies on safe water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH), timely case finding, and oral cholera vaccines (killed whole-cell formulations such as Shanchol/Euvichol-Plus or Dukoral) for outbreak control.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Risk is shaped by water access, urban density, poverty, and governance. Port cities like Venice illustrate how trade networks amplify exposure. Overburdened cisterns, contaminated canals, and inadequate waste removal convert environmental vulnerability into disease. Today, conflict, displacement, climate-driven flooding, and fragile health systems sustain transmission. Education, trust in public institutions, and equitable infrastructure investment remain decisive determinants of outcomes.
