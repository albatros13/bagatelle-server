La Migraine, or “Vapors” (1823)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The print is after a design by Alexandre Marie Colin, a French Romantic-era painter and illustrator, realized in lithography by the printer Langlumé and issued by the publisher Ambroise Tardieu. The trio participated in the early nineteenth‑century Parisian market for satirical and medical caricature, where artists, printers, and publishers collaborated to meet demand for affordable, quickly produced images. Colin belonged to a cohort that bridged high art and popular press, paralleling the rise of lithography as a democratic medium.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (time‑frame)

Dated 1823, the work emerges under the Bourbon Restoration in France, a period negotiating post-Revolutionary social orders and a flourishing print culture. Lithography, introduced to France around 1816, enabled rapid, nuanced tonal reproduction, fueling albums that satirized professions, urban types, and medical fads. The “Album comique de pathologie pittoresque” translated the authority of clinical medicine into domestic comedy, reflecting a culture in which health, sensibility, and gendered decorum were public conversation pieces.

[c] Symbolism and/ or Iconography

A recumbent woman occupies a canopied bed, flanked by attendants and a practitioner preparing remedies. Iconic props—salts or a cordial on the table, a basin for emesis at the bedside, an open window for “airing,” and a folding screen for modesty—signal the diagnosis of “vapors” or migraine. The swooning gesture, hand to head and half‑closed eyes, visually encodes photophobia and throbbing pain; the drawn curtains imply a quest for darkness. The juxtaposition of domestic elegance with bodily distress ironizes fashionable fragility and the medicalized household.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

The lithographic crayon yields soft, granular lines, allowing Colin’s subtle modeling of fabrics and faces. Hand-applied tints animate select passages—the blue dress and pink upholstery—creating focal accents against a restrained palette. Compositionally, diagonals from the canopy and the patient’s arm converge on the head, emphasizing the pain locus. Repetition of curved forms (chair back, bed scrolls, basin) counterbalances verticals (screen, window frame), stabilizing a scene of agitation. The style mediates between caricature and genre painting: exaggerated expressions remain within naturalistic space and perspective.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The print satirizes a diagnosis historically gendered as feminine. “Vapors,” a catch‑all for migraine, syncope, and anxiety, pathologized women’s bodies and emotions, often discounting their pain. The domestic setting suggests class privilege: privacy, attendants, and physician visits. Yet even privileged sufferers faced dismissal as fashionable invalids. The image thus exposes asymmetries of credibility in clinical encounters and the moralizing of women’s health, patterns whose legacies persist in contemporary pain management disparities.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Migraine is a neurobiological disorder characterized by recurrent attacks of moderate to severe head pain, often unilateral, with nausea, photophobia, and phonophobia; about a third experience aura. Current evidence centers on trigeminovascular activation, release of calcitonin gene‑related peptide (CGRP), brainstem dysmodulation, and cortical spreading depolarization in aura. Genetic susceptibility interacts with triggers such as hormonal fluctuation, stress, sleep disruption, and sensory stimuli. Prevalence is higher in women, likely due to estrogen modulation of nociceptive pathways.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Colin visualizes clinical features recognizable today: the patient averts light beneath canopy drapery (photophobia) and reclines with a hand to the temple (pulsating hemicrania). The basin hints at nausea/vomiting; the open window reflects prescriptions for “fresh air” and ventilation rooted in miasmatic thinking. Smelling salts suggest syncope or orthostatic intolerance co‑interpreted as vapors. The anxious attendants and measuring practitioner allegorize the negotiation between subjective pain and observable signs, a core tension in headache medicine.

[h] Treatment

In 1823, recommended therapies included bloodletting, purgatives, opiates, blisters, cold compresses, and aromatic stimulants; regimen emphasized air, rest, and dietary temperance. Contemporary evidence‑based care differentiates acute and preventive strategies. Acute: NSAIDs, triptans, gepants (ubrogepant, rimegepant), and ditans (lasmiditan) with antiemetics as needed. Preventive: beta‑blockers, topiramate, tricyclics, onabotulinumtoxinA for chronic migraine, and CGRP‑pathway monoclonal antibodies (erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab, eptinezumab). Nonpharmacologic measures include regular sleep, hydration, trigger management, aerobic exercise, cognitive‑behavioral therapy, and neuromodulation devices. The historical accoutrements in the scene underscore how therapeutics shifted from humoral evacuation to targeted neuropeptide modulation.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Migraine burden is shaped by gender norms, occupational demands, and access to specialist care. The scene’s bourgeois interior indicates resources for respite, yet stigma frames the sufferer as delicate or performative. In contemporary settings, lower income and marginalized racial/ethnic groups face underdiagnosis, limited preventive therapy, and workplace penalties for presenteeism or absenteeism. Environmental exposures (noise, shift work, indoor air quality) and reproductive health access influence frequency and severity. Images like this one reveal that beyond neurobiology, power and place contour who receives belief, treatment, and relief.
