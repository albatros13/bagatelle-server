A Rake’s Progress: Tom Rakewell in Bethlem Hospital (Madhouse)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
William Hogarth (1697–1764) was a London-based painter, printmaker, and polemicist whose serial “modern moral subjects” fused narrative art with social critique. A Rake’s Progress, painted 1733–34 and engraved in 1735, follows Tom Rakewell from profligate inheritance to ruin; this final scene, set in Bethlem Hospital (“Bedlam”), concludes the cautionary arc. Hogarth was also an entrepreneur who circulated his images through prints and promoted the 1735 Engravers’ Copyright Act, shaping the economics and audiences of British art.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
Set in Georgian London’s 1730s, the work crystallizes tensions of a fast-commercializing city: credit-fueled consumption, the “gin craze,” and widening gaps between polite society and the urban poor. Bethlem, Europe’s oldest psychiatric hospital, had by this time become a quasi-public spectacle, where paying visitors could gawk at inmates. The scene also intersects Enlightenment rhetoric about reason and unreason, and the growth of philanthropic hospitals tempered by coercive poor-law regimes. Hogarth’s satirical cycles were consumed by a thriving print culture, allowing moral and medical debates to circulate beyond elite galleries.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
Iconographically, the madhouse functions as moral terminus: the rake’s private vices made publicly legible as insanity. Hogarth arrays archetypal “mad” figures—delusions of grandeur (a self-styled monarch), pseudo-scientific obsession (the astronomer sketching cosmological diagrams), religious enthusiasm, and melancholic withdrawal—thus cataloguing contemporary taxonomies of disorder. Paper scraps, chains, barred windows, and the indifferent attendants stage the carceral environment. The loyal Sarah Young appears as a counter-image of compassion, her presence underscoring society’s failure of care. The raised hands of visitors register both curiosity and recoil, indicting spectatorship itself.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
Hogarth’s painterly naturalism is charged with theatrical staging. A lattice of diagonals—beams, limbs, papers—drives the eye toward Tom’s slumped body, while warm browns and ashen grays produce a damp, airless tonality. Chiaroscuro carves psychological space, isolating faces in pools of light against institutional gloom. The crowded, “readable” detail anticipates the sequential logic of the engraved plates, aligning viewing with moral inference. Brushy passages alternate with crisp contours, a hybrid idiom suited to translation into print.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
The picture exposes inequities built into eighteenth‑century systems of care. Overcrowding and fee-based visiting turned illness into entertainment. Class dynamics appear in the stylish tourists contrasting with poorly clothed inmates, while gendered labor is embodied by attendants who restrain, not treat. Charity subscriptions and parish poor-laws produced patchwork access to aid, often conditioned on moral judgments. Hogarth condemns a society that commodifies suffering and externalizes responsibility, foreshadowing modern critiques of carceral medicine and the medicalization of poverty.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
Rakewell’s decline invites medical readings alongside moral ones. Contemporary viewers associated libertinism with venereal disease; several scholars propose that Tom exhibits late neurosyphilis—general paralysis of the insane—characterized by executive dysfunction, mania, tremor, and progressive dementia after Treponema pallidum infection. Alcohol misuse and malnutrition, ubiquitous in Hogarth’s London, also precipitate psychosis and cognitive failure. The surrounding inmates map nosological types recognizable to eighteenth‑century medicine: melancholia, religious mania, and monomania. Hogarth compresses heterogeneous etiologies—infectious, toxic, and psychosocial—into one ward, reflecting pre-psychiatric medicine’s porous boundaries.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
Visual signifiers aid diagnosis: Tom’s vacant gaze, slack posture, and partial undress mark loss of self-governance; a keeper clasps his wrist, implying risk of agitation or self-harm. Nearby figures enact symptom clusters—grandiose delusion (the “king”), compulsive calculation or astrological fixation, and ecstatic religiosity—each a vignette of altered reality testing. Scattered papers and broken objects imply cognitive fragmentation and impaired executive control. Hogarth privileges observable behavior over hidden lesions, mirroring a period when diagnosis was grounded in public conduct.

[h] Treatment
Treatment depicted is custodial: confinement, restraint, and surveillance. Standard eighteenth‑century interventions included bleeding, purgatives, blistering, emetics, cold immersion, and occasional opiates—meant to “cool” or evacuate presumed humoral excess. None addressed the underlying neuropsychiatric disease; for neurosyphilis, effective therapy arrived only with malariotherapy and later penicillin. Reformist “moral treatment” associated with Pinel and Tuke later prioritized routine and minimal restraint, anticipating modern psychosocial care. Hogarth’s ward prefigures the critique that asylums managed bodies more than they healed minds.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Root causes in the narrative are social: precarious credit, debt laws, alcohol availability, sexually transmitted infections linked to transactional sex, and minimal primary care for the poor. Stigma attached to “madness” reduced help-seeking and legitimated coercion. Housing insecurity and unstable labor worsened outcomes. Hogarth links moral failure to structural failure, implying that prevention lies as much in regulating urban vices and expanding humane care as in treating individual pathology.
