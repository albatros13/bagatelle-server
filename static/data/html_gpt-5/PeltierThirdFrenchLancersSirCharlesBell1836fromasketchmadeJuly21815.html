Peltier Third French Lancers Sir Charles Bell July (1836 21815)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Sir Charles Bell (1774–1842) was a Scottish surgeon, anatomist, and accomplished draughtsman whose clinical eye shaped a distinct visual language for recording trauma. Trained in Edinburgh and later active in London, Bell combined surgical practice with meticulous illustration, bringing together observation from the dissecting room, battlefield, and studio. The work shown here is a later watercolour developed from an on-the-spot sketch made shortly after the Battle of Waterloo, attesting to Bell’s dual role as physician and eyewitness recorder.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The image derives from field encounters in July 1815, immediately after Waterloo (18 June 1815), and was completed in the 1830s when Bell reworked his notebooks into finished sheets. Warfare medicine at this moment stood on the cusp of modernity yet lacked anesthesia (ether was introduced in 1846) and antisepsis (Lister’s methods spread in the late 1860s). French, British, and allied medical services operated large, improvised hospitals in barns and houses. Dominique-Jean Larrey’s “flying ambulances” had improved evacuation for the French, and British regimental surgeons managed triage under extreme scarcity. Bell’s images circulated in medical and artistic circles, shaping how European publics visualized the human cost of Napoleonic warfare.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The reclining cavalryman, his torso bared, presents the viewer with an opened abdomen and a lobulated, yellow-pink protrusion—almost certainly omentum—spilling from the wound. The gesture of lifting the cloth functions like a pietà of self-revelation: a stark invitation to witness injury rather than heroism. The moustache and shorn scalp allude to lancer iconography without relying on a full uniform, focusing attention on flesh. Bell’s medical naturalism displaces battlefield spectacle with the sacramental intimacy of the sickbed, transforming military identity into corporeal vulnerability.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Bell employs a restrained palette of sanguine reds, ochres, and ashen greys, emphasizing value contrasts over chromatic complexity. Broad washes establish ground and body planes, while tighter graphite and bodycolour articulate the wound’s edges and fatty lobules with anatomical precision. A diagonal composition drives the eye from the subject’s gaze to the exposed viscera, using foreshortening and chiaroscuro to model volume. The result merges documentary clarity with expressive economy, anticipating later medical illustration standards that privilege accuracy, legibility, and didactic focus.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Rank-and-file soldiers experienced profound inequities in survival: limited analgesia, contaminated environments, and delayed evacuation disproportionately harmed those without status or resources. Enemy combatants relied on the mercy of captors and the capacity of overstretched hospitals. Linguistic barriers, stigma toward prisoners, and class-based prioritization shaped access to care. Bell’s humanizing portrayal of an opposing lancer implicitly critiques nationalist dehumanization, insisting on the universality of bodily suffering across lines of allegiance.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The pathology depicted is consistent with a penetrating abdominal injury with evisceration, most plausibly from a lance or sabre thrust, though ballistic fragments are possible. The protruding tissue appears to be omentum—fatty, lobulated, yellow tissue—extruded through a fascial defect. Such trauma risks peritoneal contamination, hemorrhage, and shock. In 1815, the absence of sterile technique, antibiotics, and reliable hemorrhage control made peritonitis and sepsis common, with high mortality.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Visual signs include: a gaping abdominal wound with everted, edematous margins; herniated omentum with inflammatory erythema; possible contusions around the defect; and the subject’s strained facial expression, suggestive of pain and catecholamine surge. Physiologically, such a patient would face hypovolemia, risk of bowel injury, and impending peritoneal inflammation. The artistic emphasis on the moist sheen and warm coloration of the omentum conveys both the vitality and precarious exposure of visceral tissue.

[h] Treatment

Contemporary (1815) management would aim to return viable omentum to the peritoneal cavity, ligate bleeding vessels, excise devitalized tissue, and close the fascia and skin with linen or silk sutures, often under alcohol or opiate sedation and without asepsis. Dressings and poultices followed, with outcomes limited by infection. Modern care follows ATLS principles: rapid airway and hemodynamic stabilization; tetanus prophylaxis; broad-spectrum antibiotics; FAST ultrasound and CT if stable; and exploratory laparotomy for peritonitis, hemodynamic instability, or evisceration. Current practice may include damage-control surgery, omental or bowel resection if devitalized, layered closure, and negative-pressure dressings, drastically improving survival.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Determinants shaping outcome then and now include proximity to evacuation routes, the organization of military medical services, hygiene of encampments, nutritional status, and the patient’s rank and nationality. Post-injury rehabilitation and pensions affected long-term disability, while psychological trauma—today recognized as PTSD—went largely unaddressed. The image documents how structural conditions of war, not just the weapon, inscribe themselves on the body.
