The Hand of Sarah Nelmes Infected with the Cowpox (1798)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Attributed to the draughtsman William Cuff and the engraver William Skelton, this plate belongs to the visual program surrounding Edward Jenner’s work on vaccination. Cuff’s role likely involved clinical sketching from observation, while Skelton translated the drawing into a stipple and line engraving suitable for letterpress publication. Their collaboration represents the specialized ecosystem of late eighteenth-century scientific illustration, in which accuracy, legibility, and reproducibility were paramount for medical didactic purposes.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Produced in 1798 at the height of Enlightenment empiricism, the plate circulated alongside arguments for vaccination as a safer alternative to variolation. Britain faced recurrent smallpox epidemics; dairy work and rural husbandry shaped popular observations that exposure to cowpox conferred protection. The image functioned within print culture as evidence for a new biomedical paradigm, speaking to physicians, apothecaries, and lay readers amid debates over public health, risk, and moral economy.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The isolated, outstretched hand serves as an icon of empiricism—an indexical record of lesions rather than an allegorical scene. The pustules are presented as “tokens” of immunity: visible marks translating folk knowledge of dairy workers into clinical proof. The empty background suppresses narrative, elevating the lesion as the principal sign. The careful emphasis on the vesicle’s central umbilication and surrounding erythema turns the body into a demonstrative surface where immunity is both seen and argued.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Skelton’s stipple engraving modulates tone with dense dotting, producing soft gradients across the knuckles and wrist. Limited color—typically a reddish-brown ink—mimics inflammation while maintaining the economy of print. Contour and cross-hatching establish anatomical volume without distracting from the lesions, whose crisp edges and highlights anchor the composition. The serial display of multiple pustules suggests stages of development, using repetition and rhythm as pedagogical design principles.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The image emerges from unequal social relations. Dairy workers’ experiences informed the discovery, yet their knowledge was filtered through elite medical publishing. Access to early vaccination varied by class, parish resources, and geography; resistance among the poor intersected with coercive public-health measures. Global dissemination later took place within colonial networks, where vaccination campaigns could save lives yet also operate through uneven power structures and credit-claiming narratives that minimized non-elite and non-European contributions.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Cowpox is a zoonotic orthopoxvirus infection acquired through contact with infected animals, historically cattle but more often rodents as the reservoir. Human infection typically follows inoculation through skin breaks on the hands and forearms. The virus shares antigenic epitopes with Variola virus, enabling cross-protective immunity. Clinically, disease is usually localized and self-limited in immunocompetent hosts, with a single or few lesions at the point of contact and mild systemic symptoms.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The engraving emphasizes hallmark orthopox morphology: a progression from macule to papule to tense vesicle and umbilicated pustule, with a surrounding erythematous halo and eventual crusting. The depicted lesions vary in stage—one with a central depression and thicker rim, another smaller and pearly—articulating the temporal evolution of pox lesions. The distribution on the dorsal hand is consistent with occupational exposure. The artist’s precision in surface texture, swelling, and shading functions as a proto-clinical atlas.

[h] Treatment

In the eighteenth century, management was supportive: cleanliness, protection of the lesion, and avoidance of secondary bacterial infection. The biomedical significance lay not in treatment but in intentional transfer of lesion material to produce protective immunity against smallpox. Today, cowpox in humans remains self-limiting in most cases; modern antivirals such as tecovirimat, active against orthopoxviruses, may be considered for severe disease or immunocompromised patients. Vaccine strategies have shifted to safer, standardized products (e.g., MVA-BN/JYNNEOS) rather than live cowpox lymph.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Occupational exposure (milking, animal husbandry), gendered labor in dairying, and rural living conditions shaped who encountered cowpox. Literacy and trust in medical authorities affected uptake of vaccination; parish funding, travel distance, and cost influenced access. Print images like this one sought to bridge these divides by translating clinical observation into persuasive evidence, yet disparities in distribution and public messaging often determined who benefited from early preventive measures.
