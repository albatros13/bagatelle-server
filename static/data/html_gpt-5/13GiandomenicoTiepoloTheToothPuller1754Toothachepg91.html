Giandomenico Tiepolo, The Tooth Puller (1754)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Giandomenico Tiepolo (1727–1804) was a Venetian painter and printmaker, trained in the workshop of his father, Giovanni Battista Tiepolo. While the elder Tiepolo specialized in grand, luminous fresco cycles, Giandomenico cultivated keen observation of urban life, theatricality, and irony. He developed a distinctive interest in popular spectacle—especially commedia dell’arte—and frequently depicted itinerant healers, charlatans, and carnival crowds, situating refined Venetian painting in the realm of everyday social drama.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (time-frame)

The mid-eighteenth century in Venice was a period of brilliant cultural production amid political stagnation. Carnival season expanded into a near year-round theater of masks, temporary stages, and market performance. Medical practice before the widespread acceptance of germ theory and standardized licensing was pluralistic: university-trained physicians, barber-surgeons, apothecaries, midwives, bonesetters, and tooth-drawers competed in the piazza. Public dentistry—often performed by traveling practitioners—was both cure and spectacle. Tiepolo’s painting crystallizes this environment around 1754, just decades before modern anesthesia and bacteriology transformed oral care.

[c] Symbolism and/ or Iconography

The central figure, elevated on a makeshift platform, holds aloft an extracted tooth as a talismanic proof of efficacy. Such display symbolized mastery over pain and persuaded onlookers to purchase panaceas or submit to extraction. The banner and props evoke a pseudo-religious theater of healing, while masks and commedia costumes underscore deception and role-play. Gestures—hands to cheeks, grimaces, and craned necks—index unseen pain and curiosity. Dogs and street sellers animate a marketplace of bodies and commodities, suggesting porous boundaries between entertainment, commerce, and care.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Giandomenico marshals a broad, clear light and a calibrated chromatic range—ochres, reds, and lagoon blues—producing readable groups in a complex crowd. Diagonal vectors lead from the platform to clusters of masked figures, ensuring compositional balance while sustaining narrative movement. The paint handling is brisk yet descriptive, with faces and hands rendered in quick, telling touches. Such clarity of gesture makes the clinical operation legible at a distance, a painterly analogue to the charlatan’s own rhetoric of visibility.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The scene registers class and gendered inequities in access to care. Urban poor relied on public tooth-drawers because licensed surgeons charged more and were less accessible. Women and laborers appear prominently as patients and spectators, reflecting differential burdens of oral disease and limited autonomy in medical decision-making. The performative context also hints at literacy gaps: persuasion, not written credentialing, secures trust.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The implied conditions are dental caries and its sequelae—pulpitis, periapical abscess, and periodontitis. In eighteenth-century Europe, high-carbohydrate diets (refined sugar and white bread), poor oral hygiene, and limited fluoride exposure accelerated demineralization by acidogenic bacteria (notably Streptococcus mutans and Lactobacillus spp.). Untreated caries allows bacterial penetration to the pulp, provoking inflammation, ischemic necrosis, and spread to periapical tissues.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Iconographic signs include cheek clutching, facial swelling, open-mouthed wincing, and the attention of bystanders—all visual shorthand for acute odontogenic pain. The practitioner’s raised trophy-tooth often appears with a rag or instrument, implying hemorrhage control and the extraction of a carious or loosened tooth. Crowded viewers signal the high prevalence of oral disease and the social contagion of hope in quick cures.

[h] Treatment

Before asepsis and anesthesia, definitive therapy for irreversible pulpitis or abscess was extraction with forceps or pelicans. Pain control relied on alcohol, opiates, herbal anodynes, or simple restraint; antisepsis was absent, increasing risk of alveolar osteitis and invasive space infections. Skilled tooth-drawers could be effective, yet complications—fractured roots, retained infection, or hemorrhage—were common. Today, comparable pathology is treated with local anesthesia, pulpectomy/root canal therapy, antibiotics for spreading infection, and, when necessary, atraumatic extraction with sterile technique and post-operative analgesia.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

The painting points to structural drivers: poverty limits diet choices and hygiene materials; crowded housing and limited clean water hinder preventive care; and irregular labor reduces capacity to seek licensed practitioners. Cultural traditions—carnival, marketplace trust, charismatic healers—shape health-seeking behavior. Education level modulates susceptibility to quackery and understanding of preventive practices. These determinants remain relevant today, visible in contemporary gradients of caries burden across income, education, and neighborhood indices.
