A young woman aged 23 depicted before and after contracting cholera

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This coloured stipple engraving is attributable to an anonymous nineteenth‑century European medical illustrator and publisher. It belongs to a broad genre of didactic medical prints that sought to codify the appearance of disease for physicians and the lay public. The technique—stipple engraving hand‑tinted with watercolor—allowed for smooth gradients in flesh tones and subtle cyanotic hues, qualities essential to conveying pathological color changes. The image is preserved in medical art collections such as the Wellcome Collection, which amassed many such clinical portraits intended for atlases, popular broadsides, and public health campaigns.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The work most plausibly dates to the era of the second and third cholera pandemics (c. 1826–1860), when cholera spread from South Asia across Europe and the Americas. In these decades, cholera’s terrifying rapidity—healthy in the morning, moribund by night—made “before-and-after” imagery a persuasive visual format. The print participates in debates of the period between miasma theorists and emerging contagionists. By the 1850s, John Snow’s epidemiological investigations in London (notably the Broad Street pump episode) challenged environmentalist explanations and redirected attention to waterborne transmission. Public sanitation movements, the rise of municipal boards of health, and a burgeoning market for illustrated health ephemera provided the social matrix in which this image circulated.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The dyadic composition juxtaposes vigor and collapse. On the left, the sitter’s upright carriage, warm complexion, and tied blue scarf signal health, modesty, and social propriety. On the right, the head tilts, the mouth slackens, and the décolletage loosens, signifying loss of control and vitality. The pallor shaded toward bluish-grey evokes the “blue death,” a period term capturing peripheral cyanosis from hypovolemia and hypoxia. Sunken eyes and sharpened clavicles iconographically encode dehydration and catabolism. The artist reduces background to blank paper, a conventional strategy to isolate the body as bearer of diagnostic signs, converting the sitter’s likeness into a legible medical text.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Stipple engraving produces halftones via arrays of dots, ideal for modeling cheeks, lips, and ocular shadows. Color is restrained: limited blues and cool greys set up a high-contrast didactic pair. Balance is achieved through near-symmetry of bust-length portraits, while contrast (rosy vs. ashen skin; tidy knot vs. loosened neckline) drives the narrative of illness. Line remains delicate around features, while value shifts carry the medical information. The work exemplifies the nineteenth-century “clinical gaze,” in which style serves observational precision and moral legibility rather than individual psychology.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Cholera exploited urban poverty—crowded housing, contaminated wells, inadequate sewers—conditions concentrated among laboring classes, migrants, and colonized populations. Visual culture often encoded class bias: prints like this juxtaposed order and disorder, implicitly linking respectability with health and casting sufferers as cautionary figures. Women’s bodies in such imagery could be moralized, their dishevelment read as lapse in domestic hygiene. Meanwhile, state responses (cordons, quarantines) frequently burdened the poor while failing to address structural sanitation deficits.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Cholera is an acute watery diarrheal disease caused by toxigenic Vibrio cholerae, primarily serogroups O1 and O139. After ingestion via contaminated water or food, bacteria colonize the small intestine and express cholera toxin (an AB5 toxin encoded by CTXΦ bacteriophage). The A subunit ADP‑ribosylates Gsα, driving persistent adenylate cyclase activation, elevated cAMP, and massive chloride and water efflux through CFTR channels. The result is profuse “rice-water” stools, rapid dehydration, electrolyte loss (notably bicarbonate and potassium), metabolic acidosis, and shock. Incubation is typically 12 hours to 5 days.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The right-hand portrait compresses classic facies cholerica: sunken orbits, pinched nose, cyanotic lips, and skin turgor loss producing angularity of neck and chest. Additional clinical correlates (not pictured but characteristic) include afebrile presentation, tachycardia, hypotension, painful muscle cramps from hypokalemia, cold clammy extremities, oliguria/anuria, and “washerwoman’s hands” from dehydration-related wrinkling. The artist’s emphasis on perioral slackness and ocular hollowness mirrors the clinical priority of assessing hydration and perfusion.

[h] Treatment

Modern case management centers on rapid fluid and electrolyte replacement. Oral rehydration solution (ORS: glucose-salt solution exploiting sodium-glucose cotransport) is first-line for most patients; severe dehydration requires intravenous therapy, ideally Ringer’s lactate. Adjunctive antibiotics (e.g., doxycycline, azithromycin, or ciprofloxacin depending on local resistance) shorten illness and stool volume. Zinc supplementation benefits children. Killed oral vaccines (e.g., Shanchol, Euvichol-Plus, Dukoral) provide short‑term population-level protection in outbreaks. With prompt treatment, case fatality falls below 1%; historically, before rehydration therapy, mortality was high—an outcome hauntingly intimated by this print.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Risk is driven by WASH deficits: unsafe water sources, poor sanitation, and limited hand hygiene. Conflict, displacement, climate shocks (floods and cyclones), and fragile governance disrupt infrastructure and surveillance, amplifying outbreaks. Structural investments—piped water, fecal sludge management, cholera vaccination in hotspots, community-based ORS access, and data-informed rapid response—are the durable determinants that convert the image’s tragic “after” into a preventable outcome.
