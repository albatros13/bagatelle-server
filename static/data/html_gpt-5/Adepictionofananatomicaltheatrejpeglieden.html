[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This print belongs to the Dutch Republic’s early seventeenth‑century culture of anatomical illustration, often associated with Leiden University’s anatomical theater and the city’s active printmakers. Comparable images were designed by Jan Cornelisz. Woudanus and engraved by Willem van Swanenburgh around 1610 to publicize the theater’s scholarly prestige. Whether or not this impression is by those hands, it clearly participates in that milieu of university‑sponsored, publicly circulated images that translated learned anatomy into a civic spectacle.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The scene reflects the Dutch Golden Age, c. 1600–1630, when universities such as Leiden, founded in 1575, became centers for empirical inquiry. Winter dissections, timed for preservation of bodies, were simultaneously teaching exercises and events of urban pride, drawing magistrates, guild members, and travelers. The print also registers Reformation-era values: learning pursued as a communal, moral enterprise, with the body disciplined into an object of knowledge that could improve public health and civic reputation.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The raised skeleton holding a banner is a memento mori—death as the ultimate demonstrator—reminding viewers that anatomical knowledge proceeds through mortality. Displayed skulls and bones act as vanitas emblems, while the tiered amphitheater proclaims ordered ranks of knowledge and authority. Dogs at the steps, common in such depictions, can signify fidelity to truth; they also allude to comparative anatomy and the porous boundary between human and animal bodies in early modern inquiry. Instruments, jars, and the round dissection table function iconographically as attributes of the anatomist’s craft.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

The print’s linear system of parallel hatching, crisp contours, and strong perspective draws the eye to the illuminated cadaver. Concentric composition—audience, table, corpse—organizes the space around scientific seeing. Alternating bands of light and shadow create a chiaroscuro that dramatizes revelation: darkness recedes as anatomy is made legible. The crowded figuration and careful costume detail index the printer’s documentary ambitions, while the axial alignment between the corpse and the standing skeleton transforms the scene into a didactic allegory.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Access to bodies relied on judicial systems that supplied executed criminals and the unclaimed dead, disproportionately the poor and marginalized. Women appear rarely as subjects or practitioners, reflecting institutional exclusions. Colonial networks also entered the theater through “exotic” specimens and curiosities, embedding global asymmetries within local practices of demonstration. Seating stratification—professors, civic officials, then students and public—materializes hierarchies of power and knowledge.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

For early seventeenth‑century physicians, illness was interpreted through humoral theory—imbalances of blood, phlegm, black bile, and yellow bile—alongside growing anatomical empiricism after Vesalius (1543). Etiologies included miasma (corruption of air), contagion, diet and regimen, passions, and divine providence. Dissections sought to correlate symptoms reported in life with “morbid anatomy” found after death: ulcers of the gut, pulmonary lesions, calculi in bladder or gallbladder, aneurysms, and tumors. Leiden would later become a center for iatrochemical and mechanical explanations, but this image captures a transitional nexus where scholastic categories, humanist philology, and hands‑on cutting coexisted.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The opened abdomen signals a search for visceral pathology—sites where autopsies frequently revealed causes of death: perforated ulcers, peritonitis, volvulus, or hepatic disease. Jars and basins suggest the saving of organs or calculi as teaching specimens. The skeleton emphasizes the didactic mapping from structure to dysfunction: bony deformities, fractures, and dental disease were legible markers, while soft‑tissue lesions were described in lecture. Although no single disease is specified, the image encodes a repertoire of pathological signs becoming standard in European theaters.

[h] Treatment

Treatments in this period combined regimen (diet, sleep, exercise), phlebotomy and purgation, topical and internal botanicals, and surgical intervention for hernias, stones, or amputations. Mercury and guaiac were applied to venereal disease; cautery and ligature accompanied operations. Leiden instructors increasingly adopted chemical remedies and quantitative observation—precursors to later clinical pharmacology—while surgeons refined techniques using knowledge gained from cadaveric anatomy.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Urban density, seasonal labor, poor sanitation, and limited heating shaped exposure to infection and nutritional stress. Legal status determined risk of becoming a cadaver, underscoring how punishment and poverty intersected with medical supply chains. Literacy, guild membership, and university affiliation mediated access to care. Religious and ethical debates about dissection—permissible in the Dutch Republic but contested elsewhere—also structured whose bodies taught medicine and whose illnesses were recorded.
