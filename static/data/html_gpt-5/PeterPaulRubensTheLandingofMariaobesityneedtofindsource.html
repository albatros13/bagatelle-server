[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
Peter Paul Rubens (1577–1640) was a Flemish Baroque painter renowned for vast court commissions, dynamic compositions, and an aesthetic celebrating vigorous, fleshy bodies. Trained in Antwerp and influenced by Italian masters (Titian, Carracci), he led a large workshop and operated as a diplomat-artist. The work here relates to his Medici cycle, produced for Marie de’ Medici and now in the Louvre.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
Painted circa 1622–1625, the Medici cycle lauds the French queen mother through allegorical spectacle. Early seventeenth-century Europe embraced absolutist courts that used mythological pageantry to project power. Antwerp’s Catholic Baroque favored theatricality and sensuous surfaces in the wake of the Counter-Reformation. Within this courtly milieu, corpulent, glowing bodies signified prosperity, fertility, and dynastic abundance—values aligned with royal propaganda.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
In The Landing of Marie de’ Medici at Marseilles, sea deities—tritons and nereids—heave the ship ashore while personifications welcome the queen. The voluptuous sea nymphs symbolize nature’s submission to royal destiny and the fecundity associated with successful rule. Billowing drapery, marine fauna, and the churned surf signal divine orchestration. The sensual abundance of the figures forms an allegory of plenty, linking bodily plenitude to political promise.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
Rubens mobilizes sweeping diagonals, serpentine poses, and muscular torsions to produce motion. Thick, loaded brushwork and a warm, golden palette model flesh with palpable tactility. Chiaroscuro heightens relief against a turbulent sea, while overlapping bodies create deep spatial recession. The rhythm of curves and counter-curves, plus strategic highlights on shoulders, bellies, and thighs, guide the gaze across the pictorial field and unify the composition’s tumultuous energy.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
The painting’s celebration of idealized female bodies is embedded in a patriarchal court culture that instrumentalized women’s images to advance dynastic narratives. The bodies of the nereids are displayed for the viewer’s gaze as decorative guarantors of fertility and state vigor. Such imagery coexists with the historical marginalization of women’s agency in political decision-making and with class disparities—only elites could commission art equating abundance with virtue.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
From a biomedical standpoint, modern medicine defines obesity as excess adiposity that impairs health, commonly operationalized by BMI thresholds (≥30 kg/m² in adults). Etiology is multifactorial: chronic energy imbalance; genetic susceptibility (e.g., polygenic influences including FTO variants); endocrine and hypothalamic pathways governing appetite and satiety; medications; sleep, stress, and microbiome factors; and environmental drivers such as ultra-processed diets and sedentary built environments. Crucially, BMI is a population screening tool, not a diagnostic measure of health for individuals, and it cannot be applied to painted figures.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
Visually, Rubens’s nereids exhibit abundant subcutaneous fat with a gynoid distribution (hips and thighs), consistent with early modern ideals of fecund beauty. In clinical contexts, central (visceral) adiposity correlates more strongly with metabolic risk than peripheral fat. However, art cannot yield clinical inference: painterly exaggeration, allegorical intent, and period aesthetics mean these figures are signifiers of prosperity and sensuality, not medical documentation. Any interpretation of “obesity” in this image must be read as cultural symbolism, not diagnosis.

[h] Treatment
Contemporary management of obesity recognizes it as a chronic, relapsing disease. Effective care integrates: dietary quality and energy balance; increased physical activity and resistance training; behavioral therapy; and, when indicated, anti-obesity pharmacotherapy (e.g., GLP‑1 receptor agonists such as semaglutide, and dual GIP/GLP‑1 agonists such as tirzepatide) under medical supervision. For severe disease or refractory cases, bariatric metabolic surgery (sleeve gastrectomy, Roux‑en‑Y gastric bypass) provides durable weight loss and cardiometabolic improvement. Interventions should be stigma-free and individualized, with long-term follow-up.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Body size is shaped by social context. Food environments, marketing, neighborhood walkability, shift work, psychosocial stress, and access to preventive care all influence risk. Economic inequality and food insecurity can paradoxically promote excess weight through reliance on calorie-dense, inexpensive foods. Weight stigma itself undermines health by discouraging care-seeking and increasing stress. Historically, as in Rubens’s court culture, larger bodies signified wealth; in many modern societies, the burden of obesity falls disproportionately on disadvantaged groups—an inversion highlighting how cultural values and material conditions co-determine bodies.
