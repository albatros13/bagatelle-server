A Surgeon Holding a Dental Key Behind His Back to Conceal It from the Patient (1900)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Luciano Nezzo was an Italian painter active at the turn of the twentieth century, associated with genre realism. His work, often intimate and observational, explored everyday scenes with a compassionate yet unsentimental eye. In this painting he directs attention to the fraught encounter between healer and patient, a subject that allowed him to probe ethics, trust, and the body.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Painted circa 1900, the scene sits at a hinge in European medical history. Dentistry was professionalizing, with dental schools, licensing, and scientific bacteriology reshaping practice. Yet in rural and working-class contexts, tooth-pulling by barber-surgeons or itinerant practitioners remained common. Listerian antisepsis was known but unevenly adopted; anesthetics such as nitrous oxide and ether were available in cities but not guaranteed for poor patients. The “dental key,” once ubiquitous for extracting molars, was being supplanted by safer forceps, but it persisted where resources and training lagged.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The hidden dental key is the picture’s moral axis. Concealment literalizes paternalism: knowledge and power are kept behind the practitioner’s back. The patient’s tilted head and parted lips expose vulnerability, while the surgeon’s splayed, work-worn hand frames the mouth like a vise. The richly patterned shawl against a black ground heightens theater and signals the sitter’s ordinary social identity; she is not an aristocratic patron but a person of modest means whose body is the site of intervention. The tool itself—called a “key” because it turned like a key in a lock—becomes an icon of pain “unlocked” rather than relieved.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Nezzo employs close cropping and a shallow depth to compress space, forcing the viewer into the clinical encounter. Strong chiaroscuro isolates faces and hands, creating emphasis through contrast. The diagonal formed by the woman’s jawline and the surgeon’s shoulder generates movement toward the mouth, while the counter-diagonal of the concealed instrument seeds tension. Tactile brushwork in skin and cloth conveys material specificity—the rough knuckles, worn fabric, gleaming metal—amplifying realism and the embodied stakes of the moment. Balance is asymmetrical, with mass on the right countered by chromatic interest on the left.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The painting exposes inequities in access to safe oral care. Poor and rural patients often faced unregulated practitioners, limited anesthesia, and nonsterile technique. Gendered power dynamics are palpable: a male provider, a female patient, and a one-directional flow of information. The concealment of the instrument suggests an ethics of persuasion without informed consent, a historical norm that disproportionately affected vulnerable populations.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The implied condition is painful dental caries progressing to pulpitis and possibly apical periodontitis, common in the era of rising sugar consumption and limited preventive care. Dental caries is a biofilm-mediated, sugar-driven dysbiosis: acidogenic bacteria (notably Streptococcus mutans and Lactobacillus spp.) metabolize fermentable carbohydrates to acids, lowering plaque pH and demineralizing enamel and dentin. Without intervention, bacterial invasion of the pulp incites inflammation, necrosis, and periapical infection. Contributing factors include high sucrose diets, infrequent mechanical plaque control, lack of fluoride, and socioeconomic barriers to routine treatment.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

While painterly, the iconography hints at posterior molar involvement—the dental key was typically applied to molars. The patient’s extended neck and open mouth suggest examination or imminent extraction; her hand gripping the practitioner’s sleeve signals anxiety and nociceptive anticipation. The dentist’s thumb stabilizing the mandible is consistent with historical extraction technique. Such scenes often followed symptoms of throbbing pain, thermal sensitivity, facial tenderness, and halitosis associated with advanced decay or abscess.

[h] Treatment

Historically, management in settings like this was extraction with a dental key: the claw was placed on the crown, tightened, and rotated to luxate the tooth—effective yet traumatic, risking root fracture, alveolar bone damage, and infection. Analgesia might have been alcohol or, less commonly in rural practice, nitrous oxide or ether; antisepsis varied. Contemporary care would prioritize caries prevention, restorative therapy, or endodontic treatment to preserve the tooth; when extraction is indicated, elevators and forceps with local anesthesia are standard, with aseptic technique and postoperative analgesia. Antibiotics are reserved for systemic involvement or spreading infection.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Dietary sugar availability, income, education, and geographic access to qualified providers are principal determinants. Fluoridated water and affordable toothpaste reduce risk, but these were absent or inconsistent at the time. Labor conditions, childcare, and gendered autonomy affect the ability to seek timely care. Trust in institutions—shaped by prior experiences of pain and deception—also influences uptake of preventive services.
