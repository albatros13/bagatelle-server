[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This image is an anonymous medical illustration, likely ink on paper converted to print through engraving or lithographic transfer. The draughtsman privileges clinical legibility rather than portraiture, building form with cross‑hatched lines. Lacking signature or institutional seal, it is best read as the product of a clinical atlas or journal workshop in which artists translated bedside observation into reproducible teaching figures.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Formal features suggest a late nineteenth to early twentieth‑century context, a period when hospitals, public health services, and tropical medicine institutes standardized visual documentation. Photography coexisted with drawing; engravings circulated globally through textbooks and reports. The era’s scientism prized “objective” looking, yet the social identity of sitters was often effaced, reflecting hierarchies of clinical authority and the growing state interest in mapping disease across populations.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The iconography is resolutely clinical. A three-quarter view isolates the periorbital region, inviting scrutiny of unilateral swelling, ptosis, and facial asymmetry. Clothing is schematized into stripes that act as ground and scale. The closed eyelid and downcast head stage the face as a site of signs rather than a biography—an instructive, diagnostic image rather than an expressive portrait.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Design choices direct attention to pathology. Cross‑hatching models soft‑tissue volume; sparing whites mark highlights. Line direction follows anatomical curvature, turning anatomy into legible tone. A blank ground heightens contrast. Slight amplification of the affected orbit may be intentional to secure notice. Unity comes from a narrow vocabulary of line; emphasis and balance are achieved through tonal hierarchy rather than color.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Such images arose within unequal clinical relations. Patients were recorded but seldom named, and consent standards were inconsistent. Atlases disproportionately pictured people with limited care access, shaping public narratives through the lens of pathology. Reuse without context can perpetuate stigma. Ethical viewing reconnects the picture to infrastructures—housing, sanitation, wages—that made certain diseases visible and others invisible.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

A unilateral, heavy upper eyelid with periorbital and malar swelling is the dominant sign. Plausible differentials include: acute American trypanosomiasis with Romaña sign after conjunctival inoculation; trachomatous inflammation leading to edema and progressive scarring; bacterial preseptal or orbital cellulitis; plexiform neurofibroma producing mechanical ptosis and tissue overgrowth; and localized lymphedema from filarial obstruction. Etiologies span protozoa (Trypanosoma cruzi), Chlamydia trachomatis, Staphylococcus/Streptococcus species, germline NF1 variants disrupting Ras signaling, and filarial nematodes. The image stabilizes pattern recognition while leaving etiology indeterminate—a reminder that visual evidence alone cannot close the diagnostic loop.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Noted signs: ptosis; eyelid and infraorbital edema; cheek fullness; subtle effacement of the palpebral fold; apparent absence of purulent discharge. Head inclination may indicate photophobia or fatigue. If interpreted as Romaña sign, the edema is classically unilateral and relatively painless following a bite or contamination by triatomine bug feces. In trachoma, chronic follicular conjunctivitis, pannus, and eventual trichiasis/entropion are expected though not rendered here. Orbital cellulitis typically presents with fever, painful extraocular movements, and proptosis—features not explicit in the drawing.

[h] Treatment

Historical interventions ranged from caustics to nonspecific antiseptics, often ineffective. Contemporary management is etiology‑specific: benznidazole or nifurtimox for acute Chagas disease; the WHO‑endorsed SAFE strategy (Surgery for trichiasis, Antibiotics, Facial cleanliness, Environmental improvement) for trachoma; intravenous broad‑spectrum antibiotics and possible surgical drainage for orbital cellulitis; debulking or reconstruction for plexiform neurofibroma; and antiparasitics plus lymphedema care for filariasis. The artwork underscores that therapy rests on careful history, laboratory confirmation, and attention to complications.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Vector ecology, sanitation, crowding, indoor residual spraying, housing materials, and reliable access to care shape risk. Labor migration and economic precarity delay diagnosis and increase exposure. Education, language access, and stigma around visible facial differences affect adherence to therapy. Durable prevention couples biomedical treatment with infrastructure: clean water, waste management, vector control, and primary care reachable without catastrophic cost.
