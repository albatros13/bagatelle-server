PE html> Pieter van Laer, The Flagellants (c. 1635)

The Flagellants (c. 1635), attributed to Pieter van Laer

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
Pieter van Laer (c. 1599–c. 1642), a Dutch painter active in Rome, was known by the nickname “Il Bamboccio” and is closely associated with the Bamboccianti, a circle of mainly Northern European artists who depicted everyday Roman life. Van Laer blended Northern naturalism with Italianate settings, elevating seemingly humble subjects—street scenes, beggars, confraternities—into serious pictorial narratives. The scene of hooded penitents processing among the poor exemplifies his interest in civic ritual and the social realities of the early modern city.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
The painting belongs to the 1630s, a period marked in Italy by the Counter-Reformation’s emphasis on visible acts of piety and by recurrent epidemic disease, including the devastating Italian plagues of 1629–1631. Penitential confraternities, sometimes practicing public flagellation, were part of urban religious life in Rome and other centers. Their rituals intersected with charity, hospital care, and funerary services. Artists of the Bamboccianti attended closely to these practices, offering viewers both ethnography and moral reflection in a time when medicine, religion, and municipal authority overlapped in responding to illness and poverty.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
The white-hooded robes and pointed capirotes signal a penitential confraternity, whose members enacted self-mortification as expiation for communal sin and as intercession during outbreaks. The church façade, steps, and gathering of the needy form a triad of sacred space, ritual procession, and social obligation. Dogs, children, and derelict architecture serve as vanitas-like motifs of precarity and transience. Almsgiving gestures near the church doors convey institutional charity, while the stark whiteness of the confratelli against the dark courtyard dramatizes the claimed purity of penitence within a morally and epidemiologically contaminated city.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
Van Laer uses a low chromatic key and a controlled chiaroscuro to model figures and to emphasize volume within shallow, stage-like architecture. The diagonal recession of rooflines and walls creates a perspectival funnel that guides the eye toward the communal center of action. Scale is compressed: small, carefully observed figures populate the space, reinforcing the painter’s documentary impulse. Balance is achieved through counterposed groups—the white-robed pair at left offset by clusters of women, children, and infirm at right—while rhythm emerges from repeated triangular silhouettes of hoods and gables.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
The image makes visible hierarchies of health, class, and gender. Confraternity members embody sanctioned religious authority; the poor and the sick queue for aid at the threshold of the church. Women appear as caregivers and recipients of charity, reflecting uneven burdens during crises. The scene also hints at spatial inequity: dilapidated structures and cramped courtyards where contagion could thrive juxtapose with the more stable institutional presence of the church. Such imagery registers how ritual discipline and charity coexisted with structural poverty that intensified disease vulnerability.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
While the painting does not diagnose a specific illness, its penitential ritual and relief setting resonate with plague culture in seventeenth-century Italy. The principal agent in bubonic plague is Yersinia pestis, transmitted primarily via flea vectors on rodents, with pneumonic forms capable of human-to-human aerosol spread. Contemporary observers debated miasma (bad air), astral influences, and moral causation; contagionist ideas led to quarantines and lazaretti. Modern microbiology clarifies vector ecology, reservoir species, and the conditions—crowding, trade networks, and sanitation deficits—that facilitated periodic epidemics.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
Artistic signifiers of illness appear in bent postures, assisted gait, wrapped limbs, and the clustering of supplicants who display need rather than clinical lesions. In historical plague, patients often presented with high fever, painful buboes (inguinal, axillary, cervical), prostration, and petechial hemorrhages; pneumonic cases produced cough and hemoptysis. Artists typically encoded such states indirectly through gesture and social context. Here, the visual grammar—outstretched hands for alms, isolation at thresholds, and the presence of caregivers—communicates the lived experience of disease without anatomical explicitness.

[h] Treatment
Seventeenth-century responses combined spiritual and medical measures. Confraternities organized processions, vows, and burial services; municipal authorities imposed cordons sanitaires, quarantine, and hospital isolation. Physicians employed bloodletting, purgatives, and theriac, along with fumigation and aromatic prophylactics rooted in humoral and miasmatic theories. From a modern standpoint, evidence-based treatment for plague includes rapid antibiotics (e.g., streptomycin, gentamicin, doxycycline) and supportive care; public health focuses on surveillance, vector control, and targeted prophylaxis for exposures. The work underscores a historical moment when ritual consolation met nascent public health infrastructure.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Poverty, overcrowded housing, seasonal labor migration, and limited sanitation heightened morbidity. Food insecurity and war-related displacement weakened immunity and increased exposure. Access to institutional charity, membership in confraternities, and proximity to hospitals shaped who received care or burial; stigma could marginalize the visibly ill. The painting captures these determinants—space, social role, and access—by situating bodies at the civic-religious interface where relief and regulation converged.
