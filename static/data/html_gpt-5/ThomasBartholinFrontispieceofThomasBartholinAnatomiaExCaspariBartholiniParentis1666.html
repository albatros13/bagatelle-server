Frontispiece of Thomas Bartholin Anatomia Ex Caspari Bartholini Parentis (1666)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Thomas Bartholin (1616–1680), the Danish anatomist famous for describing the human lymphatic system, is the author of the book whose 1666 edition this frontispiece introduces. The plate likely involved a professional engraver working for the printer Adrian Vlacq at The Hague; while the specific engraver is uncertain, the image functions as a visual manifesto for Bartholin’s “Anatomia reformata,” signaling a renewal of anatomical knowledge in the wake of earlier authorities, including his father, Caspar Bartholin the Elder.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The mid-17th century was an era of vigorous reassessment of the human body in Northern Europe. Following the Vesalian revolution of 1543, anatomy shifted from bookish scholasticism to hands-on dissection. University anatomy theaters proliferated, and printing centers in the Dutch Republic circulated illustrated medical texts across Europe. In this milieu, Bartholin consolidated and revised anatomical learning, integrating observations from public dissections and recent discoveries—especially of the lymphatics (1652–1654)—that challenged Galenic circulation models. The Latin title and typographic framing (“Hagae-Comitis… Adriani Vlacq… 1666”) place the work firmly in a humanist, pan-European republic of letters.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The image presents a flayed human skin hung like a drapery, a stark trompe-l’œil that merges two potent early modern motifs: the “anatomy theater” curtain and the memento mori. The skin-as-veil suggests that scientific inquiry pulls back the covering of appearances to reveal corporeal truth. Simultaneously, it alludes to classical and Christian narratives of flaying—Marsyas and Saint Bartholomew—associating knowledge with sacrifice. Inscribed text on the torso functions as an ex libris carved into flesh, implying that anatomy is literally written on the body. The framing architecture elevates the specimen to the status of a reliquary or altar piece, sacralizing empirical study.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Executed in fine intaglio, the plate relies on cross-hatching, stippling, and contour lines to simulate texture: the coarse granularity of epidermis, the sag of loosened dermis, and the ragged margins where scalpels have parted skin from fascia. Strong verticals of the architectural surround stabilize the composition while diagonal folds animate the hanging “garment.” The illusionism aims to persuade—precision of line stands in for precision of observation—linking graphic craft to scientific credibility.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Seventeenth-century dissections drew disproportionately on the bodies of executed criminals, the unclaimed poor, and marginalized strangers. Consent was rare; punishment and poverty structured the supply of cadavers, embedding inequity into the foundations of anatomical science. Latin publication restricted readership to learned elites, even as the knowledge produced shaped the treatment of broader populations. The image’s theatrical display of a nameless body echoes these asymmetries: the specimen is rhetorically universal yet socially specific.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

While the frontispiece is not a case illustration, its focus on the integument invites reflection on skin disease prevalent in the period. Smallpox, caused by the variola virus, was endemic and frequently disfiguring or fatal. Syphilis, due to Treponema pallidum, circulated widely in Europe and manifested dermatologically in secondary stages. From a modern biomedical perspective, skin functions as an immune barrier populated by keratinocytes, Langerhans cells, and a dense lymphatic plexus. Bartholin’s own emphasis on lymphatics provides a conceptual bridge to understanding how cutaneous infections drain to regional nodes and disseminate.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The peppered surface and pitted textures in the engraving are graphic conventions, yet they resonate with historical markers of disease: umbilicated pustules and residual pockmarks of smallpox; coppery maculopapular eruptions of secondary syphilis; and scabbed lacerations consistent with post-mortem flaying. Early modern viewers, familiar with epidemic scarring, would have read such surfaces as legible signs of bodily history. Importantly, any specific diagnosis from this image remains speculative; the plate’s primary aim is didactic and emblematic.

[h] Treatment

In the 1660s, therapeutic options for smallpox were largely supportive—isolation, fluid balance, and management of secondary infection—while variolation would only gain European currency in the early 18th century. Syphilis was treated with mercury and guaiacum, interventions of limited efficacy and significant toxicity. Surgical technique for dissection emphasized rapid separation of skin from subcutaneous layers without anesthesia or antisepsis; preservation relied on alcohols and aromatics. Modern treatments contrast sharply: vaccination eradicated smallpox; syphilis is curable with penicillin; and aseptic technique with antibiotics governs all invasive procedures.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Crowded housing, wartime mobility, malnutrition, and unequal access to care intensified 17th-century morbidity. Disease burden mapped onto class and occupation, with the urban poor most vulnerable to contagion and to post-mortem appropriation. Printed anatomical knowledge could, paradoxically, both ameliorate and reproduce inequality: it advanced surgery and pathology while relying on bodies obtained through coercive systems. The frontispiece thus indexes not only scientific reform but also the social scaffolding that enabled it.
