Gaetano Giulio Zumbo, The Plague (17th century)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Gaetano Giulio Zumbo (1656–1701) was a Sicilian-born master of polychrome wax, active in Florence and later in Paris. Trained amid Counter-Reformation devotional practices and courtly curiosities, he specialized in extraordinarily naturalistic wax tableaux that fused devotional moralizing with forensic observation. In Florence he worked under Medici patronage and later collaborated with the surgeon Guillaume Desnoues on anatomical preparations, bringing surgical accuracy to sculptural practice. The Plague is among his most compelling “theatres” of mortality, where wax’s translucency and malleability serve both art and proto-pathological demonstration.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Created in the late seventeenth century, the work reflects an Italy still haunted by the devastating plague waves of 1629–31 and 1656–57. Florence’s intellectual climate—shaped by academies, cabinets of curiosity, and the Medici’s scientific patronage—encouraged intersections between art, natural philosophy, and medicine. Wax modeling thrived as a medium for religious imagery and, increasingly, for anatomical education. At the same time, urban authorities were consolidating public health systems—quarantines, lazarettos, and sanitary boards—born from earlier epidemics. The tableau translates this milieu: Baroque theatricality meets empirical scrutiny, turning disaster into a site of moral reflection and practical medical instruction.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The ruinous architectural backdrop signifies civic breakdown under epidemic pressure; toppled masonry and a desolate horizon suggest a city abandoned by order and ritual. The piled bodies visualize collective mortality, while the lone upright figure dramatizes the liminal state between life and death. Wax coloration—greenish pallor, violaceous mottling, ashen yellows—functions iconographically as the chromatic language of decay. Contrapposto and collapsing diagonals evoke the fall of human pride, echoing memento mori themes. The composition borrows from Christian lamentation scenes yet secularizes them: universal suffering replaces specific saints, turning the image into an allegory of social fragility and biological vulnerability.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Zumbo deploys Baroque drama through a tight, stage-like relief: emphatic diagonals drive the eye across cadavers, while a strong chiaroscuro heightens corporeal volume. The medium is crucial: pigmented wax blends beeswax, resins, and pigments to achieve epidermal translucency, sheen, and the variegated hues of putrefaction—effects unreachable in marble or bronze. Texture ranges from taut, desiccated skin to swollen, edematous surfaces, and from crumbling stone to shredded cloth, engaging tactile imagination. Rhythms of repetition (limbs, torsos) create a visual fugue of death; asymmetry and imbalance sustain tension, and the shallow depth compresses time and space, intensifying affect and clinical legibility.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Epidemics historically magnify inequality. The poor, living in crowded quarters and constrained by precarious labor, were more exposed and less able to flee. Access to physicians, clean burial, and relief was uneven; service and caregiving labor—often performed by women and the marginalized—carried heightened risk. Quarantine regimes, while effective, could be coercive, curtailing movement and livelihoods. The tableau’s anonymous, massed dead underscores how social hierarchies collapse in mortality yet structure exposure, care, and remembrance in life.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The historical referent is plague caused by Yersinia pestis, a gram-negative bacterium maintained in rodent reservoirs and transmitted primarily via flea vectors. Bubonic plague presents after a short incubation (2–6 days) with fever, chills, and tender lymphadenopathy (buboes), typically in the groin, axilla, or neck. Septicemic spread may lead to disseminated intravascular coagulation and purpura; pneumonic plague, acquired via respiratory droplets, causes rapidly progressive pneumonia and is the most contagious form. Without timely treatment, mortality is high, particularly for septicemic and pneumonic presentations.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Zumbo’s figures display clustered groin and axillary swellings suggestive of buboes; mottled, livid skin hints at purpura and acral gangrene from septicemia. The wax’s waxy yellows and greens suggest jaundice and postmortem changes; rigid, arched postures recall perimortem spasm and early rigor. Open mouths and retracted thoraxes intimate air hunger of terminal infection or pneumonic involvement. The artist’s palette—violets, greens, sallow ochres—functions diagnostically, approximating the chromatic signatures of hypoxia, cyanosis, and decomposition recognizable to early modern observers and modern pathologists alike.

[h] Treatment

Seventeenth-century responses mixed empirical and pre-modern practice: isolation, fumigation, and lazaretto quarantine sat alongside ineffective cures such as theriac, bloodletting, and purgatives. Contemporary evidence-based care centers on early antibiotics—streptomycin or gentamicin, with doxycycline or ciprofloxacin as alternatives—plus supportive management of sepsis and respiratory failure. Post-exposure prophylaxis for close contacts, vector control, and rapid case detection underpin public health control. Vaccines are not in widespread general use; prevention focuses on surveillance, rodent-flea control, and swift clinical intervention.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Risk concentrates where human–rodent contact is high: crowded housing, poor sanitation, and precarious waste management. Trade and mobility distribute vectors along commercial networks; climatic variability influences rodent and flea ecologies. Education, trust in authorities, and access to healthcare shape timely presentation and adherence to containment measures. Zumbo’s devastated cityscape mirrors these determinants: infrastructures of shelter, labor, and governance failing under the biological and social pressures of epidemic crisis.
