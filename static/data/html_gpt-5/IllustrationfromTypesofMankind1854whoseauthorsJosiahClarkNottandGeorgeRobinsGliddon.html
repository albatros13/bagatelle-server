[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This engraved plate was compiled by physicians and ethnologists Josiah Clark Nott and George Robins Gliddon for the 1854 volume Types of Mankind. The book assembled images from classical sculpture, human profiles, skulls, and apes to argue a polygenist theory of separate human “types.” Although presented as science, the visual program functioned as propaganda for racial hierarchy and slavery, drawing on the craniological measurements popularized by Samuel George Morton and other proponents of scientific racism.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Produced in the antebellum United States on the eve of the Civil War, the plate belongs to a period when ethnology and comparative anatomy were enlisted to legitimize colonialism and chattel slavery. Polygenism contended that so-called races were created separately and unequally. This idea circulated in medical schools, museums, and learned societies, shaping curricula and public policy. The layout’s numbered figures and descriptive captions echo 19th-century natural history atlases, leveraging the authority of science to buttress social order.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The juxtaposition of a classical “Greek” head with profiled heads labeled with racialized terms (historically used, now recognized as offensive) and with apes constructs a ladder from idealized beauty to supposed primitivism. The recurrent profile view—favored by physiognomy and phrenology—invites comparison of nose, lips, and chin to assert claims about character and intellect. Skulls serve as icons of metric objectivity, while the inclusion of chimpanzees and orangutans insinuates proximity between human groups and nonhuman primates, a core visual trope of the era’s racial pseudoscience.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Executed in crisp line engraving with cross-hatching, the images adopt the taxonomic plate format: discrete specimens, uniform scale, serial numbering, and minimal background. The grid-like arrangement creates rhythm and repetition that imply comparability. The strong contour lines and high-contrast modeling intensify legibility, allowing viewers to “measure by eye.” This aesthetic of clarity, common in anatomical atlases, disguises ideological intent as neutral description—an example of how design choices can create an aura of truth.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

These plates naturalized inequality by treating enslaved and colonized peoples as biologically inferior. Such imagery influenced medical teaching, policing, and labor policy, contributing to practices that denied care, justified experimentation, and codified segregation. Its afterlives persisted in eugenics, immigration restriction, and in medical heuristics that conflated race with biology. Understanding this visual culture is essential to tracing how structural racism became embedded in institutions, with durable impacts on health and longevity.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

While no biological disease is depicted, the plate advances a biomedical claim: that cranial form and brain size determine intellect and morality and map neatly onto racial “types.” The etiologic engine of this claim was methodological error—typological thinking, selective sampling, ambiguous categories, and confirmation bias—combined with the political economy of slavery. Modern genetics shows that human variation is clinal and overlapping; most genetic diversity occurs within, not between, populations. Brain volume poorly predicts cognitive ability, which is influenced by education, environment, and polygenic factors. Thus, the “diagnosis” implied by the plate is a categorical error born of ideology.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The illustrations mobilize pseudo-diagnostic signs: prognathism, nasal index, and cephalic proportion are treated as symptom-like markers of intelligence or worth. The staging of heads from classical sculpture to apes mimics a pathological scale, implying degeneration. These signifiers do not index disease; rather, they are visual symptoms of racial science—an epistemic pathology in which iconography masquerades as evidence.

[h] Treatment

Effective “treatment” is historiographic and scientific. It includes: recontextualizing such images with critical labels; transparent reanalysis of historical measurements; replacing racial typologies with population genetics and ancestry-informative frameworks; and reforming clinical algorithms that used race as a biological proxy (for example, removing race adjustments from kidney function equations). Community-engaged curation and open data reduce the authority of outdated claims and build trust.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

The material legacy of images like this persists through discrimination, residential segregation, environmental exposure, and unequal access to care—determinants that drive disparities in maternal mortality, chronic disease, and life expectancy. The mechanism is social, not innate: racism, not race, structures risk. Addressing these determinants requires policy change, equity-centered medical education, and rigorous attention to how historical narratives shape present outcomes.
