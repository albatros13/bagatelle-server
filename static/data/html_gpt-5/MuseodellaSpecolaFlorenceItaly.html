Museo della Specola Florence Italy

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The gallery presents Florentine ceroplastic (wax) anatomy, chiefly associated with Clemente Susini (1754–1814) and the workshop at La Specola under the Grand Duchy of Tuscany. These makers belonged to a hybrid community of artisans, anatomists, and modelers who collaborated with physicians from the Hospital of Santa Maria Nuova and the University of Florence. Earlier precedents include Gaetano Giulio Zumbo, whose Baroque waxes established the expressive possibilities of the medium. The works were conceived as faithful, disassemblable models for medical training, crafted by specialists who combined sculptural virtuosity with dissection-based observation.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Produced in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, the collection sits at the confluence of Enlightenment empiricism and courtly patronage. The Medici and, later, Lorraine rulers invested in public science, founding La Specola (opened 1775) as part of a natural history museum. Wax anatomy emerged as an ethical and logistical solution to limited cadaver access and the rapid decay of organic tissue. The period also witnessed reforms of medical education, the rise of obstetrics, and a new culture of display in which knowledge was public-facing. Cabinets, vitrines, and serial panels reflect pedagogical order; yet the theatrical staging—silk pillows, glass cases, and life-sized écorché figures—maintained the aura of spectacle typical of early museums.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

Although didactic, these objects also operate within a symbolic field. The écorché—figures with skin removed—visualizes the body as a text to be read, dramatizing mortality and human interiority. The recumbent “cadaver” poses echo Christian pietà imagery, situating scientific inquiry within a moral meditation on death. Serial organ panels create an iconography of systems (vascular, muscular, reproductive), translating invisible processes into legible signs. Even when pathologies are not explicitly depicted, the emaciated forms recall “consumption,” a cultural shorthand for fragility and romanticized illness in the long eighteenth century.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Ceroplastics exploit wax’s translucency to simulate living tissue. Pigments are stippled into layers to model capillary blush and organ tonality; glass eyes and hair amplify verisimilitude. The cases orchestrate sightlines: diagonal coffins in the foreground establish perspective, while gridded panels along the walls impose taxonomic order (repetition and rhythm). Contrast between warm wooden vitrines and the luminous, flesh-toned wax heightens the tension between artifice and nature. Proportion is exacting—scaled to life—so that the viewer encounters a body both sculptural and clinical. These design decisions anchor the gallery’s dual function as aesthetic environment and epistemic instrument.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The making of such collections relied on unequal access to bodies, often those of the poor, unclaimed, or criminalized—a legacy shared with dissection theaters across Europe. Women’s bodies were frequently displayed in obstetric series as archetypes of reproduction, reflecting gendered medical scrutiny. The museum’s taxonomies universalize a European body while masking class and colonial asymmetries that structured medical knowledge production and circulation.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

As a lens for biomedical interpretation, tuberculosis (TB) is instructive. Known then as phthisis or consumption, TB is caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, transmitted through airborne droplets. In the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, its etiology was debated—hereditary, miasmatic, or moral causes were proposed—until Koch’s 1882 identification of the bacillus. Wax models at La Specola primarily present normative anatomy, yet the museum context supported instruction in diseases like TB through organ preparations and didactic comparisons of healthy versus diseased tissue.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

TB pathology characteristically includes granulomatous inflammation, caseating necrosis, and, in pulmonary forms, cavitary lesions. Systemically, wasting, rib prominence, and spinal deformity from vertebral involvement (Pott’s disease) can occur. In visual pedagogy, such signs are conveyed by sectional lungs revealing cavities, nodes with caseation, and emaciated thoraces. Even when not pathologic per se, the écorché aesthetic—taut musculature, visible ribs, hollowed abdomens—trained students to read the body’s surface as clues to internal disease, a diagnostic habit that bridged art and clinic.

[h] Treatment

Historical management of TB emphasized rest, fresh air, and diet in sanatoria; adjuvants included cod-liver oil and, earlier, misguided bloodletting. The modern standard is multidrug therapy—commonly a two-month intensive phase with isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol, followed by continuation therapy—tailored by susceptibility testing. Drug-resistant TB demands longer regimens with additional agents and adherence support. The shift from moralized regimens to bacteriologically targeted therapy reflects the epistemic arc from Enlightenment anatomy to germ theory.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

TB thrives where poverty, crowding, malnutrition, and limited ventilation intersect, and is compounded by comorbidities such as HIV, diabetes, or silicosis. Incarceration, migration, and precarious labor intensify exposure and impede treatment completion. The museum’s Enlightenment dream of universal knowledge coexisted with unequal health realities; likewise today, TB outcomes trace lines of housing, income, and access to care. Reading these wax bodies historically thus invites contemporary reflection on structural determinants of disease.
