[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
The composite image juxtaposes two traditions: a European medical illustration representing “lepra” and “elephantiasis” (often associated in modern scholarship with neurofibromatosis imagery) and a late sixteenth‑century English court portrait by an anonymous artist of the English School. The medical plate belongs to the long line of Enlightenment and nineteenth‑century didactic atlases in which anonymous draftsmen collaborated with physicians to render pathologies for teaching.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
The medical sheet emerges from a period when European medicine increasingly cataloged bodies through nosology, museums, and printed atlases. At the same time, court portraiture in Elizabethan England used costume, posture, and emblematic props to project status, navigating a culture of imperial ambition and strict social hierarchy. These distinct spheres—clinic and court—shared a fascination with visibility: the former sought to visualize disease; the latter visualized authority and virtue.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
In the medical image, walking sticks, partial drapery, and staged nude poses echo classical prototypes while guiding the viewer’s attention to cutaneous surfaces and limb contours. Such props function as diagnostic arrows, emphasizing swelling, nodularity, or deformity. The court portrait’s rich textiles, globe, and armor-like costume signal exploration, command, and decorum. A vivid facial flush, common in this portrait genre, has been read by later viewers as mimicking erythema; here it operates iconographically as vigor and warmth rather than as a clinical attestation.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
The medical plate relies on contour line, minimal background, and cool washes that isolate lesions against pale skin grounds—clarity and legibility are prioritized over expressive flourish. Repetition of three figures enables comparative seeing, a proto-epidemiologic aesthetics of pattern recognition. The court portrait mobilizes strong axial symmetry, saturated reds, and crisp highlights on satin to sculpt authority. Textural contrasts—stiff ruff versus soft flesh—activate surface as a sign of rank and discipline.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
The pairing underscores historical inequities in how bodies were seen. Diseased or impoverished bodies were rendered as typologies in manuals, their individuality effaced in favor of pedagogy. By contrast, elite sitters were singularized and aggrandized. Stigma around visible difference often led to exclusion from guilds, marriage markets, and civic life, while court portraiture helped reproduce the legitimacy of those who controlled resources, including early medical institutions.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
The left image references conditions historically labeled “lepra” and “elephantiasis,” a terminology that blurred entities we now separate: Hansen’s disease (Mycobacterium leprae infection), lymphatic filariasis–related elephantiasis (mosquito-borne Wuchereria bancrofti and related parasites), and neurocutaneous disorders such as neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF1), a genetic condition due to pathogenic variants in NF1 disrupting RAS/MAPK signaling. The right portrait’s ruddy facial tonality has been compared by modern commentators to the visual phenotype of erythematotelangiectatic rosacea; such readings remain interpretive and should not be treated as diagnosis of the historical sitter.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
In the medical illustration, artists accentuate nodular cutaneous growths, pendulous soft-tissue masses, and asymmetric limb girth—visual signifiers that align with neurofibromas, plexiform thickening, or lymphedema. Other markers, such as altered posture or reliance on a staff, denote functional impairment. In portraits, painters used controlled redness at the cheeks and nose and fine vermilion lines to suggest vascular flush; while visually akin to rosacea’s centrofacial erythema and telangiectasia, this remains a stylistic convention rather than clinical proof.

[h] Treatment
Historically, early modern regimens ranged from topical unguents and bloodletting to isolation for “leprosy,” and mercury-based or botanical remedies—often ineffective and harmful. Contemporary care diverges: NF1 management emphasizes surveillance, surgical debulking of symptomatic tumors, and targeted therapy (e.g., MEK inhibition with selumetinib for pediatric inoperable plexiform neurofibromas). Lymphatic filariasis is addressed through mass drug administration (ivermectin or diethylcarbamazine with albendazole) and morbidity management for lymphedema. Hansen’s disease is curable with multidrug therapy. For rosacea-like facial erythema, modern options include trigger avoidance, topical metronidazole or azelaic acid, vasoconstrictive agents, and pulsed-dye laser for telangiectasia.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Poverty, crowding, vector exposure, and inadequate sanitation historically amplified elephantiasis and Hansen’s disease, while stigma curtailed care-seeking. Genetic disorders like NF1 intersect with social determinants through access to specialty care, insurance coverage, and disability accommodation. In contrast, elites immortalized in portraits had access to physicians and to images that framed redness as health, not pathology. The visual economy of illness is thus inseparable from labor conditions, environment, and class-coded notions of beauty and value.
