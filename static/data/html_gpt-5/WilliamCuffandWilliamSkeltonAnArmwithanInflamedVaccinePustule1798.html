William Cuff and William Skelton, An Arm with an Inflamed Vaccine Pustule (1798)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This print is associated with the British engraver William Skelton and the colorist or publisher William Cuff, working in the milieu of late eighteenth‑century medical illustration. Produced in the early years of vaccination, the image likely circulated alongside scientific treatises and pamphlets that followed Edward Jenner’s 1798 publication. The collaboration exemplifies the period’s division of labor in print culture—draftsman, engraver, and colorist—applied to medico‑scientific subjects with the aim of standardizing visual knowledge for clinicians and the public.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

In 1798, smallpox remained one of the deadliest infectious diseases. Variolation—deliberate exposure to smallpox—had been practiced, but with significant risk. Jenner’s vaccine, using cowpox (vaccinia) material, promised immunity with far lower danger and rapidly became a matter of urgent public interest and controversy. Britain’s print market, scientific societies, and charitable hospitals created a fertile ecosystem for disseminating images that documented typical vaccine reactions. Such images not only instructed practitioners performing arm‑to‑arm vaccination but also helped negotiate public trust at a moment when state, parish, and philanthropic actors debated compulsory measures and mass campaigns.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The composition isolates a single bent arm and focuses on the circular pustule with an erythematous halo. This target‑like form became an icon of successful “take,” a visible seal of acquired protection. The wrapped cloth near the shoulder evokes clinical procedure and cleanliness, while the absence of a face or identity abstracts the subject into a universalized patient. The restrained palette uses flesh tones and localized pink to signal inflammation without sensationalism, reinforcing the message that the lesion is controlled, expected, and ultimately protective.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Delicate stipple and cross‑hatching model the arm’s volume, while a vast field of negative space concentrates attention on the lesion. The gentle chiaroscuro and even lighting avoid dramatic effects in favor of empirical clarity—a hallmark of late Enlightenment scientific illustration. The curve of the arm creates a quiet, near‑classical arabesque that directs the eye to the umbilicated center. Tonal transitions are precise enough to communicate differential tissue states—normal skin, reactive erythema, and pustular center—blending aesthetics with diagnostic utility. The print exemplifies the atlas tradition that sought “objective” images to standardize medical observation.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Access to vaccine lymph and trained vaccinators was uneven across class and geography. Pauper children were often used as carriers in arm‑to‑arm chains, raising ethical concerns about consent and exploitation. Women’s labor as milkmaids supplied the empirical foundation for cowpox knowledge yet was seldom credited. Colonial administrations later used vaccination as both a public‑health tool and a technology of governance, with coercive campaigns disproportionately affecting colonized populations. Literacy barriers and mistrust also meant that misinformation clustered in disadvantaged communities, compounding vulnerability to outbreaks.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The artwork records a typical reaction to vaccination with vaccinia virus, a live orthopoxvirus immunologically related to variola (smallpox). Cross‑reactive antigens stimulate durable cellular and humoral immunity, conferring protection against smallpox. After inoculation, a papule appears around days 3–5, evolves to a tense vesicle by days 5–7, and to a pustule by days 8–10, usually with umbilication. Regional lymphadenopathy, low‑grade fever, and malaise may accompany the local lesion. By days 14–21 the pustule crusts and forms a scab; detachment leaves a characteristic scar, once a visible index of participation in collective immunity.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The central umbilicated pustule, surrounding erythematous flare, and subtle induration are rendered with clinical specificity. The neat, circular contour distinguishes vaccine lesions from traumatic ulcers, while the uniform halo suggests a robust local immune response rather than spreading cellulitis. The absence of streaking lymphangitis visually reassures; the print also omits suppurative complications, implicitly defining the depicted state as normal. Such semiotic choices helped physicians and lay viewers recognize a “good take” versus adverse reactions.

[h] Treatment

Contemporary guidance emphasized keeping the site clean and dry, avoiding lancing, and preventing autoinoculation. In the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, complications included accidental transmission of blood‑borne infections via arm‑to‑arm methods, later reduced by calf‑lymph production. Modern management for rare severe reactions involves vaccinia immune globulin (VIGIV) and, in specific scenarios, antivirals such as tecovirimat; for eradicated smallpox, care would be largely supportive. Current vaccines differ: replicating ACAM2000 produces a take and scar, whereas non‑replicating MVA‑BN (JYNNEOS/Imvanex) generally does not, reflecting evolving risk‑benefit profiles.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Overcrowding, poverty, and precarious labor heightened smallpox transmission before eradication and shaped who accessed vaccination early and safely. Supply chains for vaccine lymph depended on institutional capacity and politics, while trust hinged on respectful communication across class, gender, religion, and language. The print functions as health communication technology, aiming to standardize expectations and reduce fear. Its very legibility—plain background, single lesion—acknowledges that clear images can mitigate structural barriers to understanding and thereby influence uptake.
