Dalton Rashof Sores1858

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The work is a mid‑nineteenth‑century medical illustration, likely produced within a hospital or medical school atelier. Authorship is unknown; many such plates were created by anonymous illustrators working with dermatologists to document clinical appearances before routine photography. The caption “Dalton Rash of Sores 1858” suggests cataloging under a patient or case name and date rather than a signed artistic authorship. The drawing aligns with the British tradition of dermatologic plates associated with figures like Thomas Bateman, Erasmus Wilson, and Jonathan Hutchinson, where observational fidelity served pedagogical aims.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

In 1858, European and American medicine was consolidating specializations, including dermatology as a distinct field. Hospitals maintained “museums” of wax models, casebooks, and watercolor atlases to standardize diagnosis. Photography existed but struggled with tonal nuance for skin, making watercolor a preferred didactic medium. Public health was shaped by urbanization, industrial labor, and crowded housing, all of which intensified communicable skin diseases and venereal infections. Therapeutically, antisepsis was emergent, germ theory contested, and treatments for cutaneous and sexually transmitted conditions ranged from mercury and iodine to baths and isolation. The image embodies this transitional moment: empiric, taxonomic, and moralizing, yet increasingly clinical in tone.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The figure’s turned back and lack of facial identity signal the clinical gaze: the person becomes a bearer of lesions rather than a portrait subject. The neutral ground and draped lower body remove social markers, emphasizing morphology and distribution. The constellation of punctate red and brown macules and papules functions iconographically as a diagnostic map—each dot a datum. The downcast posture and exposed back can also be read as a subtle Victorian trope of vulnerability and modesty, balancing the need for visibility of pathology with decorum.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Executed in watercolor and graphite, the image relies on controlled wash to model the spine, scapulae, and paraspinal musculature, while stippling marks lesions with chromatic specificity. Limited palette—flesh tones, muted grays, and crimson accents—heightens contrast between healthy skin and eruption. The composition’s vertical orientation and central axis of the spine guide the viewer’s eye, while rhythmic repetition of lesions creates pattern and emphasis on the lower back. Edges are soft except at lesion sites, where sharper contours aid identification of papules and pustules. This marriage of anatomical verisimilitude and schematic clarity typifies nineteenth‑century diagnostic illustration.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Dermatologic atlases often drew from hospital charity patients, disproportionately representing the poor, migrants, sex workers, and soldiers. Stigma attached to visible skin disease, especially when suspected of venereal origin, shaped who was depicted and how. Gendered and classed assumptions influenced nosology—“moral” categories intersected with clinical ones. Access to effective care was uneven; those without means faced hazardous remedies, institutionalization, or public shaming. The image thus encodes structural inequities: whose bodies were made visible for teaching, and whose suffering furnished scientific knowledge.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The back displays numerous discrete erythematous to brownish papules and pustules, more concentrated in the lumbar and sacral regions, with scattered lesions along the thoracic spine and shoulders. Differential diagnoses in an 1858 context would include secondary syphilis (papulosquamous or papulopustular eruption), variola or varicella (if vesiculopustular and systemic), scabies with excoriated papules over the beltline and buttocks, impetiginized folliculitis, or prurigo. Secondary syphilis is plausible given the diffuse, relatively symmetrical distribution and “coppery” tonality noted in period texts; however, absence of palm/sole data and mucosal findings limits certainty. Follicular accentuation in the lower back might also indicate bacterial folliculitis exacerbated by friction and hygiene constraints. Etiologies therefore span Treponema pallidum infection, poxvirus infection (historically), Sarcoptes scabiei infestation, or Staphylococcus aureus in folliculitis.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Key signifiers include: discrete papules some with central crusting (suggesting pustulation or excoriation), relative sparing of midline except along spinous processes, and clustering over the sacrum. Color modulation from bright red to umber implies lesions at varied stages—fresh inflammation and resolving post‑inflammatory change. Lack of confluent plaques argues against psoriasis; absence of ringed borders reduces the likelihood of tinea corporis. The didactic intent is to fix morphology and distribution, the twin pillars of dermatologic diagnosis.

[h] Treatment

Historically (circa 1858), suspected syphilis would prompt mercurial inunctions or ingestion, sometimes combined with potassium iodide; outcomes varied and toxicity was common. Scabies was treated with sulfur ointments and thorough laundering. Folliculitis care centered on hygiene, antiseptic washes, and, later, topical antimicrobials. In contemporary evidence‑based practice, confirmed secondary syphilis is treated with intramuscular benzathine penicillin G; scabies with permethrin cream or oral ivermectin; bacterial folliculitis with targeted topical or systemic antibiotics and decolonization strategies. Supportive therapies—antipruritics, wound care, and vaccination (for poxviruses)—reflect advances unavailable to the image’s makers.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Crowded housing, limited sanitation, occupational sweat and friction, restricted access to clean clothing, and barriers to confidential sexual health services intensify both transmission and chronicity of skin disease. Stigma discourages presentation until lesions are advanced. Labor precarity and military service historically increased exposure and reduced convalescence time. Education, literacy, and clinic proximity shape adherence to complex regimens, while gendered norms influence who seeks and receives care. These determinants remain pertinent, refracting the image’s nineteenth‑century realities into present public health.
