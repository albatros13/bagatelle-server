William heals a blind woman York Minster

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
This stained-glass panel is attributed to the skilled glaziers working at York in the early fifteenth century, part of the accomplished workshops serving York Minster. While individual names are largely unrecorded, the glass reflects the refined practices of northern English Gothic glazing, closely related to the milieu that produced York’s celebrated windows. The panel’s confident figure drawing, saturated ruby grounds, and judicious use of silver stain align it with the top tier of English medieval glassmaking.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
The subject depicts Saint William of York, a twelfth-century archbishop whose cult grew after his death in 1154 and canonization in the thirteenth century. The window itself belongs to the early fifteenth century, a period when English cathedrals not only celebrated local saints but also competed as pilgrimage destinations. York Minster’s glazing campaigns communicated doctrine and civic identity to a largely illiterate public, and miracle cycles—such as a healing of blindness—validated the saint’s intercessory power and the Minster’s status as a sacred locus.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
Iconographically, the bishop’s mitre, crozier, and nimbus identify William’s ecclesiastical authority and sanctity. His right hand raised in benediction and the tactile contact with the supplicant’s head are standard hagiographic signs of healing. The kneeling woman, hands at her eyes, signifies visual affliction and humility. The radiant red ground functions as both a dramatic backdrop and a theological sign—charity and the fire of divine grace—visually encoding the miracle. The chromatic hierarchy (golden highlights on episcopal regalia, deep greens for the petitioner) creates spiritual and social distinction while directing the viewer’s gaze to the encounter.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
Formally, the composition employs hierarchical scale and clear silhouettes to read at distance. Pot-metal ruby and green pieces are unified by strong lead cames that double as contour drawing. Silver stain produces warm yellows on the crozier and mitre, a technique perfected in fourteenth–fifteenth-century England. Vitreous paint delivers facial modeling and drapery folds, with linear hatching to give volume. The red field provides high chromatic contrast, while the oblique “rays” structure space and suggest emanating grace, achieving both narrative clarity and devotional intensity.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
Medieval miracle imagery frequently stages poor, disabled, and especially female petitioners as recipients of healing. Such scenes register real inequities: limited access to learned medicine, gendered constraints on mobility and resources, and the precariousness of laborers whose livelihoods depended on unimpaired vision. Pilgrimage offered an alternative pathway to care—spiritual and social—though it also underscored dependence on charitable networks and the Church’s authority over bodies and cures.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
“Blindness” in medieval narratives encompassed several conditions: corneal scarring from trachoma or infectious keratitis; cataract (then understood as an “humor” descending, now recognized as lens opacity); vitamin A deficiency causing xerophthalmia; advanced glaucoma; smallpox scars; and trauma. In northern Europe, chronic conjunctival infections, smoke exposure in domestic interiors, malnutrition, and untreated ocular injuries were prevalent etiologies. Without modern diagnostics, symptoms such as photophobia, corneal opacity, or milky pupils were grouped under a broad category of sight loss.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
Artists rarely distinguished precise ophthalmic disease. Instead, codified signs—hands shielding the eyes, downcast face, a guide figure, or schematic white dots for pupils—signal visual impairment. Here, the woman’s gesture toward her eyes and her kneeling posture indicate both disability and supplication. The lack of anatomical specificity reflects the panel’s didactic aim: to testify to sanctity rather than render clinical taxonomy.

[h] Treatment
Contemporary to the window, cataract “couching” (displacing the opaque lens with a needle) was known from classical and Arabic medicine and described by medieval surgeons. Outcomes were variable and often complicated by infection. Herbal salves, cautery, and regimen-based therapies addressed conjunctival disease with limited efficacy. Miraculous healing, as depicted, offered a spiritually grounded alternative. By contrast, modern evidence-based treatments include small-incision cataract extraction with intraocular lens implantation, antibiotics for trachoma with SAFE strategy (Surgery, Antibiotics, Facial cleanliness, Environmental improvement), pressure-lowering therapy for glaucoma, and vitamin A supplementation for deficiency-related blindness.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
The image tacitly indexes determinants still relevant today: poverty limiting diet diversity (vitamin A), inadequate sanitation facilitating trachoma transmission, indoor smoke exacerbating ocular surface disease, occupational hazards causing trauma, and gendered disparities in access to care. Pilgrimage economies could redistribute alms and attention, yet structural risks remained for those outside elite networks.
