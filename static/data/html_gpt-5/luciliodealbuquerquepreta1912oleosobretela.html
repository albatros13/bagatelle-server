Preta (1912) — Oil on canvas

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Lucílio de Albuquerque (1877–1939) was a Brazilian painter trained at the Escola Nacional de Belas Artes in Rio de Janeiro and later in Paris, where he absorbed academic classicism alongside plein-air and impressionist currents. Returning to Brazil, he taught at the ENBA and became a key mediator between late academic conventions and the pictorial modernizations of the 1910s–1920s. His practice often dignified everyday subjects while preserving careful drawing, tonal modeling, and pedagogical clarity—traits visible in this intimate scene popularly identified as Preta (1912), a work that foregrounds Black motherhood and labor within Brazil’s urban domestic sphere.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Painted in 1912, the work belongs to Brazil’s First Republic (1889–1930), a period marked by the afterlives of slavery (abolished in 1888), accelerated urbanization, and stark racialized inequality. Public health reforms spearheaded by figures linked to the Oswaldo Cruz Institute coexisted with precarious housing, poor sanitation, and high infant mortality, particularly among Afro-Brazilian communities. Domestic service and wet-nursing—long entangled with slavery—remained common forms of employment for Black women, now recast as poorly regulated wage labor. Artistic institutions favored French-derived academism, yet a growing interest in social themes paralleled emergent debates about hygiene, maternity, and national identity.

[c] Symbolism and/or iconography

The composition centers a seated mother nursing one child while another lies nearby. The bare architecture, worn floor, and simple garments stage poverty and the vulnerability of domestic interiors. The iconography of breastfeeding evokes charity and sustenance but, in Brazil, also indexes the history of the ama de leite (wet nurse): Black women hired to breastfeed elite infants, often while their own children received less attention. The racialized hierarchy of care, suggested by the juxtaposition of infants and the mother’s introspective gaze, transforms a maternal motif into a social allegory about nourishment, dependence, and sacrifice embedded in class and color lines.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Warm earth tones, balanced by the cool plastered wall, create a restrained palette that emphasizes flesh and fabric. Loose but purposeful brushwork softens contours, producing atmospheric unity while preserving anatomical credibility. A triangular arrangement—mother, nursing child, and recumbent infant—stabilizes the design and draws attention to hands and breast as the narrative fulcrum. Light skims across skin and cloth, modeling volumes without theatrical contrasts, aligning the painting with academic naturalism tempered by impressionist luminosity. The spare setting, compressed perspective, and low vantage intensify empathy, as if the viewer shares the confined domestic space of the sitter.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The image implicates systems that conscript Black women’s bodies into reproductive and nutritional labor. Post-abolition legal freedom did not dismantle economic compulsion or racial stigma; rather, domestic work and informal caregiving persisted with meager pay, limited medical oversight, and exposure to infectious disease. The health of wet nurses, their infants, and the infants they fed depended on unequal access to sanitation, clean water, and clinical care. The painting’s quiet dignity contrasts with the structural violence of inadequate housing, precarious employment, and state policies that moralized motherhood while underfunding maternal-child health.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Breastfeeding is protective against diarrhea, respiratory infections, and malnutrition, yet the conditions surrounding it can generate risk. In early 20th-century Brazil, endemic hookworm contributed to iron-deficiency anemia in adults, including lactating women, through chronic intestinal blood loss. Crowded dwellings and poor ventilation increased tuberculosis transmission. Peripartum infections, mastitis, and cracked nipples could complicate lactation, and untreated syphilis risked congenital infection or transmission via breast lesions. For infants, abrupt weaning or mixed feeding with contaminated water and utensils led to enteric disease and high mortality. The canvas invites consideration of these intertwined biological and environmental etiologies.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

While no clinical diagnosis can be inferred, the artist cues fatigue and possible undernourishment through downcast eyes, slumped posture, and attenuated limbs. The austere interior and minimal bedding imply exposure to cold floors and pathogens. The infants’ recumbency and thin swaddling recall vulnerability to dehydration and respiratory infection. These visual signifiers function not as medical records but as rhetorical prompts, directing attention to maternal strain and infant fragility shaped by poverty.

[h] Treatment

Historically, public health responses emphasized breastfeeding promotion, milk inspection, and hygienic instruction. Early anti-hookworm campaigns deployed anthelmintics like thymol; today, albendazole or mebendazole, sanitation, and footwear are standard. Mastitis management centers on continued emptying of the breast, analgesia, and antibiotics when bacterial infection is suspected; fissures require latch correction and topical care. Screening and treatment for syphilis and tuberculosis reduce maternal-infant transmission. Nutritional support—iron and folate, balanced diet, and, when needed, supplementation—improves maternal reserves critical for successful lactation.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Income, racial discrimination, housing quality, education, and access to clean water and primary care shape risks more than individual behavior. Labor precarity and lack of maternity protections constrain breastfeeding duration and care-seeking. Community sanitation, vaccination coverage, and women’s legal rights correlate with lower infant mortality. The painting’s domestic stillness thus encodes a wider epidemiology of inequality, where structural conditions decide who thrives.
