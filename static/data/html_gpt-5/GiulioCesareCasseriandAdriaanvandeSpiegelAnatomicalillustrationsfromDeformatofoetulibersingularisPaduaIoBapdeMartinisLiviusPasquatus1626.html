Giulio Cesare Casseri and Adriaan van de Spiegel, De formato foetu liber singularis (Padua, 1626)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The image reflects the collaboration between the anatomist Adriaan van den Spiegel (Spigelius), professor at Padua, and the Paduan anatomist-draughtsman Giulio Cesare Casseri (Casserius). The plates in this tradition were engraved by the Venetian artist Odoardo Fialetti after anatomical drawings associated with Casseri, demonstrating the close interdependence of artisanal print culture and university anatomy in early seventeenth-century Italy.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Produced in 1626 in Padua, a leading center for anatomical teaching, the work belongs to the post-Vesalian period when printed atlases standardized human anatomy for physicians and surgeons. Public dissections, civic patronage, and sophisticated presses enabled such hybrid images to circulate across Europe. Obstetrics remained largely in the hands of midwives, but university anatomists increasingly claimed authority over reproduction, theorizing fetal development from classical sources, dissection of cadavers (including the pregnant), and animal analogies.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The standing female figure, poised in contrapposto within a landscape, echoes the “anatomical figure” conventions of Vesalius and Casseri: a living body theatrically reveals its interior. The uterus is opened like a blooming flower surrounding a curled fetus, merging botanical metaphor with obstetric description. Such floral iconography equated fecundity with natural growth, while labeled letters guide scholarly reading. The gesture and idealized nudity align the body with classical virtue, legitimizing exposure as knowledge rather than indecency.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Fialetti’s engraving combines crisp line, tonal hatching, and emphatic contours to produce legibility and elegance. Spatial recession of the landscape sets a serene backdrop, heightening the didactic centrality of the womb. The composition uses balance and rhythm—curving torso, circular placenta, and looping cord—to lead the eye from figure to fetal form. The hybrid of emblematic allegory and analytic diagram exemplifies early modern strategies for communicating complex anatomy before color lithography and photography.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The authority to represent pregnancy shifted from women’s experiential knowledge to male, university-sanctioned expertise. Cadavers often came from the poor, executed, or unclaimed, revealing structural inequities in whose bodies were opened for science. Literacy and book cost restricted access to knowledge, while midwives—though essential—were frequently excluded from academic credit. Maternal mortality disproportionately affected lower-status women lacking attendants, clean environments, or timely intervention.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

While the plate shows a normative in-utero fetus, it speaks to obstetric dangers: malpresentation (breech, transverse), obstructed labor from cephalopelvic disproportion, placenta previa or abruption causing hemorrhage, and postpartum infection (puerperal fever). Early modern etiologies drew on humoral imbalance and maternal impressions; today we understand mechanical obstruction, abnormal placental implantation, hypertensive disorders, and microbial causes (notably Streptococcus pyogenes and mixed flora) as key drivers of morbidity.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

An image like this trained viewers to locate the fetus, membranes, and placenta—anatomical landmarks crucial for diagnosing problems. Malpresentation is inferred by fetal orientation relative to the pelvic outlet; placenta previa is suspected when placental tissue overlies the cervical os; umbilical cord issues (true knots or nuchal loops) risk hypoxia. Although the engraving is idealized, its labeled structures underpinned clinical reasoning that connected symptoms—labor arrest, hemorrhage, fetal distress—to internal arrangements.

[h] Treatment

Seventeenth-century management emphasized manual skills: external and internal version to convert a transverse or breech to cephalic delivery; embryotomy in catastrophic obstructed labor to save the mother; and compresses or cautery for hemorrhage. The obstetric forceps, guarded as a family secret by the Chamberlens, were not widely disseminated until later. Cesarean section on living mothers carried extreme mortality before antisepsis and transfusion. Modern care—prenatal screening, safe cesarean, uterotonics for postpartum hemorrhage, antibiotics, and antiseptic technique after Semmelweis—dramatically reduces risk, yet equitable access remains decisive.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Outcomes in pregnancy hinge on nutrition, chronic disease burden, age, parity, sanitation, and access to skilled attendants. Legal and cultural norms governing sexual behavior and marriage shaped care-seeking and stigma. Geography and poverty limit transport and referral in emergencies; educational disparities affect recognition of warning signs. Structural racism and gendered power imbalances continue to produce uneven maternal mortality, despite technical advances unknown to early modern anatomists.
