PE html> Santi Buglioni — Seven Works of Mercy (detail: Charity)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Santi di Michele Buglioni (1494–1576) was a Florentine sculptor working in the lineage of glazed terracotta perfected by the Della Robbia and practiced by the Buglioni family workshop. Trained by his uncle Benedetto Buglioni, Santi translated devotional narratives into durable, brilliantly colored reliefs suited to public façades. The present detail from a Seven Works of Mercy cycle exemplifies his capacity to merge liturgical instruction with observant description of charitable healthcare, an essential civic mission in Renaissance Tuscany.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Executed around 1525 for a hospital setting in Tuscany, the relief occupied a façade frieze that taught lay viewers the duties of Christian charity. Early sixteenth-century hospitals in central Italy—such as the Ospedale del Ceppo in Pistoia—were hybrid spaces of medical care, hospitality for travelers, and spiritual consolation. Confraternities administered endowments; physicians, surgeons, and religious personnel collaborated within a regimen emphasizing diet, order, and prayer. This was also the generation that confronted syphilis (“the great pox”), recurrent plague waves, and endemic poverty. The relief’s public placement aligned with humanist civic ideals: art functioned as moral pedagogy, while advertising the institution’s competence and piety.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The episode shows a friar washing a traveler’s foot: a tangible image of Charity among the corporal works of mercy. The staff, travel-worn garments, and the seated pose mark the figure as a pilgrim or wayfarer, a canonical proxy for the socially vulnerable. The basin and poured water signify cleansing and care; they also allude to biblical foot-washing as humility and service. Halos indicate sanctified exemplars who model virtuous conduct. By coupling tools of hygiene (pitcher, bowl, towel) with compassionate gestures, the iconography binds salvation history to routine hospital practice, translating theology into recognizable protocols of bedside care.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Buglioni employs tin-glazed terracotta to achieve a luminous palette—cobalt blue grounds, creamy whites, and earth-tone flesh—whose gloss enhances legibility outdoors. Figures are modeled in moderately high relief, creating rhythmic overlaps that guide the eye from the pitcher to the basin to the patient’s foot. Drapery carving crisply articulates volume and movement; the diagonal of the bent leg anchors the composition. Color serves narrativity: white garments mark caregivers, while green and violet accents differentiate roles within the group. The glazed surface, both protective and reflective, fuses medium and message: mercy is presented as durable, radiant, and public.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Hospitals primarily served the poor, pilgrims, and the sick without kin. Access to specialized treatment depended on civic status, gender, and patronage; contagious patients could be diverted to separate lazaretti. The relief naturalizes an asymmetry—the kneeling caregiver and seated beneficiary—that mirrors contemporary hierarchies of authority, yet it also dignifies the recipient by placing him at the compositional center. By monumentalizing charity on the façade, institutions sought donations and legitimation, even as structural drivers of illness—poverty, housing precarity, and hazardous labor—remained largely unaddressed.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The scene plausibly depicts lower-limb ailments common among travelers: maceration and blistering progressing to cellulitis; tinea pedis leading to fissures and secondary infection; traumatic wounds contaminated by soil; and ulcers exacerbated by malnutrition. Before germ theory, etiologies were framed by humoral imbalance and environmental “miasma,” yet practical experience recognized the role of contaminated surfaces and unclean wounds. Modern microbiology would identify likely pathogens as beta-hemolytic streptococci and Staphylococcus aureus entering through compromised skin; chronicity could be fueled by peripheral vascular insufficiency or neuropathy, though these would not have been clinically named at the time.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Iconographically, the elevated leg, the caregiver’s palpation at the ankle, and the ready basin imply swelling, tenderness, and the need for irrigation—visual proxies for edema, erythema, or ulceration. The staff doubles as a pilgrim’s attribute and an assistive device, signaling impaired ambulation. Facial expressions—downcast lids, parted lips—suggest pain and fatigue. The white cloth at the limb may denote a dressing, anticipating modern bandaging.

[h] Treatment

Depicted care aligns with period protocols: bathing the feet in warm water, wine, or vinegar; gentle debridement; application of herbal poultices (plantain, myrrh, rosemary); and clean linen dressings. Surgeons lanced abscesses and cauterized when necessary; diet and rest were part of hospital regimen. Isolation for suspected contagion was practiced unevenly but theorized later by Fracastoro. Though antisepsis lay centuries ahead, wine, honey, and certain resins offered modest antimicrobial effects. The relief shows the crucial therapeutic triad of cleanliness, touch, and social support that improved outcomes even in the absence of modern antibiotics.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Long-distance travel, poverty, poor footwear, exposure to cold and damp, and limited access to clean water increased risk for skin and soft-tissue infections. Nutritional deficits impaired wound healing; overcrowded lodgings facilitated transmission of parasites and contagious disease. The hospital’s charitable infrastructure mitigated some risks—providing shelter, food, and supervised hygiene—yet the work visualized here underscores that health was inseparable from mobility, class, and urban governance.
