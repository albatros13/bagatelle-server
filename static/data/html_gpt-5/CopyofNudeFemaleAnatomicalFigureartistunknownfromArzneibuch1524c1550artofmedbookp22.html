Caption: Copy of Nude Female Anatomical Figure artist unknown from Arzneibuch (1524–1550, p. 22)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
The maker is unidentified, likely a workshop-trained scribe-illustrator linked to German-speaking medical compilers who produced vernacular remedy books (Arzneibücher). Such artisans mediated between university medicine and lay practice, adapting learned schemata into accessible images that could circulate among apothecaries, barber-surgeons, and household healers.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
The image belongs to the early modern threshold, roughly 1524–1550, a period marked by the printing revolution and the Reformation’s push toward vernacular literacy. While Vesalius’s anatomical reforms (1543) began to reorient medicine toward dissection-based knowledge, much everyday healing in Central Europe still relied on Galenic and Arabic authorities refracted through recipe books. Gendered divisions of labor—midwives for childbirth, surgeons for procedures, physicians for theory—structured who used such manuals and how.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
Radiating red lines connect handwritten terms to bodily zones, mapping humors (sanguis, cholera, melancholia, phlegma) and ailments to a female figure. The opened abdomen stylizes viscera and reproductive organs, emblematic of the period’s special attention to womb-centered etiologies. The diagram doubles as a memoria aid: color, line, and Latinized vernacular labels form a “pathway” for diagnosis and regimen. Without strict anatomical fidelity, the image indexes a cosmological body whose parts are governed by qualities (hot/cold, wet/dry) and the balance thereof.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
The figure is presented frontally with strong contour and minimal modeling, prioritizing legibility over realism. Ink lines and rubrication create emphasis and visual hierarchy, while the figure’s vertical axis stabilizes the page. Proportion is intentionally schematic; organs are diagrammatic emblems rather than observed structures. Handwritten script integrates text as image, an infographic strategy that fuses calligraphy with medical mapping typical of manuscript-to-print transitional culture.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
The image reflects asymmetries in whose bodies were studied and how. Women’s health is framed through fertility and menstrual regulation, while women’s experiential knowledge (midwifery) was often subordinated to male-authored theory. Access to such books skewed by class and literacy; elite households and guild practitioners held informational advantages. Moreover, humoral essentialism naturalized gendered stereotypes (e.g., “moist” female temperament), shaping care pathways and sometimes justifying intrusive controls on women’s bodies.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
The etiological logic is humoral. Disease arises when the four humors lose equilibrium through diet, season, emotions, or life stage. For female patients, etiologies frequently centered on menstrual irregularities, “retention of menses,” or uterine disorders such as the much-discussed “suffocation of the mother.” Environmental miasmas, cosmic influences, and life regimen were considered causal, while internal putrefaction explained fevers and abscesses. Contagion was recognized in practice but not framed by germ theory.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
The labels suggest symptom clusters mapped to locales: headache and melancholy to the head; sanguine disorders to vessels; colic and flux to the abdomen; and uterine pathologies to the pelvis. Visual emphasis on the belly indicates diagnostic importance of digestion, menstruation, and pregnancy. Practitioners read complexion, pulse, urine, and menstrual flow alongside this cartography, translating visible signs into humoral imbalances to be corrected.

[h] Treatment
Recommended care combined regimen (diet, sleep, exercise), evacuation (phlebotomy, cupping, purging, emetics), and pharmacology from herbal materia medica cataloged in Arzneibücher. For menstrual obstruction, emmenagogues such as rue, pennyroyal, or savin were typical; for heat disorders, cooling syrups and barley waters; for “thick” phlegm, warming aromatics. Obstetrical guidance included positioning, lubricants, and postpartum management. Surgery was limited and usually the purview of barber-surgeons. All of this predated antisepsis and anesthesia, affecting risk and outcomes.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Diet tied to harvest cycles, crowded housing, seasonal labor, and smoke-filled interiors shaped respiratory and gastrointestinal disease. Women’s reproductive burdens—early marriage, repeated pregnancies, and constrained mobility—intersected with limited property rights and medical access. Literacy gaps and linguistic barriers (Latin vs. vernacular) influenced who could consult books like this and interpret their recommendations. Religious and civic regulations modulated midwives’ authority, further mediating care quality and timeliness.
