Zhou Pei Qun — A Patient Receiving Manipulating Treatment for a Pain in the Shoulder (1890)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
Zhou Pei Qun (active late 19th century) was a Chinese painter working in the late Qing period. His careful line work and flat, luminous colors evoke the gongbi tradition used in manuals, medical albums, and courtly or urban workshops that produced didactic images. The present sheet, likely part of a series on therapeutic techniques, depicts a clinician delivering manual manipulation to a seated patient—an image that bridges pictorial art and medical pedagogy.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
Dated to 1890, the work belongs to a moment when Qing China confronted modernity and medical pluralism. Western biomedicine was entering treaty-port hospitals while long-established practices—acupuncture, moxibustion, die-da bone-setting, and tuina massage—remained central to everyday care. Illustrated albums circulated among literate artisans and practitioners, functioning as guides for technique and as advertisements of skill. The sparse setting, portable stools, and domestic interior suggest vernacular, street-side practice rather than elite court medicine, reflecting how manual therapies served an urban populace of merchants, porters, and craftsmen whose livelihoods depended on functional limbs.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
The scene is economical: two stools, a discarded shoe, and an inscription that instructs or contextualizes the maneuver. The shoe and relaxed robe index the patient’s surrender to treatment and trust in the healer. The therapist’s elevated seat and firm but gentle hand symbolize guided alignment; the patient’s abducted arm and tilted head signal submission to corrective force. In Chinese medical iconography, such gestural vocabularies evoke the restoration of qi and xue (blood) flow through opening of joints and sinew channels.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
Zhou’s gongbi outlines define volume with restrained contour, while mineral pigments create cool tonal harmonies of blue and grey against expansive negative space. The asymmetrical composition concentrates action in the lower left, counterbalanced by the vertical column of calligraphy, a classic text-image pairing in Qing albums. Rhythm emerges from the repeated diagonals of limbs and benches, guiding the eye through the arc of manipulation. Emphasis falls on the contact point at the shoulder, where line density thickens slightly, translating bodily pressure into pictorial weight.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
The image hints at inequities in late Qing urban life. Manual therapy was accessible and relatively inexpensive, serving laborers whose musculoskeletal pain arose from repetitive work and limited rest. Women and poorer patients had constrained access to formal clinics; itinerant bone-setters filled gaps in care but operated without standardized credentials, leaving outcomes dependent on reputation and apprenticeship lineages. Colonial-era public health structures privileged Western hospitals, marginalizing traditional practitioners even as communities continued to rely on them.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
The subject is shoulder pain, which in contemporary terms might include rotator cuff tendinopathy, adhesive capsulitis (“frozen shoulder”), glenohumeral instability, or cervical radiculopathy with referred pain. Overhead labor, cold exposure, and immobilization are risk factors. In traditional Chinese medicine (TCM), such pain often falls under “bi syndrome,” arising from wind-cold-damp invasion obstructing the jingluo (channels) or from qi and blood stasis after strain or contusion. The therapeutic aim is to free obstruction, warm the channels, and restore range of motion.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
Visually, the patient exhibits limited abduction and compensatory trunk lean, suggesting capsular tightness or subacromial irritation. The therapist’s stabilizing hand at the scapula indicates attention to scapulothoracic mechanics, essential for normal humeral elevation. In TCM semiology, localized tenderness at Jianyu (LI15) or Jianliao (SJ14) and painful arcs during movement would confirm a pattern of stagnation along the Large Intestine and Sanjiao channels traversing the shoulder.

[h] Treatment
The maneuver resembles graded glenohumeral mobilization with passive abduction and external rotation—parallels to modern Maitland/Kaltenborn techniques for capsular restriction. In TCM practice it corresponds to tuina methods: rolling, kneading, and traction, followed by oscillatory mobilizations and moist-heat or herbal liniments (die-da jiu) to promote circulation. Contemporary evidence supports manual therapy combined with therapeutic exercise for adhesive capsulitis and rotator cuff–related pain; manipulation alone offers short-term relief but gains are optimized when paired with stretching, scapular strengthening, and patient education. Safety requires avoiding high-velocity thrusts in suspected fractures or acute inflammation.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Recurrent shoulder pain is shaped by occupation (load-bearing work), housing and heating (cold-damp exposure), nutrition, and the ability to rest or modify tasks. Payment models—fee-for-service at the point of care—can delay presentation until disability threatens income. Cultural familiarity with tuina and bone-setting may encourage early, low-cost care but can also limit referral to imaging or surgery when indicated. Community networks and apprenticeship systems historically mediated trust, outcomes, and dissemination of technique.
