Samuel George Morton Differences Skulls (1839)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Samuel George Morton (1799–1851) was a Philadelphia physician and naturalist whose collection of human crania became the empirical foundation for nineteenth‑century American ethnology. Trained in anatomy and committed to measurement, Morton sought to classify humankind through cranial capacity and contour. His published plates—of which this diagram is an example—were presented as neutral science but now stand as artifacts of “scientific racism.” The image functions both as medical illustration and as ideological instrument, translating human diversity into rigid “types.”

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Produced in 1839, at the height of U.S. chattel slavery and global imperial expansion, the diagram belongs to an era when polygenism (the belief that human “races” had separate origins) circulated among some American and European intellectuals. The young disciplines of physical anthropology and comparative anatomy were consolidating methods: calipers, cranial indices, and anatomical atlases. Morton's Crania Americana supplied visuals that traveled through lecture halls, museums, and pro‑slavery pamphlets, lending quantitative sheen to political projects. The lithographic clarity and serial reproducibility of the print amplified its authority, while the acquisition of skulls—often taken from Indigenous graves and the bodies of enslaved people—revealed the violent infrastructures of knowledge production.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The three plan views (norma verticalis) depict skull outlines aligned on a shared baseline, a visual rhetoric that implies objective comparability. Labels in the original publication employ racialized terms now recognized as harmful; their typological sequencing performs a hierarchy. Measured arcs and emphasized zygomatic breadths act as icons of supposed characterological difference, encoding moral and intellectual claims into bone. The spareness of line, absence of flesh, and exclusion of individual variation together symbolize a fantasy of pure anatomical essence. The drawing transforms bodies into data while masking the social choices that made the comparison possible.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Formally, the work relies on contour line, bilateral symmetry, and repetition. Scale is held constant and the heads are cropped to the cranial vault, creating a strict graphic field. Rhythm emerges through the trio’s left‑to‑right progression, while balance is achieved by the equal spacing and identical vantage point. The design claims neutrality—thin line, minimal shading—but that economy is rhetorical: uniform presentation encourages the viewer to read tiny deviations as decisive. The seriality anticipates later museum typologies and medical atlases, using the design language of measurement to confer objectivity.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

This image participated in creating and legitimizing racial hierarchy. Morton's measurements were mobilized to rationalize slavery, colonial dispossession, and immigration restriction. The skulls themselves were unequally sourced: Indigenous remains were looted; bodies of enslaved and poor people were disproportionately used without consent. In medicine, such “race science” seeded durable myths of biological ranking that echo in contemporary “race correction” formulas and clinical bias. Art historically, the plate exemplifies how visual systems of classification—charts, grids, collections—can aestheticize inequality. Ethical repair today includes repatriation of remains, contextualization of collections, and rejection of racial typologies in clinical practice.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Although Morton's plate depicts putatively “normal” skull types, modern biomedicine recognizes that head shape varies widely and can be altered by disease. A key condition is craniosynostosis, the premature fusion of one or more cranial sutures. Etiology can be syndromic (mutations in FGFR2, FGFR3, TWIST1 and others) or nonsyndromic, with multifactorial genetic and environmental contributions. Nutritional disease such as rickets (vitamin D and/or calcium deficiency) can also remodel the cranium, producing craniotabes and frontal bossing. Historically, such pathologies could be misread as racial traits within typological frameworks, illustrating how biased sampling confounded interpretation.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Craniosynostosis yields characteristic morphologies: sagittal synostosis produces scaphocephaly (elongated, narrow skull); coronal synostosis can cause brachycephaly or anterior plagiocephaly with frontal flattening; metopic fusion may create trigonocephaly with a keel‑shaped forehead. Rickets may present with delayed fontanelle closure, parietal thinning, and frontal bossing. None of these shapes signal intellect or character; they are biomechanical consequences of suture dynamics and mineral metabolism. Recognizing such clinical signifiers helps disentangle disease from the typological readings promoted by plates like Morton’s.

[h] Treatment

Contemporary management of craniosynostosis includes early referral, CT or 3‑D imaging for suture mapping, and surgery—either open cranial vault remodeling or endoscopic suturectomy followed by orthotic helmet therapy—to restore cranial volume and prevent elevated intracranial pressure. Rickets is treated with vitamin D and calcium repletion plus correction of underlying malabsorption or renal disorders; prevention relies on supplementation, sunlight safety, and food fortification. These evidence‑based approaches replace the moralizing narratives once attached to skull form.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Risk and outcomes are shaped by nutrition, housing, latitude and sun exposure, access to prenatal and pediatric care, and structural racism that limits diagnostic and surgical services. Historical disinvestment in communities of color has produced delayed referrals and under‑treatment for craniofacial disorders. Ethical medical practice requires addressing these determinants while rejecting race as a biological proxy and centering individual genetic, developmental, and environmental factors.
