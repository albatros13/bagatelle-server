Andreas Vesalius De Humani Corporis Fabrica Libri Septem Basel Johannes Oporinus (1555)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
The plate is associated with the physician-anatomist Andreas Vesalius, whose De Humani Corporis Fabrica transformed European anatomy. While Vesalius authored the text and directed the iconographic program, the images were produced in collaboration with skilled cutters and a Venice–Basel network of designers, often attributed to Jan Stephan van Calcar, a pupil of Titian. The printer Johannes Oporinus in Basel oversaw the sophisticated woodcut production that allowed Vesalius to integrate image, sequence, and typography into a didactic whole.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
Published in 1555 as the second edition of the Fabrica, the work belongs to the High Renaissance and the age of print humanism. Universities such as Padua promoted empirical dissection that challenged medieval reliance on Galenic dogma. Basel’s print industry enabled large-format technical images to circulate across Europe, standardizing anatomical knowledge for physicians, barber-surgeons, and artists. Public dissections, secular patronage, and a growing market for scientific books intersected with confessional upheavals and civic regulation of bodies, including the use of executed criminals for anatomical study.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
This “muscleman” (écoresché) stands in contrapposto within an idealized landscape, echoing antique statuary and Renaissance rhetoric of revival. The figure’s demonstrative gesture stages anatomy as a public performance; the open countryside signifies the liberation of knowledge from cloistered scholasticism. The flayed skin, sometimes draped or held, functions as memento mori and as a visual tool to reveal the body’s inner order. Sequential plates guide the viewer from superficial to deep layers, encoding a narrative of inquiry and revelation.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
Rendered in fine woodcut, the plate uses cross‑hatching and parallel lines to model volume, with crisp contours locating muscle origins and insertions. Proportion and balance derive from classical canons, while directional line and torsion create movement through the torso and limbs. The landscape establishes depth via aerial perspective, contrasting organic terrain with the engineered “architecture” of musculature. The integration of Latin headings, folio numbers, and figure letters exemplifies Renaissance page design in which image, text, and didactic sequence operate as a single informational object.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
The progress the image celebrates rested on constrained bodies and unequal access. Cadavers were typically drawn from executed criminals, the poor, or marginalized groups who could not refuse post‑mortem use. Women’s bodies were underrepresented, limiting knowledge of female anatomy and health. Latin literacy and the cost of folio volumes restricted readership, while guild and university structures excluded many artisans and midwives from formal anatomical learning.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
Although the plate depicts normative musculature, it illuminates the anatomy underlying rotator cuff disease, a common source of shoulder pain. The supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis form a tendinous cuff stabilizing the glenohumeral joint visible at the figure’s shoulder. Etiology is multifactorial: intrinsic tendon degeneration with age and poor vascularity; extrinsic factors such as subacromial impingement; repetitive overhead activity; acute trauma; and systemic risks including smoking, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia. Microtears provoke inflammation and fibrocartilaginous change, which can progress to partial‑ or full‑thickness tears and secondary stiffness.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
Clinically, patients report lateral shoulder pain exacerbated by abduction and night lying on the affected side. On examination, painful arc between 60–120 degrees, weakness in abduction (supraspinatus) or external rotation (infraspinatus/teres minor), and positive Neer or Hawkins–Kennedy impingement signs are typical. Palpation corresponds to the tendon insertions on the greater and lesser tubercles represented in the plate’s shoulder complex. Ultrasound and MRI demonstrate tendon thickening, bursal fluid, or discontinuity; plain radiographs may show acromial morphology and calcific tendinopathy.

[h] Treatment
Initial care emphasizes activity modification, NSAIDs, and structured physical therapy targeting scapular stabilization and eccentric rotator‑cuff strengthening. Subacromial corticosteroid injections can provide short‑term relief to facilitate rehab. For persistent pain with functional limitation, imaging‑confirmed full‑thickness tears in active patients may benefit from arthroscopic repair; evidence suggests earlier repair yields better tendon quality, while routine acromioplasty is not universally indicated. For degenerative partial tears, nonoperative management often achieves comparable outcomes at one year. Postoperative protocols progress from immobilization to passive, then active‑assisted, and finally resisted motion, balancing tendon healing with prevention of stiffness.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Occupations requiring repetitive overhead work (construction, warehouse labor) and limited access to physiotherapy increase risk and worsen outcomes. Older adults, uninsured patients, and those with comorbid diabetes or smoking exposure face delayed diagnosis, reduced rehab adherence, and higher retear rates. Workplace protections, paid leave, and community physical‑therapy access are key modifiers.
