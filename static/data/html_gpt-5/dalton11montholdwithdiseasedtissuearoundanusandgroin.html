Dalton, 11-month-old with diseased tissue around anus and groin

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The work is attributable to an anonymous hospital medical artist working within the Euro‑American tradition of clinical watercolour illustration. Such artists functioned as intermediaries between surgeon, patient, and page, synthesizing careful observation with didactic clarity. Their training overlapped with that of topographical and botanical draughtsmen: accuracy, muted palettes, and economy of line were prized to convey pathology without unnecessary narrative embellishment.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

This image likely belongs to the long nineteenth century, when clinical casebooks and atlases relied on watercolour before routine hospital photography. Urbanization, crowding, and infant malnutrition produced high burdens of infectious disease. Charitable hospitals and teaching institutions commissioned images like this to standardize knowledge across wards and to instruct students. The focus on an infant body also reflects period anxieties about public health, sanitation, and the emergent specialty of pediatrics.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The sheet is resolutely anti‑allegorical. Its iconography is indexical: cropped anatomy, neutral ground, and chromatic emphasis on inflamed tissue direct the viewer’s gaze to lesions as evidence. The anonymity of the child and absence of contextual props create what art historians call the clinical gaze, turning the body into a bearer of signs. The composition’s tight cropping de‑sexualizes the subject and frames the image as documentation rather than genre scene.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Form is modeled with pale washes and spare graphite contour, while saturated reds and violets articulate ulceration and granulation. Light originates from the upper left, producing soft shadows that gently model infant fat pads and emphasize protruding lesions. The asymmetrical placement and oblique foreshortening give diagnostic access to perianal and inguinal surfaces simultaneously. Line economy, restrained palette, and matte paper surface enhance legibility—key criteria for teaching images.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Images like this are artifacts of unequal health landscapes. Infants from poor households were disproportionately exposed to contaminated water, inadequate diapering materials, and limited maternal leave, all of which predisposed to dermatologic and enteric disease. Access to early prenatal screening for maternal infections—now routine in many settings—was historically absent. The picture thus documents pathology but also the structural conditions (poverty, overcrowding, stigma) that make such pathology common and severe.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The painting depicts ulcerated, exophytic lesions around the anus and adjacent groin folds, with moist, lobulated tissue suggestive of hypertrophic granulation. In an infant, a cautious differential diagnosis would include: (1) condyloma lata of congenital syphilis (Treponema pallidum), which can present as broad, flat, highly infectious perianal papules; (2) severe irritant diaper dermatitis with secondary Candida albicans and/or bacterial superinfection; (3) perianal streptococcal dermatitis (Group A Streptococcus), a well-described cause of sharply demarcated erythema and fissuring; and (4) rectal mucosal prolapse or trauma-related granulation from persistent straining due to diarrhea or malnutrition. Rare considerations include Langerhans cell histiocytosis or Crohn disease, though the latter is uncommon in this age group.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Visual signifiers include macerated epidermis, irregular ulcers with undermined edges, and a lobulated, pink-red protrusion possibly representing prolapsed mucosa or hypertrophic granulation. The peri-wound halo of erythema and denuded skin is consistent with chronic moisture exposure and friction. Where lesions are flat-topped and broad, they would align with condyloma lata; where satellite pustules cluster at the margins, candidiasis becomes more likely. The absence of systemic markers in the image reminds us that clinical correlation—fever, lymphadenopathy, rash elsewhere, anal fissuring, or mucous membrane lesions—would shape the diagnosis.

[h] Treatment

Historical management in the nineteenth century would have ranged from topical caustics (e.g., silver nitrate) and poultices to mercurial or iodide therapy when syphilis was suspected—approaches now recognized as toxic or suboptimal. Contemporary evidence-based care depends on etiology: confirmed congenital syphilis requires parenteral penicillin G according to age and evaluation; severe diaper dermatitis calls for barrier protection (zinc oxide), frequent diaper changes, gentle cleansing, and topical antifungals for candidiasis; perianal streptococcal dermatitis responds to systemic penicillin/amoxicillin, sometimes with adjunct topical agents; rectal prolapse management begins with treating constipation or diarrhea, stool softeners, nutritional rehabilitation, and, if persistent, surgical consultation. Pain control, meticulous wound hygiene, and safeguarding against secondary infection are essential across scenarios.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Prevention and recovery hinge on determinants beyond the clinic: clean water, reliable diapering supplies, affordable barrier creams, adequate nutrition, and caregiver education. Universal prenatal screening and treatment for maternal syphilis, vaccination programs, and robust primary care reduce infant morbidity. Policies that support paid caregiving time, reduce overcrowding, and ensure access to sanitation materially alter the epidemiology represented by this image.
