A dissected pregnant female — Jacques-Fabien Gautier d’Agoty (Wellcome Library, London)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Jacques-Fabien Gautier d’Agoty (1717–1785) was a French printmaker-anatomist famed for pioneering multi-plate color mezzotint printing. Trained with Jacob Christoph Le Blon, he adapted and promoted four-color printing for scientific illustration and produced spectacular anatomical atlases in collaboration with physicians and surgeons. His plates straddle art and science: they are theatrically staged, yet precise enough to serve medical education.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The work belongs to the mid-eighteenth century Enlightenment, a period that embraced empirical observation, the public demonstration of anatomy, and the rise of “man-midwifery.” Parisian print culture disseminated anatomical knowledge widely to academies, private collectors, and students. At the same time, obstetrics was professionalizing, with debates over the role of traditional midwives versus university-trained male practitioners. Images like this mediated between spectacle and instruction, satisfying curiosity about the hidden body while asserting medicine’s authority over reproduction.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The standing flayed figure—an écorché—echoes classical contrapposto, lending dignified poise to a dissected subject. The parted abdominal wall and opened uterus reveal a near-term fetus, turning the womb into a theater of generation. Dispersed anatomical fragments at the base intensify the memento mori undertone: life and death cohabit one body. The gesture upward, common in academic figuration, may allude to enlightenment—knowledge drawn from the opened body. Such staging aligns the “anatomical Venus” tradition: a feminized, idealized corpse presented for contemplation and instruction.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Color mezzotint enables sumptuous reds for muscle and warm flesh tones that read volumetrically. The dark ground throws the body into relief (chiaroscuro), while the tall, narrow format concentrates vertical movement, echoing the uterine axis. Edges are softened by mezzotint grain, a choice that aestheticizes gore and makes the body legible. D’Agoty’s hybrid style—equal parts anatomical accuracy and pictorial flourish—helped standardize a visual grammar for obstetric illustration used well into the nineteenth century.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Such plates depended on cadavers often acquired from marginalized populations, including poor women and the executed, raising questions of consent. Visual authority accrued to male artists, printers, and surgeons; midwives—who historically managed childbirth—were displaced and sometimes denigrated. Access to printed atlases was limited to elites, reinforcing knowledge hierarchies that mapped onto class and gender. The spectacle of a sexualized yet passive female body reveals how reproductive knowledge was extracted within unequal social relations.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The image itself depicts normal pregnancy anatomy rather than disease, but it participates in efforts to understand—and reduce—maternal mortality. Major eighteenth-century threats included obstructed labor (often due to cephalopelvic disproportion or malpresentation), postpartum hemorrhage (uterine atony, placenta previa/accreta), and puerperal fever (streptococcal sepsis). Contemporary observers lacked germ theory; etiology of fever was debated and often misattributed to “miasma.” Anatomical atlases clarified fetal positions, pelvic relations, and uterine vascularity, foundational for safer delivery practices.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

While not diagnostic, several anatomical features bear on pathology: the enlarged gravid uterus displaces abdominal viscera; the uterine wall’s vascularity foreshadows hemorrhage risk after placental separation; fetal orientation determines likelihood of obstructed labor. Because placenta and cervix are not explicitly shown, the print cannot indicate previa or dilation state. Its primary value lies in visualizing spatial relationships—how soft tissues and pelvis negotiate passage—knowledge crucial for recognizing when labor deviates from normal progress.

[h] Treatment

In the artist’s era, interventions included manual version, the increasingly standardized use of forceps (advocated by William Smellie and André Levret), and destructive operations on the fetus when maternal life was at risk. Caesarean section was rare and perilous; antisepsis, anesthesia, uterotonic drugs, and blood transfusion were not yet established. Management of puerperal fever lacked effective therapy until antiseptic practice (Semmelweis, later Lister) and, ultimately, antibiotics. Modern care contrasts sharply: active management of the third stage with oxytocin, surgical hemostasis, safe caesarean, asepsis, and broad-spectrum antibiotics have transformed outcomes.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Maternal morbidity and mortality correlate with poverty, nutrition, age, parity, distance from skilled birth attendants, and hygienic conditions of delivery. Legal status and education affect timing of care-seeking; racialized and class-based discrimination produce disparate outcomes in many health systems. The print’s history mirrors these determinants: the bodies used to advance knowledge were often those least protected by law, and the benefits of that knowledge accrued unevenly. Today, addressing transport, trained personnel, respectful care, and access to emergency obstetric services is essential to redress inequities.
