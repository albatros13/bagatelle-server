Worrell Maker of Strong Medicine Contemporary

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

This work is associated with the American sculptor Bill Worrell, whose elongated bronze “shaman” figures are informed by Lower Pecos Canyonlands rock art of southwest Texas and northern Mexico. Rather than reproducing any single tribal tradition, Worrell abstracts pan–Archaic healer imagery into contemporary sculpture, engaging with deep-time iconographies related to ceremony, mediation, and “medicine.”

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

The piece is contemporary, yet it converses with imagery from the Archaic period (ca. 2500 BCE–500 CE) pictographs of the Lower Pecos. Those murals depict ritual specialists, animals, and power objects and have been linked by scholars to shamanic cosmologies. Worrell’s translation of this corpus into bronze emerged in the late twentieth century amid renewed archaeological study and public interest in Indigenous rock art of Texas. The work thus sits at a nexus of contemporary Southwestern art markets, heritage tourism, and academic interpretation of prehistoric art.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The figure’s upraised arms signal invocation or supplication, a gesture seen in Lower Pecos anthropomorphs. The attenuated body, bird-like head form, and hand-held staff or bone suggest intercession with nonhuman powers. The turquoise-green patina evokes copper minerals prominent in the Southwest and often associated with healing and protection. The rock base recalls canyonstone, anchoring the figure to a specific geological place while elevating the healer as a liminal mediator between earth and sky. Collectively these signs articulate a “maker of strong medicine”—a ritual agent focusing communal hope on protection from harm and the restoration of balance.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Formal elongation emphasizes vertical thrust and transcendence. The simplified geometry reduces detail to essential profiles, allowing negative space around the upraised arms to dramatize the figure’s appeal to the unseen. Surface patination and textural contrasts between cast bronze and rough stone create a dialogue of refined artifice and primordial terrain. Balance is achieved through the narrow stance on a broad boulder, a visual metaphor for precarious human health stabilized by ritual and knowledge.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The Lower Pecos visual heritage belongs to descendant Indigenous communities and regional histories often marginalized in mainstream U.S. narratives. Contemporary adaptations raise questions about authorship, benefit, and consent: who profits from ancient iconographies, and whose stories are centered? The history of disease in the Americas—especially post-contact epidemics—magnifies inequities, as Native populations disproportionately suffered mortality due to colonial displacement, crowding, and restricted access to care. A sculpture celebrating “medicine” thus inevitably invokes ethical discussions about representation and historical justice.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

As a biomedical lens, one can read the work against the history of epidemic smallpox, a defining affliction in the colonial Southwest. Smallpox is caused by Variola virus (Orthopoxvirus) and transmitted primarily through respiratory droplets and, less commonly, fomites. After an incubation of roughly 10–14 days, patients develop a prodrome of high fever and malaise followed by a characteristic centrifugal rash progressing from macules to papules, vesicles, pustules, and scabs. Though eradicated globally by 1980, smallpox’s historical devastation in Indigenous communities contextualizes the social meaning of “strong medicine” as both ritual and community protection.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Pathophysiologically, viremia seeds dermal microvasculature, producing the synchronous rash and deep-seated lesions. Clinical signs include high fever, severe back pain, prostration, and lesions with umbilication that evolve uniformly across body areas, with facial and distal limb predominance. Mortality in variola major historically reached 20–30%. Scarring and blindness were significant sequelae among survivors. In a symbolic register, the figure’s raised arms and staff can be read as amplifiers of communal vigilance and care in the face of such unmistakable visible disease.

[h] Treatment

Historically, care was supportive: hydration, management of fever, and prevention of secondary bacterial infection. Vaccination (variolation historically; later vaccinia-based vaccines) proved decisive in control and eradication. Modern preparedness references antivirals such as tecovirimat (TPOXX), which inhibits the viral VP37 protein to block egress; cidofovir and brincidofovir have in vitro activity. Contemporary vaccines like ACAM2000 and MVA-BN (Jynneos/Imvamune) are maintained for biodefense. In parallel, medical anthropology underscores how ritual, placebo-responsive pathways, and social cohesion can modulate symptom meaning, adherence, and stress physiology, complementing biomedical interventions.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Disease vulnerability hinges on social conditions: crowding, nutritional stress, displacement, and limited access to trusted health services. Historical policies that fractured Indigenous lifeways amplified contact-based transmission and curtailed care-seeking. Today, structural barriers—geographic isolation, underfunded clinics, linguistic mismatches, and historical trauma—continue to shape infectious-disease risk and chronic disease burdens. The sculpture’s rock foundation can be read as a call to re-ground health in place-based knowledge, equity, and culturally safe care.
