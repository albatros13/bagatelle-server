Painting by Nil Sari depicting the treatment of insane patients by musical therapy

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe
Often attributed to Nil Sarı, a Turkish historian of medicine who has written extensively on Ottoman healing traditions, this image adopts the visual language of Ottoman court miniatures to narrate care for the mentally ill through music. Whether executed by Sarı or produced under her scholarly influence, the work functions as an educational tableau: it synthesizes textual sources on Ottoman hospital practice with the iconography of musicians, attendants, and patients gathered in a curative setting.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)
The composition references late fifteenth- to seventeenth-century Anatolia and Rumelia, when charitable hospital complexes (darüşşifa) flourished under waqf endowments. The most cited exemplar is the Sultan Bayezid II complex in Edirne (founded 1488), where archival and museological evidence indicate the therapeutic use of water sounds and art music. Ottoman medical thought blended Galenic–Avicennian humoralism with Sufi metaphysics and courtly musical knowledge. In this milieu, music was not mere diversion but a calibrated intervention believed to restore equilibrium of temperament and spirit.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography
The central fountain symbolizes purification, auditory masking, and humoral balance—water’s cool, moist qualities were thought to temper excess heat or dryness. A ring of performers with oud, ney, rebab or kemençe, kanun, and small kettledrums (kudüm) signifies the orchestration of modes (makam), timbre, and rhythm for particular ailments. Patients sit at the perimeter, some shaved for hygiene, signaling institutional care. Windows behind imply the controlled light of a ward, while the circle of figures visualizes concord, an allegory of the harmonized soul.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)
The miniature style employs flat perspective, saturated yet matte color fields, and strong contour lines. Hieratic scaling centers the therapeutic apparatus—the fountain and instruments—over individual psychology, aligning with manuscript illustration’s didactic aim. Repetition and radial symmetry around the basin create visual rhythm analogous to rhythmic cycles (usul) in Ottoman music. Patterned tiles and textiles establish unity, while controlled negative space keeps the eye circulating, echoing the cyclical temporality of musical treatment sessions.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities
Despite charitable foundations, access clustered in urban centers attached to imperial patronage; rural populations had fewer options. Gender norms limited women’s presence within certain wards and ensembles. Definitions of “madness” were shaped by religious and legal authority, sometimes conflating deviance with illness. Enslaved, migrant, or non-Muslim subjects could face differentiated pathways to care, despite theoretical inclusivity of waqf charters.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology
Historical texts reference melancholia (melankoli), “sinn-i perişan” (deranged mind), mania, epilepsy, and states akin to anxiety or depressive disorders. Etiology was humoral—imbalances of black bile, phlegm, blood, and yellow bile—modulated by season, diet, and emotion. Today we would parse these presentations into mood disorders, psychoses, epilepsy, or trauma-related conditions. While taxonomies differ, both frameworks acknowledge interactions among body, environment, and affect.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness
The artwork codes illness through posture and spatial relations: patients are seated, inward-turned, or attended by caretakers. Shaved scalps and uniform garments signal institutional hygiene and identity as patients. The serene, enclosed garden anticipates contemporary insights that structured, low-arousal settings support de-escalation in acute psychiatric distress.

[h] Treatment
Ottoman protocols matched specific makams, rhythms, and instruments to temperaments, hours, and seasons, sometimes integrating aromatics and dietary regimens. Water provided continuous broadband sound to dampen intrusive thoughts and promote sleep. Contemporary evidence indicates music therapy can reduce negative symptoms in schizophrenia, alleviate anxiety and depression, and regulate autonomic function. Neurobiologically, emotionally salient music engages dopaminergic reward circuits, modulates limbic and prefrontal networks, entrains motor timing, and influences vagal tone—mechanisms consistent with the historical aim of rebalancing affect and physiology.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness
Waqf financing, urban proximity, social stigma, and family support shaped who entered and remained in care. Poverty, displacement, and conflict increased vulnerability to mental illness while limiting access. Literacy and musical education affected the availability of trained performers, concentrating sophisticated therapies in courtly centers like Edirne and Istanbul.
