A seated female figure with a dissected abdomen (photograph after a woodcut, ca. 1525–1530)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The image derives from an anonymous early sixteenth‑century European woodcut, likely produced within the Germanic or Northern Italian print traditions that supplied illustrations for medical and midwifery manuals. Such images circulated widely across workshops and editions, so authorship often resides with a printer’s shop rather than a named artist. The figure belongs to a corpus of pedagogical images that visualized female generative anatomy for readers of obstetrical treatises and compendia of practical medicine.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Dated roughly 1525–1530, the print sits in the transitional decades before Vesalius (1543), when book printing amplified older Galenic-Aristotelian doctrines even as humanist anatomy revived dissection. Manuals for midwives—circulating in Latin and vernacular—sought to standardize reproductive knowledge amid urban regulation of birth work. Visual conventions borrowed from medieval “anatomical tables” and astrological medicine persisted alongside new empirical interests, producing hybrid images that are at once iconic and didactic.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The seated, frontal woman, thighs apart, echoes the birthing chair and stages the womb as a site of revelation. The flayed abdomen functions as a “cutaway” emblem rather than a forensic dissection, turning the body into a legible text. Radiating lines that connect organs to marginal inscriptions typify symptom-and-remedy charts, guiding readers from bodily zones to therapeutic advice. The exposed uterus, often schematized like a vessel with attached conduits, condenses medieval ideas of channels, humors, and maternal nurture; enlarged breasts signal fecundity and lactation within a moralized cycle of reproduction.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

Thick contour lines, minimal modeling, and a strong, centralized symmetry produce legibility over naturalism. Proportions are subordinated to clarity: the uterus and its attachments are oversized, the viscera simplified into emblematic forms. Linearity and frontality make the figure read like a diagram; captions and numbers integrate image and text in a tightly orchestrated page design typical of relief printing, where blocks and type locked together in the press.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The visual authority of printed medicine participated in the professional displacement of women’s embodied knowledge. While marketed to midwives, many editions were authored, translated, and glossed by male physicians, often in Latin, which restricted access. The normative European female body stands as the template for “woman,” erasing variation in anatomy, ethnicity, disability, and age. Legal and ecclesiastical oversight of midwifery intersected with poverty, literacy, and gendered power, shaping who received care and whose expertise counted as science.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The biomedical content centers on pregnancy and disorders of the uterus. Early modern etiologies framed reproductive problems—infertility, miscarriage, obstructed labor, “uterine suffocation” (later pathologized as hysteria)—through humoral imbalance, congestion of menstrual blood, or unfavorable celestial influences. From a modern standpoint, many conditions referenced by such charts map onto anemia, endocrine disorders, pelvic inflammatory disease, malpresentation, or cephalopelvic disproportion. Postpartum fever—now known to be infectious—was then linked to putrefaction of lochia or corrupt airs rather than microbial pathogens.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The radiating pointers mark diagnostic sites: abdomen for uterine pain, breasts for lactation disorders and mastitis, groin for hernias or varicosities, and back for labor pain. The schematic womb, often drawn with a single cavity, reflects pre-Vesalian misconceptions (e.g., the “seven-celled” uterus theory lingered), which shaped how symptoms were interpreted. Visual simplifications could mislead—placental position, fetal lie, and pelvic architecture are not anatomically accurate—yet the diagram effectively indexed where patients and practitioners localized complaint and intervention.

[h] Treatment

Contemporary remedies combined regimen (diet, rest), thermal therapies (baths, fomentations), manipulations for turning the fetus, pessaries, and a robust pharmacopoeia: emollients, aromatics, and emmenagogues. Bloodletting and cupping targeted perceived excess or stasis of humors. Abortifacient and oxytocic plants (e.g., savin juniper, rue) appeared in recipes, with attendant toxicity risks recognized even then. Comparing with current evidence-based care, obstructed labor is addressed by skilled assisted delivery or cesarean, infection by antisepsis and antibiotics, and postpartum hemorrhage by uterotonics and surgical management—illustrating the dramatic shift from humoral theory to microbial and anatomical science.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Maternal outcomes in the sixteenth century were closely tied to parity, nutrition, age at first birth, and poverty. Crowded housing and limited sanitation increased puerperal infections; seasonal labor and famine exacerbated anemia. Access to competent midwives, proximity to hospitals, and the ability to pay for a surgeon in emergencies stratified risk. Normative expectations around chastity and fertility also shaped when women sought help and how their complaints were moralized or dismissed.
