Svamihamsasvarupakrtam Swami Hamsasvarupa (1903)

a. Artist/ Group/Tribe

The artwork entitled "Svamihamsasvarupakrtam Swami Hamsasvarupa" was created in 1903. The piece is traditionally attributed to Swami Hamsasvarupa, a figure whose works often merge spiritual and scientific philosophies, prevalent in early 20th-century Indian art. Swami Hamsasvarupa’s contributions are profound as they offer a blend of traditional Indian spiritual iconography and emerging western scientific perspectives, especially in the realms of anatomy and physiology.

b. Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-Frame)

Created in 1903 during the British colonial period in India, this piece stands at the intersection of an influential era whereby traditional Indian knowledge was being systematically juxtaposed with Western sciences. This period was characterized by reform and revivalist movements seeking to reclaim traditional knowledge systems. The artwork is reflective of a larger socio-cultural trend, which aimed to bridge Eastern spiritual wisdom with Western scientific rationalism.

c. Symbolism and/ or Iconography

This artwork serves as a spiritual and intellectual diagram, harmonizing chakras and meditative elements with anatomical features of the human brain. The juxtaposition of Sanskrit texts with anatomical labeling symbolizes a fusion of traditional and modern understandings of the human body and mind, emphasizing a holistic approach to health and spirituality.

d. Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/ Principles of Design)

The drawing predominantly utilizes line work and soft color schematics, creating a clear yet gentle illustration of the human brain's anatomy alongside Sanskrit annotations. The symmetry and balance in the composition reflect the design principles, aiming for clarity in communication. This dual representation approach not only serves educational purposes but also embodies aesthetic beauty.

e. Social / Cultural Inequities

During the time this artwork was created, significant social and cultural inequities were prevalent, particularly in terms of access to education. The piece underscores the intention of knowledge dissemination across traditional barriers, attempting to enable broader access to complex anatomical knowledge within the constraints of colonial rule.

f. Description of Disease & Etiology

The art does not directly depict any specific disease but provides a framework for understanding neurological health through both spiritual and anatomical lenses. By illustrating the brain’s anatomical structure, it implicitly directs attention toward neurological diseases, which were emerging fields of study in both Indian and Western medicine at that time.

g. Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

While specific pathological signs are not depicted, the emphasis on detailed anatomical rendering suggests an interest in understanding the workings of the brain. This indirectly points to an interest in identifying and diagnosing neurological disorders through both spiritual and scientific approaches.

h. Treatment

The work reflects a holistic view of treatment, embodying both medical and spiritual approaches. Treatments of the era likely included a combination of ayurvedic practices, meditation, and yoga as means to maintain mental and physiological balance and to address illnesses from both physical and metaphysical perspectives.

i. Social Determinants of Illness

Socioeconomic status, access to education, and cultural beliefs significantly influenced health and treatment approaches in 1903. This artwork highlights the importance of integrating spiritual and anatomical knowledge, recognizing the role of cultural heritage in health practices and the potential disparities in access to such integrated knowledge.
