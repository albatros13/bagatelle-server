Analysis of Simone Baschenis' Danse Macabre

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Simone Baschenis was an Italian painter from the Baschenis family, known for their significant contributions to fresco art during the Renaissance. Active in the early to mid-16th century, the Baschenis family worked predominantly in the region of Trentino, Italy. The artist is renowned for his depictions of the Danse Macabre, a theme that carries significant historical and cultural weight.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural context (time-frame)

Created in 1539, "Danse Macabre" by Simone Baschenis was a product of the Renaissance period, which was marked by a renewed interest in classical ideals and humanism. This era also witnessed significant social upheaval due to the Protestant Reformation and the residual effects of the Black Death. This painting reflects societal anxieties about death and the universal fate awaiting all, regardless of social standing.

[c] Symbolism and/ or iconography

The Danse Macabre trope illustrates figures from various social classes accompanied by dancing skeletons, symbolizing death. This iconography serves as a memento mori, a reminder of the transience of life and the inevitable approach of death. By depicting both the elite and commoners in this procession, Baschenis emphasizes the equality of all people in death.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

The fresco employs rhythmic repetition and contrast through the juxtaposition of lively, dynamic human characters alongside rigid skeletal figures. Baschenis utilized muted earth tones typical of fresco work, enhancing the somber mood. The composition is horizontally oriented, guiding viewers through a narrative progression of life and death.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The artwork served as a commentary on social stratification, highlighting inequities by showing that death was the great equalizer. During the 16th century, Europe's rigid class structures were often challenged by art, and the presence of various societal figures within scenes of the Danse Macabre reinforced the futility of social status in the face of mortality.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The specter of the Black Death, which devastated Europe in the 14th century, continued to haunt the Renaissance imagination. The plague, caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, was transmitted by fleas on rats and sometimes through airborne particles. It resulted in widespread mortality, reducing Europe's population significantly and altering its social structures.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The artwork ominously suggests the signs of plague and disease through the depiction of skeletal figures. These serve as an allegorical representation of death and disease, with skeletal forms juxtaposed against living individuals to symbolize the pervasive nature of illness.

[h] Treatment

Treatment during the time of the Black Death was rudimentary, involving practices such as bloodletting and the use of herbal remedies. Without knowledge of bacterial pathogens, medieval Europeans relied on superstition and astrology to guide medical treatments, leading to a myriad of ineffective and sometimes detrimental practices.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Poor sanitation and crowded urban conditions were significant contributors to the spread of disease. Overpopulation in cities and inadequate waste management led to environments where diseases like the plague could thrive. Additionally, social mobility was limited, and lower classes particularly suffered the brunt of epidemics due to inadequate access to resources.
