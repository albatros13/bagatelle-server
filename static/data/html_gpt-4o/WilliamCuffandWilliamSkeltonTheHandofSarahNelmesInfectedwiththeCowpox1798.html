The Hand of Sarah Nelmes Infected with the Cowpox (1798)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The artwork titled "The Hand of Sarah Nelmes Infected with the Cowpox" was created by William Cuff and William Skelton in 1798. William Skelton was known for his engravings, often assisting in the illustration of anatomical and medical works. The depiction of Sarah Nelmes' hand is particularly significant as it exemplifies the intersection of art and science in the 18th century.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-frame)

The engraving dates back to the late 18th century, a period characterized by significant advancements in medical science. This era marked the beginnings of modern vaccination, heavily influenced by the works of Edward Jenner, who pioneered the smallpox vaccine. In 1798, the medical community was beginning to acknowledge the interplay between cowpox and immunity to smallpox, a discovery that revolutionized public health.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography

The image features the hand of Sarah Nelmes, who was a milkmaid. Her hand exhibits pustules resulting from cowpox infection, symbolizing both illness and protection. Cowpox, viewed as a lesser disease, inadvertently became a symbol of prevention against the more deadly smallpox. The pustules are key to understanding the origin of vaccination and signify Jenner’s use of this virus to induce immunity.

[d] Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/Principles of Design)

Stylistically, the engraving uses fine line work to render a realistic image of the human hand, focusing on texture and form to highlight the lesions. The careful attention to anatomical detail reflects the influence of scientific observation. The subtle contrasts in shading emphasize the contours of the hand, bringing attention to the specific areas infected by cowpox, thereby focusing the viewer’s eye on the medical condition portrayed.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The depiction also subtly touches on social inequalities of the time. Milkmaids like Sarah Nelmes were central figures in the understanding of cowpox and its protective effects. Despite their role, they were often marginalized in society and medical discourse centered around prominent male figures. The artwork inadvertently highlights the invisible influence of women in medical history, an issue prevalent even today.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Cowpox is a viral skin infection that occurs in humans who come in direct contact with cow udders infected with the cowpox virus. This zoonotic disease presents with localized pustules, fever, and lymphadenopathy. It is caused by a virus closely related to the variola virus, which causes smallpox, thus leading to cross-immunity.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Key pathological signs of cowpox include the distinct pustular lesions depicted in the artwork. These lesions, although visually alarming, were less severe compared to smallpox and typically healed without extensive treatment. The depicted pustules in the engraving were instrumental in illustrating the early stages of induced immunity.

[h] Treatment

In the late 18th century, treatment options for cowpox were limited, with care focused on symptomatic relief. The recognition of its ability to prevent smallpox led to its use in early vaccination practices, eventually forming the basis for modern immunization techniques. The "variolation" method, which involved inoculating individuals with cowpox to protect against smallpox, was an indirect form of treatment depicted through the artwork.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

The prevalence of cowpox was closely linked to agricultural practices and economic status. Those working directly with cattle, such as rural farmers and milkmaids, like Sarah Nelmes, were more susceptible. The understanding of cowpox as a benign condition with protective benefits against smallpox transcended social determinants, laying the groundwork for accessible vaccination to people irrespective of their social standing.
