William Hogarth - A Rake's Progress: Tom Rakewell ends up in Bethlehem Hospital Madhouse

[a] Artist/Group/Tribe

William Hogarth was an English painter, printmaker, and satirist, recognized for his narrative paintings and engravings. His series "A Rake's Progress" (1733) showcases his talent for storytelling and his ability to capture the social dynamics of 18th-century England.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-Frame)

"A Rake's Progress" was created during the 1730s, a period marked by significant social and economic developments in England. The Industrial Revolution was underway, bringing about rapid urbanization and social change. Hogarth's work reflects the anxieties and moral concerns of this transformative era.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography

This piece, the final scene of "A Rake's Progress," is set in Bethlehem Hospital, known as Bedlam. The madhouse symbolizes the ultimate consequence of a reckless and indulgent lifestyle. Hogarth uses the imagery of madness to critique social inequities and the neglect of mental health.

[d] Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/Principles of Design)

Hogarth combines realism with caricature to accentuate the characters' expressions and the chaotic atmosphere. The use of light and shadow underscores the emotional intensity, while the crowded composition amplifies the sense of madness and despair inside the asylum.

[e] Social/Cultural Inequities

Hogarth's depiction of Bedlam highlights the harsh treatment and stigmatization of mental illness in the 18th century. It serves as a commentary on the social dynamics where the poorest souls end up in neglected institutions, reflecting broader societal neglect and moral decay.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Though not explicitly medical, the artwork illustrates historical perceptions of mental illness. In the 18th century, mental illnesses were poorly understood, often seen as a result of moral failings or divine punishment rather than medical conditions.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The figures' erratic gestures, vacant expressions, and unkempt appearances are artistic signifiers of madness. Hogarth uses these to personify the symptoms of mental deterioration as understood in his time, including delusions, confusion, and uncontrollable behavior.

[h] Treatment

In the 18th century, treatments for mental illness were rudimentary and often inhumane. Institutions like Bedlam rarely provided medical care; rather, they functioned as places of confinement. Hogarth's depiction critiques this lack of empathy and understanding.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

The artwork underscores how social and economic factors contribute to mental health issues. Tom's decline into madness is accelerated by his reckless lifestyle, hinting at the interconnectedness of personal choices and social circumstances in contributing to mental health.
