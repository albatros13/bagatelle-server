Caius Gabriel Cibber Meloncoly (1676)

[a] Artist/Group/Tribe: Caius Gabriel Cibber was a Danish sculptor who played a significant role in the Baroque art scene in England during the late 17th century. Known for his expressive sculptures, Cibber's work often embodies dramatic realism, capturing emotional depth in his subjects. His expertise and mastery in crafting sculptures led to his significant contributions to iconic structures, including works at St. Paul's Cathedral.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (time-frame): The sculpture "Meloncoly" was created in 1676, during a period when Baroque art was prevalent across Europe. The Baroque era, spanning from around 1600 to 1750, was characterized by grandeur and movement, expressive use of color, and dramatic effects. This period was a reaction against the more restrained aesthetics of the Renaissance, focusing instead on emotional intensity and engagement. In England, the Baroque style subtly differed by incorporating restrained elegance.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography: The sculpture of "Meloncoly" represents a powerful iconographic image of mental illness, which, during the 17th century, was often depicted with a sense of ambiguity and mystery. The posture and expression captured in the sculpture reflect deep introspection and a somber state indicative of melancholy. The iconography aligns with a historical understanding of melancholy as a condition affecting both the mind and body, often associated with profound creativity, genius, or philosophical contemplation.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities: During the 17th century, societal understanding and treatment of mental health were limited and often fraught with stigma. Melancholia, or what we might now term depression, was misunderstood and sometimes romanticized as a trait of artistic genius. Nevertheless, individuals afflicted with mental illness often faced isolation and misunderstanding, reflecting broader social inequities and lack of support structures for mental health issues.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology: Melancholy, historically described as an excess of the "black bile" humor, was believed in the humoral theory to be a physiological condition causing psychological symptoms. This condition was presumed to manifest in symptoms such as sadness, lethargy, and a sense of hopelessness, affecting daily life and productivity.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness: In art, melancholy was often depicted through an individual's posture or expression. In Cibber's sculpture, the signs of melancholy are evident in the slumped posture, hand placement, and distant, contemplative gaze. These visual signifiers act as metaphors for the internal struggle and emotional state associated with the condition.

[h] Treatment: In the 17th century, treatments for melancholy were varied and often rudimentary, relying heavily on dietary regulations, herbal concoctions, and sometimes bloodletting. As understanding was limited, treatments reflected the contemporary belief in balancing bodily humors rather than empirical medical intervention.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness: Economic status, social class, and access to support networks heavily influenced the experience and management of melancholia. Those of higher social standing may have had more access to education and thus more nuanced understanding and treatment options while the lower classes might have faced harsher conditions and limited resources.
