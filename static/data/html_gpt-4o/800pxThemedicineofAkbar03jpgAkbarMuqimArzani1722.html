The Medicine of Akbar by Akbar Muqim Arzani

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The artwork "The Medicine of Akbar" is attributed to Akbar Muqim Arzani, a prominent figure known for his contributions to medical manuscripts during the late Mughal period. Arzani, hailed from the rich tradition of Persian and Indian medical scholarship, is often recognized for synthesizing the medical knowledge of his time with traditional Islamic medicine.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (time-frame)

This work was produced in 1722, during the Mughal Empire's waning years. The Mughals were renowned for their patronage of the arts and sciences, and this era was marked by extensive cultural and artistic synthesis between Persian, Indian, and Islamic traditions.

[c] Symbolism and/ or Iconography

The artwork is rife with symbolic elements. It displays a human figure illustrating various internal organs, reminiscent of anatomical drawings. The use of vibrant colors and symbolic depictions of body parts points to a blend of religious and medical symbolism, showing the merging of art and science.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

The illustration employs bold outlines and earthy colors typical of Mughal miniature painting. The intricate detailing of human anatomy reflects a deep engagement with both aesthetic appeal and scientific accuracy, merging visual artistry with educational intent.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The illustration underscores the socio-cultural dynamics of health access during the Mughal era. Medical knowledge was often limited to the elite, highlighting a disparity in access to healthcare resources, a reflection of broader societal inequalities.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

In the context of this artwork, understanding human anatomy was crucial for disease diagnosis. The depiction does not highlight a specific illness but rather provides a broad overview, illustrating how anatomy was conceptualized and studied in the period.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The absence of specific disease markers in the artwork suggests its primary focus was on anatomical education rather than pathology. However, the detailing of organs like the heart and liver points to an understanding of their importance in diagnosing ailments.

[h] Treatment

Treatments during this time were grounded in balancing bodily humors. While the artwork does not directly depict treatments, it serves as a foundational guide for practitioners employing herbal remedies and dietary recommendations as prescribed in Unani medicine.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Access to medical knowledge was often determined by class and education. The depiction itself suggests a scholarly appreciation, likely intended for an audience with the means to access and utilize such medical texts to enhance their understanding of human health.
