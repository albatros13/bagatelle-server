Santa Isabel de Hungria Curando Tinosos

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The painting "Santa Isabel de Hungria curando tinosos" is attributed to an unknown artist from the European tradition, possibly dating back to the 17th century. Saint Elizabeth of Hungary was frequently depicted in art, emphasizing her saintly deeds and acts of charity.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-frame)

The artwork originates from a period where religious art played a crucial role in both spiritual and daily life. Saint Elizabeth, who lived from 1207 to 1231, became a symbol of Christian charity and piety. The painting likely reflects the values of the Baroque era, a time of dramatic expression and religious fervor.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography

In the painting, Saint Elizabeth is portrayed healing those afflicted with disease. Her compassionate gaze and gentle touch symbolize Christian charity. The individuals surrounding her represent societal outcasts suffering from an illness, symbolizing both physical and spiritual healing.

[d] Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/ Principles of Design)

The painting exhibits characteristic elements of Baroque art, including a strong contrast between light and shadow, which highlights the saint's divine mission. The composition draws the viewer's eye towards Elizabeth, creating a focal point amidst the chaotic scene.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The artwork underscores the disparities between social classes and the marginalized status of those suffering from contagious diseases. It reflects on the role of nobility and the church in addressing social inequities through acts of charity.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The artwork indicates a case of tinea capitis, a fungal infection of the scalp. Common in crowded living conditions of the time, it caused patches of hair loss and was often associated with poor hygiene and poverty.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The individuals depicted in the painting show signs of hair loss, indicative of tinea capitis. The focus on these physical symptoms heightens the painting's emotional and moral impact.

[h] Treatment

In the medieval period, treatment of such conditions was rudimentary. Traditional methods included herbal remedies and improved hygiene practices, though they were largely ineffective until the advent of antifungal medications in modern times.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Poverty, overcrowding, and lack of medical knowledge contributed to the prevalence of tinea capitis. The painting resonates with contemporary discussions around the impact of social determinants on health outcomes.
