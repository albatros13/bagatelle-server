Charles Eisenmann Studio: Russian Dog Face Boy (1880)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe: The photograph, taken by Charles Eisenmann, captures a unique historical figure often referred to as the "Russian Dog Face Boy." Eisenmann, a notable photographer of his time, was known for his portraits of unusual subjects, frequently exhibiting performers from sideshows and circuses.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-Frame): This photograph dates back to the 1880s, a period marked by industrialization and cultural fascination with the exotic and unusual. Public curiosity in the "freak shows" was both a form of entertainment and a reflection of the social attitudes towards physical anomalies at that time.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography: The image symbolically represents human curiosity and the perception of the "other." Acting as both a spectacle and a subject of intrigue, the figure highlights the era's interest in unique physical conditions, often exploited for their spectacle value and the allure of the unknown.

[d] Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/Principles of Design): Eisenmann's photographic style brings a formal, almost regal quality to the subjects he photographed. Compositionally, the image uses symmetry and contrast to draw attention to both the uniqueness and humanity of its subject, lending dignity often denied to performers in their public roles.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities: The photograph underscores the inequities faced by those with physical differences, who were often commodified for entertainment purposes. Their portrayal in visual media, such as photographs, contributed to their objectification and the perpetuation of stereotypes.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology: The subject of this photograph likely had hypertrichosis, a condition characterized by abnormal hair growth across the body. This rare disorder can be congenital or acquired, and is rooted in genetic mutations affecting hair follicle function.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness: The excessive facial and bodily hair seen in this photographic subject is the most prominent signifier of hypertrichosis. Unlike typical hair growth patterns, this condition involves a nearly complete coverage which is visually striking.

[h] Treatment: Historically, treatments for hypertrichosis have included various hair removal methods, ranging from shaving to more modern approaches like laser therapy. However, in the 1880s, such options were not available, and individuals often had to deal with the stigma and physical manifestation without medical intervention.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness: Factors such as socioeconomic status, public perception, and lack of access to medical care compounded the challenges faced by individuals with hypertrichosis during this period. Public exhibition and the associated stigma influenced their societal standing and quality of life.
