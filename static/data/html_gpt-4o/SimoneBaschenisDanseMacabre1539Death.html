Simone Baschenis Danse Macabre Death (1539)

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

Simone Baschenis was an Italian painter from a lineage of artists known as the Baschenis family, renowned for their frescoes during the Renaissance period. The family, active in the Trentino and Bergamo regions, achieved recognition for their unique style and thematic exploration of mortality.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (time-frame)

The artwork "Danse Macabre Death" by Simone Baschenis, created in 1539, is set against the backdrop of the Renaissance period, a time when Europe was undergoing significant cultural transformations. Plagues and pandemics, such as the Bubonic plague, had left an indelible mark on society, influencing art and literature of the era.

[c] Symbolism and/ or Iconography

"Danse Macabre" symbolizes the universality of death, depicting figures from all walks of life engaged in a dance with skeletons. This dance, often illustrating different societal classes, serves as a reminder of mortality, emphasizing the equality of all before death.

[d] Stylistic Significance (elements of art/ principles of design)

The fresco showcases the use of muted color palettes typical of the Renaissance period, with strong linear perspectives and a rhythmic flow of figures. Baschenis employed chiaroscuro to create depth and contrast, accentuating the macabre theme.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The representation of various societal classes in the dance reveals the era’s rigid social structures, yet the inclusion in the macabre dance suggests an existential egalitarianism. The artwork challenges societal hierarchies by depicting how death spares no one, rich or poor.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The artwork implicitly references the Bubonic plague, a disease characterized by swollen lymph nodes, fever, and hemorrhages. Originating from Yersinia pestis, the plague was transmitted through flea bites and deteriorated Europe’s population substantially for centuries.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Though the artwork does not explicitly depict illness, the skeletal figures and grim settings symbolize the plague's symptoms. The pervasive presence of death is mirrored in the emaciated figures reminiscent of plague victims.

[h] Treatment

In the 16th century, treatment options for the Bubonic plague were extremely limited, often involving superstitious practices. Quarantine and isolation were the primary methods to control outbreaks, although treatments like bloodletting and herbal remedies were common despite their ineffectiveness.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Poor sanitation, crowded living conditions, and limited medical knowledge were key factors in the spread of the plague. Additionally, trade routes facilitated the rapid movement of infections across regions, exacerbating the spread among varying populations.
