Art Historical and Medical Analysis of "Mrs Bennett Cured of Skin Disease"

[a] Artist/ Group/ Tribe

The artwork "Mrs Bennett Cured of Skin Disease" is part of the remarkable collection held by the Wellcome Library. Though the exact artist is unclear, it reflects the artistic style prevalent in portraiture during the 18th and 19th centuries, possibly linked to artists who worked under the tutelage or influence of well-known figures like Joshua Reynolds or Thomas Gainsborough, who were known for their attention to human detail and compassion reflected in their works.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-Frame)

Created in the tumultuous period marked by rapid developments in both the arts and sciences, the painting likely originates from the late 18th century or early 19th century—a time when both medicine and art were undergoing significant changes. The Industrial Revolution was transforming societies, while new diseases and medical conditions were being documented and studied more rigorously, thereby influencing art, literature, and public consciousness.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography

The portrait is imbued with personal symbolism. Mrs. Bennett’s serene expression, post-treatment, conveys triumph over adversity. The headscarf could symbolize modesty and protection, elements often imbued in personal recovery narratives. The dark, muted background draws attention to her countenance, emphasizing recovery and enlightenment.

[d] Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/Principles of Design)

This artwork showcases a classic approach to portraiture, relying on chiaroscuro to enhance depth and emotional engagement. The use of light and shadow gives dimensionality to Mrs. Bennett's face, while the softness around the eyes and mouth generates a sense of realism. Gentle brushwork creates a smooth texture, conveying the delicate nature of her skin post-treatment.

[e] Social/Cultural Inequities

During the 18th and 19th centuries, medical treatments were often inaccessible to the lower classes, indicating a socio-economic divide. This artwork, while celebrating personal recovery, inadvertently highlights the systemic inequities in healthcare access, reflecting class distinctions in treatment availability and quality.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

While the specifics of Mrs. Bennett’s skin disease are not detailed, such conditions during this time were often caused by industrial pollution, poor nutrition, or genetic predispositions. Skin ailments may have also been exacerbated by limited understanding of hygiene and contact with toxic substances prevalent in daily life.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The signs suggesting skin disease, treated or not visible in this portrait, might have included inflammation, discoloration, or texture changes. Artists often sensitively depicted post-recovery states to emphasize transformation and healing, perhaps omitting more disturbing visuals for aesthetic harmony.

[h] Treatment

During this period, skin diseases were treated using a variety of methods including herbal remedies, early pharmaceutical treatments, and in some cases, unconventional methods like mercury or sulfur baths. The specifics of Mrs. Bennett’s treatment might have encompassed such practices, allowing medical professionals to experiment with evolving theories of dermatology.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

The prevalence of skin diseases during this era was influenced by factors such as living conditions, occupational hazards, and nutrition. Overcrowding in urban areas, prevalent during industrialization, contributed to the spread of ailments. The dichotomy between the affluent and the poor in terms of treatment outcomes is evident in historical medical records.
