Charles Eisenmann's Sacred Hairy Family of Birma (1910)

[a] Artist/Group/Tribe

Charles Eisenmann was a renowned photographer in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, known for capturing the images of sideshow performers and various human anomalies. His work primarily focused on individuals who were considered curiosities or oddities in a period when such individuals were exhibited for entertainment.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-frame)

The photograph "Sacred Hairy Family of Birma" from 1910 reflects a time when exhibitions featuring people with unique physical features were popular forms of entertainment in Europe and America. This period was characterized by an interest in anthropological studies, often leading to the patronizing and exoticizing display of human diversity.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography

The symbolism within this photograph can be analyzed on multiple levels. The image captures the so-called “Sacred Hairy Family,” likely referring to individuals with hypertrichosis, a condition causing excessive hair growth. The term "sacred" possibly denotes an attempt to portray them with dignity or allude to a mythical or religious significance attributed to their appearance in some cultures.

[d] Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/Principles of Design)

Stylistically, Eisenmann’s photograph employs straightforward composition and lighting typical of studio portrait photography of that era. The focus remains on the subjects, with minimal background distractions, enhancing the details and texture of their hair and clothing, which were intentionally curated to accentuate their exoticized qualities.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

The photograph underscores the social and cultural inequities of the time, as it portrays individuals with a genetic condition as subjects of curiosity. This reflects broader issues of representation and exploitation, wherein individuals with physical differences were often objectified and commodified within the entertainment industry.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

Hypertrichosis is a medical condition characterized by excessive hair growth anywhere on a person’s body. The etiology can vary, encompassing both congenital forms, such as Ambras Syndrome, and acquired forms due to factors like medication or endocrine disorders. In congenital cases, it is often associated with a genetic mutation affecting hair growth regulators.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Pathologically, hypertrichosis manifests as notable and possibly burdensome hair coverage beyond the typical patterns seen in the broader population. The condition itself is benign, but is often stigmatized due to its visual impact, leading to potential psychosocial implications for those affected.

[h] Treatment

Treatment for hypertrichosis can include cosmetic procedures, such as laser hair removal, electrolysis, or topical treatments to reduce hair growth. Addressing psychosocial effects is also crucial, often requiring psychological support and counseling in addition to cosmetic interventions.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Social determinants include the stigmatization and societal perceptions of individuals with visible differences. Factors such as access to healthcare, cultural narratives around appearance, and socioeconomic status play significant roles in the experience and treatment of conditions like hypertrichosis.
