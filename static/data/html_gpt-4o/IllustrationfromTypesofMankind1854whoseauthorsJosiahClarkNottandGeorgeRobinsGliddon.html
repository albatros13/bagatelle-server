Analysis of "Types of Mankind" by Josiah Clark Nott and George Robins Gliddon (1854)

Artist/ Group/Tribe

The illustration is a product of the collaborative work of Josiah Clark Nott and George Robins Gliddon, two American physicians and authors. Nott and Gliddon were prominent figures in the 19th-century movement of scientific racism, using their work to promote theories about the inferiority of non-European races.

Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-Frame)

This work emerged in 1854 during a period marked by significant racial and scientific discourse in the United States, particularly around the time of slavery and the burgeoning anthropological studies attempting to classify human races. The socio-cultural dynamics of this period were steeped in racial prejudices that were often legitimized through pseudo-scientific literature.

Symbolism and/or Iconography

The illustration graphically depicts various human and primate profiles meant to highlight supposed physical and intellectual hierarchies among races. This visual typology reinforced the erroneous belief that physical characteristics correlated with intelligence and moral standing, a stereotype that was heavily propagated for racial justification.

Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/ Principles of Design)

The artwork uses a stark black-and-white contrast with precise line work typical of scientific illustrations of the period. The lack of color and focus on profile views were intended to emphasize objective analysis, though the interpretation was inherently biased. The structured format organizes the figures hierarchically, illustrating prejudiced comparisons.

Social / Cultural Inequities

"Types of Mankind" perpetuated notions of racial hierarchy that preserved social inequities, underpinning discriminatory practices and policies. It gave pseudoscientific credence to racial segregation and the mistreatment of people deemed racially inferior, influencing societal attitudes well into the future.

Description of Disease & Etiology

Not directly depicting any disease, this artwork parallels biological anthropology with misguided notions of 'racial science,' treating racial differences as pathological. The flawed analogy between race and disease was used to suggest inherent racial inadequacies.

Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

No explicit signs of illness are portrayed; however, the analogy between primate and human profiles was misguidedly framed as evidence of evolutionary inferiority and moral degradation in non-white races.

Treatment

The only 'treatment' suggested by adherents to the ideas presented in "Types of Mankind" was the socio-political dominance and control of certain racial groups they considered inferior. This typology excused neglect and abuse based on unjustifiable scientific claims.

Social Determinants of Illness

The mischaracterization of racial feature differences as determinants of health exacerbated societal discrimination and overlooked genuine social determinants of health like poverty, access to care, and education. These factors, rather than race, are critical in understanding health disparities.
