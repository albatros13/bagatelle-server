Art and Illness: An Analysis of "Syphillis" and "Torment of St. Anthony"

a. Artist/ Group/Tribe

The first artwork, titled "Syphillis," was painted by the artist Cooper RT in 1912 and is currently housed in the Wellcome Library, London. The second, "Torment of St. Anthony," is a creation of Savoldo GG from 1515, residing in the Timken Museum of Art, San Diego.

b. Historical and Socio-Cultural Context

Cooper RT's artwork emerges during a time when syphilis had significant social and medical implications in early 20th-century Europe. Concurrently, Savoldo GG's painting belongs to the Renaissance era, reflecting religious and cultural transitions in Italy during the early 16th century.

c. Symbolism and Iconography

In "Syphillis," the spectral figure symbolizes the haunting nature of this disease, spreading fear and moral judgment. "Torment of St. Anthony" utilizes demonic figures and a rugged landscape as metaphors for spiritual and physical struggles against disease and temptation.

d. Stylistic Significance

"Syphillis" employs dark and moody tones with realistic detailing, invoking emotional tension. In contrast, Savoldo GG's use of light and shadow in "Torment of St. Anthony" creates a dramatic chiaroscuro, emphasizing the saint’s isolation and torment against vivid landscapes.

e. Social and Cultural Inequities

The narrative in "Syphillis" critically reflects societal stigma around sexually transmitted infections, marginalizing the afflicted. Conversely, in St. Anthony’s depiction, spiritual torment parallels societal challenges of faith and morality, exploring how these become sources of communal struggle.

f. Description of Disease & Etiology

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. If untreated, it progresses through various stages, causing severe systemic damage. The erysipelas depicted in "Torment of St. Anthony" is an acute bacterial infection, often related to the Streptococcus group, affecting the skin.

g. Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

In "Syphillis," the man's expression and posture denote the despair and social degradation associated with tertiary syphilis, often linked to severe neurological and dermatological symptoms. "Torment of St. Anthony" artistically illustrates the abnormal discoloration and swelling indicative of erysipelas.

h. Treatment

At the time of Cooper's painting, mercury and salvarsan were primary treatments for syphilis, often with dire side effects. In contrast, "Torment of St. Anthony" reflects medieval medical practices for erysipelas, including herbal remedies and spiritual healing, underscoring the era's reliance on traditional medicine.

i. Social Determinants of Illness

The spread of syphilis was exacerbated by social determinants like poverty, lack of education, and moral judgments, impeding public health measures in early 20th-century Europe. Similarly, St. Anthony’s torments metaphorically align with how socio-economic conditions and spirituality influenced health perceptions in Renaissance Italy.
