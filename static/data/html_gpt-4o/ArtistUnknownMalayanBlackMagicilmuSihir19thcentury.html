Malayan Black Magic Ilmu Sihir

[a] Artist/ Group/Tribe

The artwork, "Malayan Black Magic Ilmu Sihir," is attributed to an unknown artist from the Malay region. This piece is reflective of the deeply rooted customary practices related to folklore and mysticism prevalent in Southeast Asian cultures, particularly those involving spiritual healers and shamans in the 19th century.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-frame)

This manuscript dates back to the 19th century, a period marked by the flourishing of traditional practices intertwined with Islamic mysticism in the Malay Archipelago. During this time, the belief in black magic and spiritual healing was widespread, and artworks such as this reflect the socio-cultural context where diseases were often seen through spiritual lenses.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography

The iconography in "Ilmu Sihir" employs human-like figures surrounded by Arabic script, symbolizing the integration of Islamic motifs with local mystical practices. This combination served to bridge physical ailments with spiritual beliefs, portraying a worldview where spiritual and physical worlds were deeply connected.

[d] Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/ Principles of Design)

The artwork's stylistic elements include bold outlines and fluid lines that form human figures, reflecting the animistic and dynamic portrayal typical of mystical art. The script itself is elegantly woven into the figures, indicative of the harmony between word and image in conveying spiritual messages.

[e] Social / Cultural Inequities

Social and cultural inequities of the period are evident in this artwork, as only specific societal elites, such as shamans or spiritual leaders, possessed the knowledge and power of "Ilmu Sihir." The general populace depended on these figures for guidance, often sidelining conventional medical approaches.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The diseases depicted in this form of art were typically understood as physical manifestations of spiritual affliction or imbalance. The etiology often involved supernatural causes such as curses or the influence of malevolent spirits, as interpreted through the lens of black magic practices.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Pathological signs in this context are not depicted with biological precision but rather symbolized through distorted human figures and scriptural annotations. They serve as metaphors for the perceived spiritual imbalance or affliction that transcends the visible symptoms and delves into the mystical.

[h] Treatment

Treatment in the worldview presented by such artworks involved ritualistic practices that sought to appease spirits or remove curses. Such treatments were carried out by spiritual healers using chants, herbal concoctions, and invocationsâ€”methods steeped in tradition and symbolic power.

[i] Social Determinants of Illness

Social determinants of illness in this cultural framework included social status, access to spiritual healers, and adherence to cultural beliefs. Since traditional healing was often intertwined with religious and mystical practices, it was accessible mainly to those within certain religious or social circles.
