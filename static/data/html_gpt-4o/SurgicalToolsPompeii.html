Surgical Tools from Pompeii

[a] Artist/ Group/ Tribe: The surgical instruments depicted in the image belong to the ancient Romans, a civilization known for its advancement in medicine and surgical practices. These tools were likely used by Roman surgeons known as 'medici,' who were vital in both civilian and military contexts. The tools were uncovered in Pompeii, a city famously preserved under volcanic ash after the eruption of Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context: The Roman Empire, at its peak, was a society that valued knowledge and skill, particularly in medicine. Roman medical practices were influenced by earlier Greek medical theories. The tools from Pompeii date back to the first century AD, a time of significant urban growth and prosperity in the empire, before its eventual decline. This period was marked by both technological advancements and a rich cultural life, including public health initiatives such as aqueducts and public bathing facilities.

Hygiene was a significant concern, although germ theory was not yet understood.

The tools reflect an understanding of various surgical techniques such as cauterization, incision, and extraction. Roman medical practice emphasized both surgical and nonsurgical methods, supported by comprehensive medical texts from physicians like Galen.
