[a] Artist/ Group/ Tribe

The artist behind "Daltonartandmed74" remains unknown. This watercolor portrait is a quintessential representation of medical illustrators who were key contributors to medical education and documentation during the 19th century.

[b] Historical and Socio-Cultural Context (Time-Frame)

This artwork was likely created during the 1800s, a time when medical illustrations became crucial in understanding and disseminating information about diseases. The industrial revolution brought about urbanization, which led to increased spread of diseases due to overcrowded living conditions.

[c] Symbolism and/or Iconography

The depiction of the lesions visible on the figure's skin reflects the symbolic nature of scientific observation and medical understanding at that time. They symbolize the seriousness of untreated diseases and their physical manifestation, making it a valuable resource for educating both medical professionals and the public.

[d] Stylistic Significance (Elements of Art/Principles of Design)

The artwork employs a realistic style typical of medical illustrations, focusing on accuracy rather than artistic interpretation. The use of watercolor allows for nuanced gradations that effectively capture skin tone and texture, essential for the educational purpose of the illustration.

[e] Social/Cultural Inequities

The artwork highlights socio-cultural inequities of the time, as access to medical care and education was often limited to the wealthy. Illustrations like this one were used in medical texts that were inaccessible to the general population, underscoring disparities in healthcare and knowledge.

[f] Description of Disease & Etiology

The portrait illustrates a condition consistent with syphilis, a sexually transmitted infection caused by the bacterium Treponema pallidum. During the 19th century, syphilis was a widespread epidemic, often resulting in severe physical consequences if untreated.

[g] Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The red, blotchy lesions on the skin represent secondary syphilitic eruptions, indicative of the spread of the disease through the bloodstream. These physical manifestations are crucial for the diagnosis and understanding of the illness.
