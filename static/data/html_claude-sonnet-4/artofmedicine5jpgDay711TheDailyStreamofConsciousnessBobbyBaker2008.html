The Daily Stream of Consciousness

Bobby Baker, 2008

Artist

Bobby Baker is a British performance artist and visual artist known for her autobiographical works that explore mental health, domesticity, and personal transformation. Born in 1950, Baker has been creating art for over four decades, often drawing from her own experiences with mental illness, particularly her struggles with depression and psychosis. Her work spans performance, drawing, sculpture, and installation, frequently incorporating elements of her daily life and psychological states.

Historical and Socio-Cultural Context

Created in 2008, "The Daily Stream of Consciousness" emerges from a period of increased awareness and destigmatization of mental health issues in contemporary British society. This artwork was produced during Baker's "Daily Life" series, a project documenting her recovery from a mental breakdown through daily drawings. The early 21st century marked a significant shift in mental health discourse, moving away from institutionalization toward community-based care and patient advocacy. Baker's work coincides with growing recognition of art therapy and creative expression as legitimate forms of mental health treatment and self-advocacy.

Symbolism and Iconography

The central motif of an elongated head with water flowing from the cranium directly represents the stream of consciousness concept popularized by Virginia Woolf and other modernist writers. The circular crown or halo-like structure suggests both protection and containment of mental processes. Small boats navigating the waters within the head symbolize thoughts or memories traversing the mind's landscape. The green landscape at the base represents grounding and connection to nature, while tiny figures suggest the relationship between internal mental states and external social interactions. The upward-flowing blue stream emanating from the face represents the release or expression of internal psychological content.

Stylistic Significance

Baker employs a naive or outsider art aesthetic characterized by simplified forms, bold colors, and symbolic rather than naturalistic representation. The composition demonstrates strong vertical emphasis, drawing the eye upward through the flowing stream, suggesting psychological elevation or release. The use of primary colors—blue for water/consciousness, green for earth/stability, and yellow for the protective crown—creates visual harmony while maintaining symbolic clarity. The drawing technique is deliberately childlike, rejecting academic artistic conventions in favor of direct emotional expression, which aligns with art therapy practices and outsider art movements.

Social/Cultural Inequities

Baker's work addresses the historical marginalization of individuals with mental illness, particularly women whose psychological distress was often dismissed or pathologized. Her artistic practice challenges the medical model's dominance by asserting the patient's voice and creative agency. The work also highlights class-based inequities in mental health care access, as Baker's ability to create and exhibit art provided her with therapeutic resources not available to all patients. Gender inequities are implicit, as women's mental health has historically been subject to different diagnostic criteria and treatment approaches than men's.

Description of Disease & Etiology

The artwork reflects Baker's experience with depression and psychotic episodes, conditions that affect millions worldwide. Depression involves persistent low mood, anhedonia, and cognitive dysfunction, while psychosis can include delusions, hallucinations, and disorganized thinking. The etiology of these conditions is multifactorial, involving genetic predisposition, neurochemical imbalances (particularly involving serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine), environmental stressors, and psychosocial factors. Baker's mental health challenges appear to have been triggered by life stressors combined with possible biological vulnerability.

Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The artwork visually represents several aspects of mental illness experience. The overwhelming flow of water from the head suggests racing thoughts, rumination, or the intrusive nature of depressive or psychotic ideation. The contained yet turbulent waters within the cranial space reflect the internal chaos often experienced during mental health crises. The small boats suggest the difficulty of navigating overwhelming thoughts or memories. The eyes, while present, appear somewhat vacant or distant, potentially representing dissociation or the emotional numbness associated with depression.

Treatment

Baker's artistic practice itself functions as a form of treatment, specifically art therapy and expressive therapy approaches. Her daily drawing routine provided structure, self-monitoring, and emotional processing—key components of cognitive-behavioral interventions. The creation of this work represents bibliotherapy or narrative therapy principles, where storytelling and creative expression facilitate healing. Contemporary treatment for conditions like Baker's typically includes antidepressant medications, psychotherapy (particularly cognitive-behavioral therapy), and social support systems.

Social Determinants of Illness

Baker's experience reflects how social determinants influence mental health outcomes. As an artist, she faced economic instability and social marginalization—factors that can exacerbate mental health conditions. However, her cultural capital and access to artistic communities provided protective factors unavailable to many individuals with mental illness. The gendered expectations of domestic responsibility, evident in much of Baker's work, represent additional social stressors that disproportionately affect women's mental health. Access to healthcare, social support networks, and creative outlets all function as crucial social determinants in mental health recovery.
