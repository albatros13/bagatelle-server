Preta (1912) by Lucílio de Albuquerque

Artist

Lucílio de Albuquerque (1877-1939) was a prominent Brazilian painter associated with the academic tradition and later Impressionist movement in Brazil. Born in Barras, Piauí, Albuquerque studied at the National School of Fine Arts in Rio de Janeiro and later in Paris at the École des Beaux-Arts. He became known for his portraits, genre scenes, and historical paintings that often depicted Brazilian social realities with technical precision and emotional depth.

Historical and Socio-Cultural Context

Created in 1912, "Preta" emerges from the early Republican period in Brazil, just 24 years after the abolition of slavery in 1888. This historical moment was characterized by significant social upheaval as former enslaved people and their descendants struggled to integrate into Brazilian society while facing systematic exclusion and marginalization. The painting reflects the precarious living conditions and limited access to healthcare that characterized the experience of Afro-Brazilian communities during this transitional period. The work was created during a time when Brazilian artists were increasingly turning their attention to national themes and social realities, moving away from purely European academic subjects.

Symbolism and Iconography

The intimate domestic scene carries profound symbolic weight, representing both maternal devotion and social vulnerability. The architectural setting—simple walls and basic furnishings—symbolizes the modest circumstances of the post-abolition Afro-Brazilian experience. The positioning of the mother cradling her child evokes universal themes of maternal protection while simultaneously highlighting the specific challenges faced by Black mothers in early 20th-century Brazil. The warm, earthy tones suggest both the dignity of the subjects and their connection to the Brazilian landscape, while the sparse interior speaks to economic hardship and social marginalization.

Stylistic Significance

Albuquerque employs a realistic style influenced by academic training, utilizing warm ochre and amber tones that create an intimate atmosphere. The composition follows classical principles with the diagonal line of the reclining figure creating visual movement, while the vertical elements of the architectural space provide structural stability. The artist's use of chiaroscuro—dramatic contrasts between light and shadow—draws attention to the central figures while creating emotional depth. The brushwork demonstrates technical proficiency typical of academic training, with careful attention to anatomical accuracy and spatial relationships. The color palette, dominated by earth tones, reinforces the connection between the subjects and their humble environment.

Social and Cultural Inequities

The painting powerfully illustrates the intersection of racial and economic inequalities in early Republican Brazil. The modest living conditions depicted reflect the systematic exclusion of Afro-Brazilians from economic opportunities and social advancement following abolition. The apparent illness of the child highlights disparities in healthcare access, as Black families often lacked resources for medical care and were excluded from formal healthcare institutions. These inequities were perpetuated by discriminatory practices in employment, education, and housing that confined many Afro-Brazilian families to poverty and precarious living conditions.

Description of Disease & Etiology

While the specific illness cannot be definitively diagnosed from the visual evidence, the child's apparent weakness and pallor suggest possible malnutrition, anemia, or infectious disease—conditions common among impoverished populations in early 20th-century Brazil. The etiology would likely relate to environmental factors including poor sanitation, inadequate nutrition, overcrowded living conditions, and limited access to clean water. Infectious diseases such as tuberculosis, pneumonia, or gastrointestinal infections were prevalent in urban poor communities during this period.

Pathological Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The visual indicators of illness in the painting include the child's apparent listlessness and pale complexion, contrasting with the mother's concerned expression and protective posture. The positioning suggests weakness or inability to maintain normal posture, while the mother's attentive care indicates recognition of the child's compromised health status. The sparse bedding and simple clothing may also suggest the family's limited resources for maintaining adequate warmth and comfort during illness.

Treatment

Given the historical context and socioeconomic circumstances, treatment options would have been extremely limited. The mother's care—providing warmth, comfort, and whatever nutritional support was available—represents the primary therapeutic intervention accessible to the family. Folk remedies and traditional healing practices within Afro-Brazilian communities often served as the main medical resources, as formal medical care was largely inaccessible due to cost and discrimination. The intimate caregiving depicted in the painting reflects the crucial role of family support systems in addressing health crises within marginalized communities.

Social Determinants of Illness

The painting illustrates multiple social determinants contributing to health disparities: poverty limiting access to adequate nutrition and healthcare, racial discrimination restricting economic opportunities, substandard housing conditions promoting disease transmission, and social exclusion preventing integration into formal support systems. The post-abolition period in Brazil created a particularly vulnerable population lacking the social safety nets necessary for health maintenance and disease prevention. Educational barriers and employment discrimination further compounded these health risks by perpetuating cycles of poverty and marginalization.
