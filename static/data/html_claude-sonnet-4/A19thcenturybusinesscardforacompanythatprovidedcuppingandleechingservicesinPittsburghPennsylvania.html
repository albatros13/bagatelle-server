Mr. & Mrs. Bese: Cuppers & Leechers Business Card, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania

Artist/Group/Tribe

This business card represents the work of an anonymous commercial printer or lithographer operating in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania during the 19th century. The card advertises the services of Mr. and Mrs. Bese, professional medical practitioners who specialized in cupping and leeching treatments. Such cards were typically produced by local printing establishments that served the growing commercial needs of America's expanding urban centers.

Historical and Socio-Cultural Context

This artifact dates to the mid-19th century, during the height of heroic medicine in America (approximately 1800-1860). Pittsburgh was experiencing rapid industrialization and population growth, creating demand for accessible medical services. Cupping and leeching were mainstream medical treatments during this period, practiced alongside other forms of bloodletting. The formal business card format reflects the professionalization of medical services and the emergence of a commercial healthcare economy. The inclusion of both husband and wife suggests family-based medical practices were common, with women often serving as medical practitioners despite limited formal training opportunities.

Symbolism and Iconography

The decorative border features natural motifs including plants and what appears to be a leech in its natural habitat, symbolically connecting the medical practice to nature-based healing. The pastoral scene reinforces the period's belief in the therapeutic power of natural remedies. The formal typography and ornate design elements convey professionalism and legitimacy, important considerations when medical licensing was inconsistent. The prominent display of the street address (No 177 Grand St., Cor. Strawberry Alley) emphasizes accessibility and permanent establishment in the community.

Stylistic Significance

The card demonstrates typical Victorian-era commercial design principles, employing bold typography hierarchy with the practitioners' names and services prominently displayed. The decorative elements show careful attention to balance and symmetry, with ornamental borders framing the central text. The engraving technique produces fine line work characteristic of 19th-century commercial printing. The composition uses contrast effectively, with heavy black text against white space, ensuring readability while maintaining aesthetic appeal. The integration of text and imagery reflects the period's approach to commercial advertising design.

Social/Cultural Inequities

This business card reflects significant healthcare inequities of the 19th century. Professional medical services were primarily accessible to those with financial means, while working-class populations often relied on folk remedies or charity care. The formal business structure suggests the Beses served middle-class clientele who could afford specialized treatments. Gender inequities are evident in Mrs. Bese's secondary billing despite likely equal or greater practical experience. Racial minorities and immigrant populations faced additional barriers to accessing such professional medical services, often excluded from mainstream healthcare systems.

Description of Disease & Etiology

Cupping and leeching were employed to treat numerous conditions based on humoral theory, which attributed illness to imbalances in bodily fluids (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile). Practitioners believed excess blood caused fever, inflammation, headaches, and various internal disorders. Common treated conditions included pneumonia, pleurisy, rheumatism, and mental disturbances. The underlying etiology was fundamentally flawed, as these treatments were based on ancient Greek medical theories rather than empirical understanding of disease mechanisms.

Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Practitioners identified candidates for bloodletting through observable signs including flushed complexion, rapid pulse, elevated body temperature, and patient complaints of fullness or pressure. Inflammation, swelling, and pain were considered indicators of excess blood requiring removal. The amount of blood extracted was often determined by patient response, with some practitioners removing substantial quantities. Modern understanding reveals these signs often indicated the body's natural immune responses or symptoms of underlying infections that required supportive rather than depleting treatments.

Treatment

Cupping involved creating suction using heated glass cups placed on the skin, either dry cupping or wet cupping (combined with small incisions). Leeching employed medicinal leeches (Hirudo medicinalis) applied to specific body areas to extract blood. Treatments were repeated based on practitioner assessment and patient condition. While these methods could provide temporary symptomatic relief through counter-irritation effects, they often weakened patients and delayed recovery. Modern medicine recognizes limited therapeutic applications for medical leeching in specific surgical contexts, but general bloodletting is contraindicated.

Social Determinants of Illness

Pittsburgh's industrial environment contributed significantly to health problems that drove demand for medical services. Poor sanitation, crowded housing, industrial pollution, and occupational hazards created conditions promoting respiratory diseases, infections, and injuries. Limited understanding of disease transmission meant environmental factors were poorly addressed. Economic factors determined access to professional medical care versus folk remedies. Social class influenced treatment quality and practitioner selection. The availability of specialized practitioners like the Beses reflected urban concentrations of wealth and commerce that supported professional medical services.
