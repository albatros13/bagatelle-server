Care of the Skin in the Pellegrinaio

Artist/ Group/Tribe

Domenico di Bartolo (c. 1400-1447) was an Italian Renaissance painter from Siena, considered one of the most important artists working in the Sienese tradition during the early 15th century. A student of Taddeo di Bartolo, Domenico di Bartolo represented the bridge between Gothic and Renaissance styles in Sienese painting. He was particularly renowned for his narrative abilities and his capacity to depict complex hospital scenes with both artistic sophistication and documentary precision.

Historical and Socio-Cultural Context

Created around 1440, this fresco was part of a cycle decorating the Pellegrinaio (Pilgrims' Hall) of the Hospital of Santa Maria della Scala in Siena, one of Europe's oldest and most advanced hospitals. During the 15th century, Siena was a major pilgrimage stop on the Via Francigena route to Rome. The hospital served not only local residents but also countless pilgrims, travelers, and the destitute. This period marked a significant evolution in medical care, transitioning from purely religious healing practices to more systematic approaches that combined spiritual and physical treatment. The frescoes served both decorative and promotional purposes, demonstrating the hospital's charitable work to potential benefactors and the public.

Symbolism and Iconography

The composition is rich with symbolic elements representing Christian charity and medical care. The architectural setting emphasizes the institutional nature of healthcare, while the careful attention to individual patients reflects the Renaissance humanistic approach to medicine. The presence of both clergy and lay caregivers symbolizes the integration of spiritual and physical healing. The washing and treatment of skin conditions represents purification on both literal and metaphorical levels, connecting bodily cleansing with spiritual redemption. The orderly arrangement of beds and the systematic care being provided symbolize the hospital's reputation for organized, compassionate treatment.

Stylistic Significance

Di Bartolo employs a sophisticated use of linear perspective to create depth within the hospital ward, demonstrating Renaissance artistic innovations. The composition uses rhythmic repetition of beds and figures to create visual harmony while maintaining individual character in each patient and caregiver. The artist's use of warm earth tones creates a sense of comfort and healing, while the careful attention to architectural details reflects the growing interest in realistic representation. The balanced composition, with its careful distribution of figures and architectural elements, exemplifies the Renaissance principles of harmony and proportion while serving the narrative function of documenting hospital practices.

Social/Cultural Inequities

The fresco reveals significant social stratification in medical care during the 15th century. While the Hospital of Santa Maria della Scala was progressive in treating the poor, access to quality medical care remained largely dependent on social status and geographic location. The depicted scene shows primarily male patients, reflecting gender inequities in hospital admission and treatment. Rural populations often lacked access to such sophisticated medical facilities. The presence of both religious and secular caregivers highlights the transition period where medical knowledge was slowly shifting from monastic control to secular practitioners, yet educational opportunities remained limited to the privileged classes.

Description of Disease & Etiology

The fresco depicts various skin conditions commonly encountered in medieval and Renaissance Europe. These likely included scabies, caused by the mite Sarcoptes scabiei, which thrived in overcrowded, unsanitary conditions typical of medieval urban environments. Fungal infections such as ringworm were prevalent due to poor hygiene and shared living spaces. Bacterial skin infections, including impetigo and cellulitis, were common consequences of minor wounds in an era before antiseptic practices. Pilgrims and travelers were particularly susceptible to these conditions due to prolonged journeys in poor sanitary conditions, shared accommodations, and weakened immune systems from malnutrition and exhaustion.

Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The artwork shows patients with visible skin lesions, rashes, and what appear to be ulcerative conditions. The positioning of patients suggests various stages of skin disease treatment, from acute presentations requiring bed rest to convalescent phases. The careful attention paid to washing and cleansing procedures indicates recognition of the importance of hygiene in treating skin conditions. Some figures display the characteristic postures of patients experiencing discomfort from pruritic (itchy) conditions, while others show signs of systemic illness that often accompanied severe skin infections in the pre-antibiotic era.

Treatment

Treatment methods depicted include systematic washing with water and soap, topical applications of herbal preparations, and careful wound dressing. The hospital likely employed a combination of Galenic medicine, folk remedies, and emerging empirical observations. Common treatments would have included applications of honey for its antimicrobial properties, various plant-based salves containing ingredients like chamomile and lavender for anti-inflammatory effects, and careful attention to nutrition and rest to support healing. The organized nature of care shown suggests the hospital had developed systematic protocols for treating skin conditions, representing advanced medical practice for its time.

Social Determinants of Illness

The prevalence of skin conditions reflected broader social determinants including overcrowded urban living conditions, poor sanitation infrastructure, limited access to clean water and soap, malnutrition that compromised immune function, and occupational exposures that damaged skin integrity. Pilgrims were particularly vulnerable due to their transient status, exposure to diverse pathogens during travel, and often limited resources for maintaining personal hygiene. The hospital's role in treating these conditions highlights how institutional responses to disease were necessary to address problems rooted in social and economic inequalities of medieval society.
