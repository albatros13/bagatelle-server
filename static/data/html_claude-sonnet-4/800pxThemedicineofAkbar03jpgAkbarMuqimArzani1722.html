The Medicine of Akbar: A Mughal Anatomical Treatise

Artist/Group/Tribe

This remarkable anatomical illustration was created by Akbar Muqim Arzani in 1722. Arzani was a distinguished physician and scholar working within the Mughal court tradition, representing the synthesis of Islamic, Persian, and Indian medical knowledge that characterized the late Mughal period. As court physicians, such artists-scholars held prestigious positions and were responsible for both medical practice and the creation of educational manuscripts that preserved and transmitted medical knowledge.

Historical and Socio-Cultural Context

Created during the decline of the Mughal Empire under Emperor Muhammad Shah (1719-1748), this work reflects the continued intellectual vitality of Mughal medical scholarship despite political instability. The early 18th century witnessed the integration of traditional Unani medicine (Greek-Arabic medical system) with Ayurvedic practices and emerging anatomical knowledge. This period marked a critical juncture when Islamic medical texts were being illustrated with increasingly detailed anatomical observations, influenced by both Persian medical traditions and local Indian knowledge systems.

Symbolism and/or Iconography

The illustration employs traditional Islamic medical iconography, presenting the human body as a microcosm reflecting divine order. The central figure displays internal organs in a systematic arrangement, with the heart positioned prominently as the seat of both physical and spiritual life. The surrounding individual organ illustrations with Arabic/Persian annotations represent the holistic approach of Unani medicine, where each organ is understood in relation to the body's overall humoral balance. The standing pose and frontal presentation follow classical anatomical illustration conventions established in medieval Islamic medical texts.

Stylistic Significance

The artwork demonstrates characteristic Mughal miniature painting techniques, employing flat perspective, bold color blocks, and precise linework. The artist uses a limited but symbolic color palette: reds and purples for internal organs, natural flesh tones for the body, and muted backgrounds typical of medical manuscripts. The composition follows principles of hierarchical scale and symmetrical balance, with the central figure dominating the space while peripheral organ studies provide detailed supplementary information. The style bridges scientific illustration and artistic tradition, maintaining aesthetic appeal while serving educational purposes.

Social/Cultural Inequities

This medical knowledge was primarily accessible to elite court physicians and wealthy patrons who could afford such illustrated manuscripts. The tradition of medical illustration remained largely within male-dominated scholarly circles, with women's medical knowledge often relegated to oral traditions despite their significant roles as healers and midwives. The court-based medical system served primarily the upper classes, while common people relied on traditional healers and folk medicine practices that were often dismissed or marginalized by formal medical establishments.

Description of Disease & Etiology

While the illustration presents normal anatomy rather than specific pathology, it reflects the Unani medical understanding of disease as resulting from imbalances in the four humors (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile). The detailed organ representations suggest focus on digestive and circulatory systems, which were central to humoral theory. The heart, liver, and stomach receive particular attention, reflecting their importance in maintaining humoral equilibrium and overall health according to Islamic medical doctrine.

Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

The anatomical accuracy of organ placement and proportion serves as a baseline for identifying pathological variations. In Unani tradition, practitioners would assess organ function through pulse diagnosis, urine examination, and observation of physical signs. The illustration's emphasis on organ color and form would have helped physicians recognize abnormalities such as enlarged organs, color changes, or structural irregularities that indicated humoral imbalances or specific disease states.

Treatment

Treatment approaches in this medical tradition emphasized restoring humoral balance through dietary modifications, herbal medicines, bloodletting, cupping, and lifestyle adjustments. The anatomical knowledge represented here would guide therapeutic interventions, with treatments targeted to specific organs and their associated humors. Compound herbal formulations, often including ingredients from diverse geographical regions, were prescribed based on detailed understanding of organ function and inter-relationships depicted in such illustrations.

Social Determinants of Illness

The Mughal medical system recognized that health was influenced by environmental factors, social status, occupation, and access to quality food and clean living conditions. Court physicians understood that diseases affected different social classes differently, with the wealthy having access to elaborate treatments while common people faced higher mortality rates due to poor sanitation, malnutrition, and limited medical care. Climate, seasonal changes, and geographical location were also considered crucial factors in disease causation and treatment planning.
