Steven Dohanos - Separation Anxiety (1955)

Artist

Steven Dohanos (1907-1994) was an American illustrator renowned for his contributions to American visual culture during the mid-20th century. Born in Lorain, Ohio, Dohanos became one of the most celebrated magazine illustrators of his era, creating over 125 covers for The Saturday Evening Post between 1942 and 1962. His work epitomized the golden age of American illustration, capturing the essence of post-war American life with remarkable detail and emotional resonance.

Historical and Socio-Cultural Context

Created in 1955, this Saturday Evening Post cover emerged during a pivotal period in American healthcare and social development. The post-World War II era marked significant advances in pediatric medicine and psychology, with increased attention to children's emotional wellbeing. The 1950s witnessed the establishment of modern pediatric practices, widespread vaccination programs, and growing awareness of childhood psychological needs. This period also saw the rise of suburban family life, where mothers typically managed household and childcare responsibilities while fathers worked outside the home. The waiting room scene reflects the evolving American healthcare system, which was becoming more accessible to middle-class families through employer-provided insurance plans.

Symbolism and Iconography

The composition masterfully employs symbolic elements to convey the universal experience of medical anxiety. The central mother-child dyad represents the protective bond essential for childhood security. Toys scattered throughout the waiting room serve as transitional objects, providing comfort in an unfamiliar environment. The clinical setting, marked by sterile walls and medical certificates, symbolizes institutional authority and the medicalization of childhood experiences. The juxtaposition of domestic comfort items against clinical efficiency highlights the tension between home security and medical necessity. The varied responses of children and parents create a narrative spectrum of coping mechanisms, from withdrawal to acceptance.

Stylistic Significance

Dohanos employs realistic illustration techniques characteristic of American regionalism and social realism. His composition utilizes strong diagonal lines created by the architectural elements and figure placement, leading the viewer's eye through the narrative. The color palette balances warm domestic tones with cooler clinical greens and whites, reinforcing the emotional dichotomy. The artist demonstrates mastery of proportion and perspective, creating depth through overlapping figures and diminishing scale. Light sources appear natural and diffused, avoiding dramatic shadows that might heighten anxiety. The detailed rendering of textures—from clothing fabrics to toy surfaces—demonstrates the illustrative precision valued in commercial art of this period.

Social/Cultural Inequities

The illustration reflects the socioeconomic limitations of 1950s healthcare access, depicting a predominantly white, middle-class patient population. The well-dressed families suggest economic stability necessary for routine medical care, highlighting healthcare disparities that excluded many minority and lower-income families. The presence of stay-at-home mothers reflects gender role expectations that assumed women's primary responsibility for childcare, potentially limiting their economic independence. The suburban clinic setting indicates geographic healthcare inequities, as rural and urban underserved communities often lacked similar facilities. These visual choices, while reflective of Dohanos's target audience, inadvertently document the exclusionary nature of mid-century American healthcare systems.

Description of Disease & Etiology

Separation anxiety disorder represents a developmental condition characterized by excessive distress when separated from attachment figures, typically parents or primary caregivers. The etiology involves complex interactions between temperamental predisposition, environmental factors, and developmental stages. Children naturally experience separation anxiety as part of normal development, typically emerging around 8-10 months and often resurfacing during transitions to new environments like medical settings. Genetic factors contribute to anxiety sensitivity, while environmental stressors, previous traumatic separations, or overprotective parenting styles can exacerbate symptoms. The medical setting itself becomes a precipitating factor, combining unfamiliarity, potential discomfort, and separation from security objects.

Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Dohanos captures multiple manifestations of childhood anxiety and distress within the clinical setting. Observable signs include physical proximity-seeking behaviors, as children cling to parents or position themselves close to familiar figures. Facial expressions reveal apprehension, with some children displaying wide-eyed vigilance while others show withdrawn or sad expressions. Body language indicators include defensive postures, such as children turning away from the medical office door or hiding behind parental figures. Some children engage in self-soothing behaviors through interaction with comfort objects like toys or books. The varying responses illustrated—from overt distress to apparent resignation—demonstrate the spectrum of anxiety presentations in pediatric populations.

Treatment

Treatment approaches for separation anxiety in medical settings during the 1950s were limited compared to contemporary standards. Primary interventions focused on parental presence and comfort measures, as depicted in Dohanos's illustration. Healthcare providers typically relied on distraction techniques, allowing children to bring comfort objects, and maintaining calm, reassuring demeanors. Modern treatment encompasses behavioral interventions including gradual exposure therapy, cognitive-behavioral techniques adapted for children, and family-based interventions. Contemporary pediatric practice emphasizes child life specialists, medical play therapy, and environmental modifications to reduce anxiety-provoking stimuli. Pharmacological interventions are reserved for severe cases, with preference for behavioral and environmental modifications as first-line treatments.

Social Determinants of Illness

The social determinants influencing childhood separation anxiety extend beyond individual psychology to encompass family structure, socioeconomic status, and healthcare accessibility. The nuclear family structure depicted reflects 1950s social norms that could either provide stability or increase pressure on single parental relationships. Economic stability enables regular healthcare access, early intervention, and reduced family stress that might exacerbate childhood anxiety. Educational levels influence parental understanding of child development and appropriate responses to anxiety. Geographic location affects access to specialized pediatric care and mental health services. Cultural attitudes toward mental health and help-seeking behaviors significantly impact recognition and treatment of childhood anxiety disorders, with stigma potentially delaying appropriate intervention.
