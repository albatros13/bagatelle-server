Roman Birthing Plaque from Ostia

Artist/Group/Tribe

This marble relief was created by anonymous Roman artisans working in Ostia, the ancient port city of Rome. During the late Republic and early Imperial periods, skilled craftsmen produced funerary and votive reliefs for various social classes, including freedmen, merchants, and citizens who could afford marble commemorative works. The artistic tradition draws from Hellenistic Greek influences while incorporating distinctly Roman narrative elements and practical concerns.

Historical and Socio-Cultural Context

Created between 400-300 BCE, this plaque originates from a period of Roman expansion and cultural development. Ostia, as Rome's primary port, was a cosmopolitan center where diverse populations intermingled, creating a unique cultural environment. During this era, Roman society was increasingly concerned with family lineage, succession, and the continuation of bloodlines for both political and economic reasons. Childbirth held immense significance as it determined inheritance, family honor, and social status. The creation of such reliefs reflects the growing prosperity of the merchant class and their ability to commission artistic works that addressed their most pressing concerns.

Symbolism and Iconography

The relief depicts a successful birthing scene with multiple attendant figures surrounding the laboring woman. The central figure lies in a reclined position, supported by what appears to be a birthing chair or bed, while three standing figures assist in the delivery process. The composition symbolizes community support, divine protection, and successful outcome. The presence of multiple attendants suggests both the importance of the event and the availability of skilled assistance. The successful completion of birth represented triumph over death, continuation of family lines, and divine favor, making these scenes powerful talismanic images for expectant mothers.

Stylistic Significance

The relief demonstrates classical Roman sculptural techniques with clear narrative organization and hierarchical composition. The figures are arranged in a frieze-like manner within a rectangular frame, creating visual stability and order. The artist employs shallow relief carving typical of Roman funerary art, with careful attention to drapery and anatomical detail. The composition uses principles of balance and rhythm, with the reclining central figure anchoring the scene while the vertical standing figures create dynamic movement. The modeling shows Hellenistic influence in the naturalistic treatment of human forms, while the narrative clarity reflects Roman artistic priorities.

Social/Cultural Inequities

This artwork reveals significant social stratification in Roman society regarding healthcare access. The depicted scene shows multiple skilled attendants, suggesting this represents an upper-class birth with access to professional midwives and medical care. Lower-class women, slaves, and rural populations would not have had access to such elaborate birthing assistance. The marble medium itself indicates wealth, as poorer families could not afford such commemorative works. Gender inequality is also evident, as women's primary social value was often tied to their reproductive capacity and ability to produce heirs, particularly male children who could inherit property and continue family names.

Description of Disease & Etiology

While the plaque depicts successful childbirth, it implicitly addresses the numerous complications that could arise during parturition in antiquity. Common obstetric complications included prolonged labor, breech presentations, placental complications, and puerperal sepsis. Maternal mortality rates were extremely high, with estimates suggesting 1-3% of births resulted in maternal death. Infant mortality was even higher, with approximately 25-30% of children dying before age five. Malnutrition, infectious diseases, and lack of sterile conditions contributed to poor outcomes for both mothers and infants.

Pathology Signs/Signifiers of Illness

Roman medical texts describe various signs of birthing complications that midwives and physicians monitored. These included prolonged labor exceeding normal timeframes, unusual bleeding, fever, convulsions, and abnormal fetal positioning. The relief's emphasis on multiple attendants suggests awareness of the need for skilled observation and intervention. Signs of fetal distress, maternal exhaustion, and hemorrhage were recognized but poorly understood in terms of underlying pathophysiology. The successful scene depicted here would have served as a positive visualization against these feared complications.

Treatment

Roman obstetric care relied heavily on experienced midwives who employed various techniques including massage, positioning changes, herbal remedies, and manual manipulation. Treatments included fumigation with aromatic substances, administration of wine or opium for pain relief, and use of birthing chairs to facilitate delivery. Surgical intervention was extremely rare and usually fatal. Midwives used tools such as obstetric hooks and forceps in desperate situations. Postpartum care involved dietary restrictions, herbal treatments, and gradual resumption of activities. Religious rituals and amulets were commonly employed alongside medical treatments.

Social Determinants of Illness

Multiple social factors influenced birthing outcomes in Roman society. Socioeconomic status determined access to skilled midwives, nutritious food, and clean living conditions. Urban versus rural location affected availability of medical assistance. Slave women faced particular vulnerability, as they had no control over their reproductive choices and often worked until delivery. Cultural factors including early marriage age, frequent pregnancies, and limited understanding of prenatal care contributed to poor outcomes. Geographic location within the empire affected access to medical knowledge, with frontier areas having fewer trained practitioners than established cities like Rome and Ostia.
